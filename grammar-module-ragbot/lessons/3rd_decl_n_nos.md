# Lesson: 3rd‑Declension Neuter Nouns (n_nos)

---

## 1. What Are 3rd‑Declension Neuter Nouns?

In Ancient Greek, nouns are grouped into **declensions** that determine how their endings change with case, number, and gender.  
The **3rd declension** is the most varied and includes many common nouns.  
When a noun is **neuter** (gender = neuter) and belongs to the 3rd declension, it is called a **3rd‑declension neuter noun** (often abbreviated *n_nos*).

Neuter nouns are used for objects, abstract ideas, and many collective terms.  
They behave like masculine nouns in the nominative‑accusative singular (both are identical), but they have distinct endings in other cases.

---

## 2. Declension Pattern

Below is the full paradigm for a typical 3rd‑declension neuter noun.  
The endings are the same for all 3rd‑declension neuter nouns; only the stem changes.

| Case | Singular | Dual | Plural |
|------|----------|------|--------|
| **Nominative** | –α | –α | –α |
| **Genitive** | –ας | –α | –ων |
| **Dative** | –ῃ | –α | –οις |
| **Accusative** | –α | –α | –α |
| **Vocative** | –α | –α | –α |

### Key Points

| Feature | Explanation |
|---------|-------------|
| **Nominative = Accusative** in singular and plural | The form is the same in both cases. |
| **Dual** | Rare in everyday speech; appears mainly in poetry and formal texts. |
| **Genitive plural** | Ends in **‑ων** – a distinctive marker of the 3rd declension. |
| **Dative plural** | Ends in **‑οις**. |

---

## 3. Key Rules & Exceptions

| Rule | How It Works | Example |
|------|--------------|---------|
| **Stem + α** | Most neuter nouns end in **‑α** in the nominative singular. | *ἔργον* “work” (stem *ἔργ‑*) → *ἔργον* |
| **Genitive singular** | Add **‑ας** to the stem. | *ἔργον* → *ἔργου* |
| **Dative singular** | Add **‑ῃ** to the stem. | *ἔργον* → *ἔργῃ* |
| **Plural genitive** | Add **‑ων** to the stem. | *ἔργον* → *ἔργων* |
| **Plural dative** | Add **‑οις** to the stem. | *ἔργον* → *ἔργοις* |
| **Dual forms** | Add **‑α** to the stem for nominative/accusative and **‑α** for genitive/dative. | *ἔργον* → *ἔργα* (dual nominative/accusative) |
| **Irregular stems** | Some nouns have irregular stems (e.g., *ὕδωρ* “water”). | *ὕδωρ* → *ὕδωρ* (all cases), *ὕδατος* (genitive) |

**Tip:** When a neuter noun ends in **‑α** in the nominative singular, it almost certainly belongs to the 3rd declension.

---

## 4. Example Sentences

| Greek | English |
|-------|---------|
| ἔργον ἐστὶν ἡ ἀρετὴ. | Work is virtue. |
| ὕδωρ ἐν τῷ ἀνθρώπῳ ἐστίν. | Water is in the man. |
| ἔργον ἐν ὕδωρ ἐστίν. | Work is in water. (plural) |
| ἔργον ἐν ὕδωρ ἐστίν. | Work is in water. (dual) |

*Note:* The verb *ἐστίν* (is) agrees with the neuter subject in number.

---

## 5. Usage & Context

- **Abstract nouns**: *ἔργον* “work”, *ἔργον* “action”.  
- **Collective nouns**: *ἔργον* “the whole of a thing”.  
- **Objects**: *ὕδωρ* “water”, *ἔργον* “work”.  
- **In poetry**: Dual forms appear to emphasize pairs or pairs of actions.

Neuter nouns are often used with **indefinite articles** (none in Greek) and are the default gender for objects that are not personified.

---

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology

- The 3rd declension originates from Proto‑Greek, where many nouns had stems ending in a consonant or a vowel other than **‑α**.  
- Neuter nouns in the 3rd declension often derive from abstract concepts or collective terms.  
- The endings **‑ων** (genitive plural) and **‑οις** (dative plural) are remnants of older Proto‑Greek case endings that survived into Classical Greek.  
- Over time, the dual form fell out of common use, leaving the singular and plural as the primary forms.

### Summary

- 3rd‑declension neuter nouns share a consistent set of endings.  
- They are identified by the **‑α** nominative singular.  
- The genitive plural **‑ων** and dative plural **‑οις** are key markers.  
- Use the paradigm table to decline any noun in this group.  

With this foundation, you can confidently read, translate, and construct sentences involving 3rd‑declension neuter nouns. Happy studying!
