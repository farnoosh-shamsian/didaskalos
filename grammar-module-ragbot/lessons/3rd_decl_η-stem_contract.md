# 3rd‑Declension η‑Stem Contract Nouns  

*Target level: beginner‑intermediate*  

---  

## 1. What the Concept Is  

In Ancient Greek the **third declension** contains nouns whose stems end in a vowel.  
When the stem ends in the long vowel **η** (eta) and a case ending that also begins with a vowel is attached, the two vowels **contract** (merge) into a single, longer vowel.  

Because the contraction hides the underlying η‑stem, these nouns **look** like first‑declension nouns (‑η, ‑ης, ‑ῃ, ‑ην …) but they belong to the third declension because the stem itself ends in a vowel, not a consonant.  

*Typical features*  

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Stem** | Ends in **‑η** (e.g., *γῆ‑* “earth”). |
| **Gender** | Almost always **feminine**; a few masculine nouns exist. |
| **Contracting endings** | Endings that start with **‑ο** or **‑ε** (genitive ‑ος, dative ‑ῳ, accusative ‑ον, etc.). |
| **Resulting forms** | Appear with the familiar third‑declension endings ‑ης, ‑ῃ, ‑ην, ‑ῶν, etc. |

---  

## 2. Full Paradigm  

Below is the complete declension for a regular η‑stem contract noun. The paradigm is shown for the **feminine** gender; masculine nouns follow the same pattern with the appropriate article.

| Case | Singular | Plural |
|------|----------|--------|
| **Nominative** | ‑η (γῆ) | ‑αι (γῆαι) |
| **Genitive** | ‑ης (γῆς) | ‑ῶν (γῆῶν) |
| **Dative** | ‑ῃ (γῇ) | ‑αις (γῇς) |
| **Accusative** | ‑ην (γῆν) | ‑ᾱς (γῆς) |
| **Vocative** | ‑η (γῆ) | ‑αι (γῆαι) |

### How the Forms Are Obtained  

| Step | Stem + Ending | Before contraction | After contraction |
|------|---------------|--------------------|-------------------|
| **Gen. sg.** | γῆ + ‑ος | γῆος | **γῆς** (η + ο → ῆ → ‑ς) |
| **Dat. sg.** | γῆ + ‑ῳ | γῆῳ | **γῇ** (η + ω → ῆ → ‑ι) |
| **Acc. sg.** | γῆ + ‑ον | γῆον | **γῆν** (η + ο → ῆ → ‑ν) |
| **Gen. pl.** | γῆ + ‑ων | γῆων | **γῆῶν** (η + ο → ῶ) |
| **Dat. pl.** | γῆ + ‑αις | γῆαις | **γῇς** (η + α → αι, no vowel change) |
| **Nom./Voc. pl.** | γῆ + ‑αι | γῆαι | **γῆαι** (no contraction; the ending begins with a consonant‑like *i*). |

> **Tip:** The contraction rule is the same as for verbs and adjectives that end in a vowel: a long η + a short vowel (ο, ε, ω, α) yields a long vowel (ῆ, ῶ, etc.) with the appropriate accent.

---  

## 3. Key Rules & Common Exceptions  

| Rule | Explanation | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **1. η + ο / η + ε contracts** | η + ο → **ῆ** (or η with acute); η + ε → **ῆ**. The resulting vowel carries the accent of the ending. | γῆ + ‑ος → **γῆς** |
| **2. No contraction with consonantal endings** | When the case ending begins with a consonant (‑ς, ‑ν, ‑τ, ‑ι, etc.), the stem stays unchanged. | Nominative sg.: γῆ (no ending) |
| **3. Plural nominative & vocative** | The plural nominative/vocative uses ‑αι, which begins with a consonant‑like *i*; therefore no contraction occurs. | γῆαι |
| **4. Genitive plural ‑ῶν** | The ending ‑ων begins with a vowel, so η + ο contracts to **ῶ**. | γῆ + ‑ων → **γῆῶν** |
| **5. Dative plural ‑αις** | The ending begins with a vowel, but the contraction yields **‑αις** (the vowel quality does not change). | γῆ + ‑αις → **γῇς** |
| **Exception – Proper names** | Some proper names ending in ‑η are treated as regular first‑declension nouns, not as η‑stem contract nouns. | *Ἀθηνᾶ* (Athena) |
| **Exception – Masculine η‑stem nouns** | Very few masculine nouns belong to this class; they follow the same pattern but take masculine articles. | — |

## 5. Notes on Usage and Context  

* **Semantic field** – Most η‑stem contract nouns denote natural or abstract entities (γῆ “earth”, θάλασσα is first‑declension, but *πνοή* “breath” is a first‑declension noun; true η‑stem examples are fewer).  
* **Article agreement** – Because the nouns are third‑declension, the article follows third‑declension case forms (ὁ/ἡ/τό).  
* **Literary style** – Poets often exploit the contraction to achieve metrical regularity; the contracted forms fit the dactylic hexameter more easily than the uncontracted *γῆος*.  
* **Modern Greek** – The distinction has disappeared; the forms have been regularized to first‑declension patterns (γῆ, γῆς, γῇ, γῆν, γῆς).  

---  

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology  

* **Proto‑Indo‑European origin** – The noun *γῆ* derives from PIE *gʰéy‑* “earth, ground”. The long η reflects an earlier *ē* that developed in Greek.  
* **Early Greek** – In Homeric and Classical Greek, nouns with vowel‑ending stems (including η‑stems) behaved like consonant‑stem nouns in the third declension, but the vowel of the stem could contract with vowel‑initial case endings.  
* **Contraction process** – The phonological rule *V₁ + V₂ → V₃* (where V₁ is a long vowel and V₂ a short vowel) was already active in the pre‑Classical period and became grammaticalized for noun morphology.  
* **Later Greek** – By the Koine period the distinction between η‑stem contract nouns and first‑declension nouns had largely faded; the former were re‑analyzed as first‑declension nouns, which explains why modern Greek treats *γῆ* as a regular first‑declension noun.  

---  

### Summary  

η‑stem contract nouns belong to the third declension because their stems end in a long η. When a vowel‑initial case ending is added, the η contracts with that vowel, producing the characteristic forms ‑ης, ‑ῃ, ‑ην, ‑ῶν, etc. The paradigm is regular, with only a few exceptions (proper names, rare masculine nouns). Mastery of the contraction rule and the full paradigm enables accurate reading and composition of Classical Greek texts that contain these nouns.
