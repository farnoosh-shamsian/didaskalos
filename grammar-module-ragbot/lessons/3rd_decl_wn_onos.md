# The 3rd Declension (wn_onos) – A Complete Lesson

---

## 1. What Is the 3rd Declension?

In Ancient Greek, nouns are grouped into four declensions.  
The **3rd declension** is the most common and the most varied.  
Its defining feature is that the **stem ends in a consonant** (or a vowel‑consonant cluster that behaves like a consonant).  
Because the stem never changes, only the endings are added to show case, number, and gender.

> **Mnemonic:** *wn_onos* – think of the stem as “w‑n‑o‑n‑o‑s”, a consonant‑ending stem that behaves like a “w” (a consonant) for the purposes of case endings.

---

## 2. Declension Patterns

Below are the full paradigms for the three genders.  
The examples are chosen to illustrate the most common endings.

### 2.1 Feminine – πόλις (city)

| Case | Singular | Plural |
|------|----------|--------|
| **Nominative** | πόλις | πόλεις |
| **Genitive** | πόλεως | πόλεων |
| **Dative** | πόλει | πόλεσι(ν) |
| **Accusative** | πόλην | πόλεις |
| **Vocative** | πόλι | πόλεις |

### 2.2 Masculine – ἄνθρωπος (man)

| Case | Singular | Plural |
|------|----------|--------|
| **Nominative** | ἄνθρωπος | ἄνθρωποι |
| **Genitive** | ἀνθρώπου | ἀνθρώπων |
| **Dative** | ἀνθρώπῳ | ἀνθρώποις |
| **Accusative** | ἄνθρωπον | ἄνθρωπους |
| **Vocative** | ἄνθρωπε | ἄνθρωποι |

### 2.3 Neuter – δῶρον (gift)

| Case | Singular | Plural |
|------|----------|--------|
| **Nominative** | δῶρον | δῶρα |
| **Genitive** | δώρου | δώρων |
| **Dative** | δώρῳ | δώροις |
| **Accusative** | δῶρον | δῶρα |
| **Vocative** | δῶρον | δῶρα |

> **Tip:** The genitive singular (*-ου* or *-ου* ending) is the key to finding the stem.  
> For nouns ending in *-ς*, drop the *-ς* and add *-ου* (e.g., πόλις → πόλεως).

---

## 3. Key Rules & Common Exceptions

| Rule | What It Means | Example |
|------|---------------|---------|
| **Fixed Stem** | The stem never changes; only endings vary. | πόλις → πόλεως |
| **Accusative Singular Ends in -ν** | All 3rd‑declension nouns take *-ν* in the accusative singular. | πόλην |
| **Vocative Singular Often Same as Nominative** | For nouns ending in *-ς*, the vocative is *-ς* (same as nominative). | πόλι |
| **Neuter Nominative = Accusative** | In the singular, the nominative and accusative are identical. | δῶρον |
| **Irregular Genitive Singular** | Some nouns have a *-ου* ending that does not follow the regular pattern. | ὁ, ἡ, τό (definite article) |
| **Nouns Ending in -ι, -υ** | These often keep the *-ι* or *-υ* in the stem and may take a *-ι* or *-υ* in the genitive. | ἡμέρα → ἡμέρας |

### 3.1 Common Irregularities

| Noun | Declension | Irregular Feature |
|------|------------|-------------------|
| ὁ, ἡ, τό | 3rd | Acts as a demonstrative pronoun; the genitive is *ὁ* (masc.), *ἡ* (fem.), *τό* (neut.) |
| ἡμέρα | 1st | Feminine only (not 3rd) |
| ἄνθρωπος | 3rd | Regular, but the genitive *ἀνθρώπου* shows the *-ου* ending |
| δῶρον | 3rd | Neuter; nominative = accusative |

## 4. Example Sentences

| Greek | English |
|-------|---------|
| ὁ ἄνθρωπος ἀκούει τὴν πόλιν. | The man hears the city. |
| ἡ πόλις ἐστὶν ἡμέρα. | The city is a day. |
| ὁ δῶρον ἐστὶν ἀγαθός. | The gift is good. |
| ὁ ἄνθρωπος ἀνθρώπου ἐστὶν ἀνθρώπου. | The man’s man is a man. *(illustrates genitive)* |

> **Note:** In the first sentence, *πόλιν* is the accusative singular of *πόλις* (city).  
> In the second, *ἡμέρα* is a 1st‑declension noun, but it shows how the article *ὁ* can be used with a 3rd‑declension noun.

---

## 5. Usage & Context

- **Most everyday nouns** belong to the 3rd declension: *ἄνθρωπος* (man), *πόλις* (city), *δῶρον* (gift).  
- **Proper names** are often 3rd‑declension: *Ἀλέξανδρος*, *Ἀθηναῖος*.  
- **Adjectives** that agree with 3rd‑declension nouns must also be in the 3rd declension.  
- **Pronouns**: The definite article *ὁ, ἡ, τό* is a 3rd‑declension pronoun and follows the same pattern.

---

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology

- The 3rd declension is the **oldest** of the Greek declensions, tracing back to Proto‑Indo‑European consonant‑ending stems.  
- The term *wn_onos* reflects the **phonological behavior** of the stem: it behaves like a consonant for case endings, even though it may contain a vowel‑consonant cluster.  
- Over time, many nouns that originally belonged to other declensions (e.g., 1st‑declension *ἡμέρα*) were re‑classified into the 3rd declension due to changes in vowel length and consonant clusters.  
- The irregular genitive singular (*-ου*) found in many 3rd‑declension nouns is a remnant of an older *-ōs* ending that contracted over centuries.

### Quick Reference Checklist

- **Identify the stem** by looking at the genitive singular (*-ου*).  
- **Add the appropriate endings** for case, number, and gender.  
- **Remember the special rules**: accusative singular ends in *-ν*, neuter nominative = accusative, vocative often same as nominative.  
- **Watch for irregularities** in the genitive singular and in nouns ending in *-ι* or *-υ*.

With these tools, you can confidently decline any 3rd‑declension noun and use it correctly in sentences. Happy learning!
