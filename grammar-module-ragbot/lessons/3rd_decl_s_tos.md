### Introduction to 3rd Declension (στος)
The 3rd declension in Greek is a fundamental part of the language, encompassing a wide range of nouns. This declension is characterized by its diverse stem types and endings, which can sometimes seem daunting to learners. However, understanding the patterns and rules governing the 3rd declension is crucial for mastering Greek.

### Forms and Patterns
The 3rd declension includes nouns that have a stem ending in a consonant or a vowel (except for α, ε, ο, which typically belong to the 1st or 2nd declensions). The nouns in this declension are divided into several subcategories based on their stem type and the genitive singular form.

#### Consonant Stems
These are the most common type of nouns in the 3rd declension. The paradigm for consonant stems can be illustrated with the noun στός (mast, pillar), which follows this pattern:

| Case | Singular | Plural |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Nominative | στός | στόες |
| Genitive | στόος | στόων |
| Dative | στόι | στόσι(ν) |
| Accusative | στόν | στόας |
| Vocative | στός | στόες |

#### Vowel Stems
Nouns with stems ending in ι, υ also belong to the 3rd declension. An example is the noun ἄκτις (ray), which declines as follows:

| Case | Singular | Plural |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Nominative | ἄκτις | ἄκτιες |
| Genitive | ἄκτιος | ἄκτιων |
| Dative | ἄκτι | ἄκτισι(ν) |
| Accusative | ἄκτιν | ἄκτιας |
| Vocative | ἄκτις | ἄκτιες |

Another example is the noun στόμα (mouth), which declines as follows:

| Case | Singular | Plural |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Nominative | στόμα | στόματα |
| Genitive | στόματος | σтомάτων |
| Dative | στόματι | στόμασι(ν) |
| Accusative | στόμα | στόματα |
| Vocative | στόμα | στόματα |

### Key Rules and Exceptions
- **Stem Changes:** Some nouns undergo changes in their stem when forming different cases. For example, nouns with stems ending in τ or δ may change to σσ or σ in certain forms.
- **Contracted Forms:** Some vowel stems contract with their endings, leading to changes in the vowel sound or length.
- **Iota Subscriptum:** In some cases, especially with nouns ending in ι or υ, an iota subscript (ᾳ, ῃ, ῳ) may appear under certain vowels in the dative singular.
- **Nominative Singular**: The nominative singular form of 3rd declension nouns can vary significantly. Some common endings include -ς, -ν, and -ρ, but there are many exceptions.
- **Genitive Singular**: The genitive singular usually ends in -ος, but there are cases where it ends differently, such as in nouns that originally had a different stem.
- **Dative Plural**: The dative plural often ends in -σι(ν), with the nu movable (elidable) before a vowel or another nu.

### Example Sentences with Translations
1. Ὁ στός ἦν ὑψηλός. (The mast was tall.)
2. Ἡ ἄκτις τοῦ ἡλίου ἦν ἰσχυρά. (The sun's ray was strong.)
3. Ὁ ἄνθρωπος ἔχει στόμα μέγα. (The man has a big mouth.)
4. Τὰ στόματα τῶν ἀνθρώπων εἰσὶν ὅμοια. (The mouths of men are similar.)

### Notes on Usage and Context
The 3rd declension is used for a wide variety of nouns, including many body parts (like στόμα), concepts, and objects. Understanding the declension patterns helps in correctly using these nouns in sentences, especially when indicating possession, location, or direct object.

### Historical Development and Etymology
The complexity of the 3rd declension in Greek reflects the language's evolution. Many of the nouns in this declension have roots in ancient Proto-Indo-European, showing similarities with nouns in other Indo-European languages. The development of Greek nouns over time, including changes in stem types and endings, has contributed to the diversity seen within the 3rd declension. Studying the etymology of these nouns can provide insights into their meanings and usage, as well as the historical context of the Greek language.
