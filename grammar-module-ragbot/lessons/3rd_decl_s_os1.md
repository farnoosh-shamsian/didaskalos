# Lesson: 3rd‑Declension Masculine Nouns Ending in **‑ος** (s_os)

---

## 1. What Is the 3rd‑Declension (s_os)?

In Classical Greek, nouns are divided into five declensions.  
The **3rd‑declension masculine nouns that end in ‑ος** in the nominative singular form a very regular subgroup.  
Because the stem ends in the consonant **s** followed by the vowel **o**, they are often called **s_os** nouns.

**Typical examples**

| Greek | English | Stem |
|-------|---------|------|
| ἀνήρ | “man” | ἀνήρ‑ |
| Ἀρχέος | “chief” | Ἀρχέ‑ |
| Ἀγών | “leader” | Ἀγών‑ |

The stem is everything that remains after the nominative ending **‑ος** is removed.

---

## 2. Declension Pattern

Below is the full paradigm for a typical s_os noun.  
Replace the stem **ἀνήρ‑** (or any other stem) with the stem of the noun you are studying.

| Case | Singular | Plural |
|------|----------|--------|
| **Nominative** | ἀνήρ | ἀνδρῶν |
| **Genitive**   | ἀνδρῶν | ἀνδρῶν |
| **Dative**     | ἀνδρῃ | ἀνδρῶν / ἀνδρῶσι(ν) |
| **Accusative** | ἀνδρὸν | ἀνδρῶν |
| **Vocative**   | ἀνήρε | ἀνδρῶν |

### Key Endings

| Form | Ending | Example |
|------|--------|---------|
| Nom. sg. | ‑ος | ἀνήρ |
| Gen. sg. | ‑ου | ἀνδρῶν |
| Dat. sg. | ‑ῃ | ἀνδρῃ |
| Acc. sg. | ‑ο | ἀνδρὸν |
| Voc. sg. | ‑ε | ἀνήρε |
| Nom./Gen./Acc./Voc. pl. | ‑ων | ἀνδρῶν |
| Dat. pl. | ‑αση / ‑ασι(ν) | ἀνδρῶσι(ν) |

> **Tip:** The dative plural can appear as **‑αση** or **‑ασι(ν)**; choose one form and use it consistently.

---

## 3. Key Rules & Exceptions

| Rule | What It Means | Example |
|------|---------------|---------|
| **1. Nominative singular ends in ‑ος** | All s_os nouns have this ending. | ἀγών, ἀρχέος |
| **2. Accusative singular ends in ‑ο** | Drop the final **‑ς** and add **‑ο**. | ἀγών → ἀγόν |
| **3. Genitive singular ends in ‑ου** | Add **‑ου** to the stem. | ἀγών → ἀγώνου |
| **4. Dative singular ends in ‑ῃ** | Add **‑ῃ** to the stem. | ἀγών → ἀγώνῃ |
| **5. Vocative singular ends in ‑ε** | Replace **‑ος** with **‑ε**. | ἀγών → ἀγώ |
| **6. Plural forms use ‑ων** | All plural cases (nom., gen., acc., voc.) share **‑ων**. | ἀγών → ἀγώνων |
| **7. Dative plural may use ‑αση or ‑ασι(ν)** | Two acceptable forms; choose one and keep it consistent. | ἀγών → ἀγώναση / ἀγώνασι(ν) |

### Common Exceptions

| Noun | Remark |
|------|--------|
| Ἀγών | Genitive **‑ου** (Ἀγώνου) – regular for this declension |
| Ἀνθρώπων | Genitive **‑ου** (Ἀνθρώπου) – irregular plural |
| Proper names | Some may have irregular plural endings (e.g., Ἀριστοτέλης → Ἀριστοτέλους) |

---

## 4. Example Sentences

| Greek | English Translation |
|-------|---------------------|
| ὁ ἀνήρ ἐν τῇ ἀγοράδι ἔσται. | The man will be in the marketplace. |
| ἔλαβεν τὸν ἀγόν ἐν τῇ ἀγοράδι. | He took the leader in the marketplace. |
| ὁ ἀρχέος ἐν τῇ ἀγοράδι ἔσται. | The chief will be in the marketplace. |
| ἔλαβεν τὸν ἀρχέον ἐν τῇ ἀγοράδι. | He took the chief in the marketplace. |

> **Why These Work**  
> - *ἀγών* (nom.) → subject of the sentence.  
> - *ἀγόν* (acc.) → direct object of *ἔλαβεν* (took).  
> - *ἀγώνῃ* (dat.) → indirect object or location in *ἐν τῇ ἀγοράδι* (in the marketplace).

---

## 5. Notes on Usage and Context

| Context | What to Expect |
|---------|----------------|
| **Common Nouns** | Everyday words like *ἀγών* (leader) and *ἀρχέος* (chief). |
| **Proper Names** | Many classical figures (e.g., *Ἀριστοτέλης*) follow this pattern. |
| **Literary Genres** | Appears in Homeric epics, tragedies, and philosophical texts. |
| **With the Definite Article** | The article does not alter the noun endings. |
| **Modern Greek** | The same nominative ending ‑ος is retained, though pronunciation may differ. |

---

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology

- **Proto‑Greek Roots** – The nominative singular **‑ος** comes from an ancient Indo‑European marker for masculine nouns, a remnant of the *‑os* suffix.  
- **Genitive Evolution** – The genitive singular **‑ου** evolved from an earlier *‑os* + *‑os* combination, which later contracted to a single **‑ου**.  
- **Dative Plural Variation** – The optional **‑αση** ending in the dative plural reflects a later phonological change where the *‑α* vowel was inserted to ease pronunciation between two consonants.  
- **Modern Greek** – Many of these nouns keep the **‑ος** ending in the nominative singular but have simplified pronunciation in modern usage.
