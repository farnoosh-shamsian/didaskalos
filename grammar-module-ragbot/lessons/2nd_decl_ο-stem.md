# The Second Declension (ο‑stem) – Masculine Nouns  

*Target level: Beginner – Intermediate*  

---  

## 1. What the Second Declension Is  

The second declension groups Greek nouns whose **stem ends in ‑ο‑**.  
For masculine nouns the nominative singular ends in **‑ος** (e.g., *ποταμός* “river”, *ἀδελφός* “brother”).  
Because the genitive singular shows the stem vowel (**‑ου**), these nouns are called **ο‑stem** nouns.  

Key characteristics  

| Feature | Detail |
|---------|--------|
| **Gender** | Primarily masculine (a few neuter nouns also belong here). |
| **Stem vowel** | ‑ο‑, visible in the genitive singular ‑ου. |
| **Typical nominative ending** | ‑ός (acute) – becomes grave (‑ὸς) when not word‑final. |
| **Accent pattern** | The acute of the nominative changes to a circumflex in the genitive and dative; a grave accent appears on the nominative when the word is not clause‑final. |

---  

## 2. Full Paradigm  

The same set of endings is used for every regular masculine ο‑stem noun.  
Below are the complete case‑number forms, first for the regular singular‑plural paradigm and then for the rare dual.

### 2.1. Singular & Plural  

| Case | Singular | Plural |
|------|----------|--------|
| **Nominative** | ‑ός → **ποταμός** | ‑οι → **ποταμοί** |
| **Genitive**   | ‑οῦ → **ποταμοῦ** | ‑ῶν → **ποταμῶν** |
| **Dative**     | ‑ῷ → **ποταμῷ** | ‑οῖς → **ποταμοῖς** |
| **Accusative**| ‑όν → **ποταμόν** | ‑ούς → **ποταμούς** |
| **Vocative**   | ‑ε → **ποταμέ** (or **ποταμέ** after dropping ‑ός) | — (same as nominative plural) |

> The paradigm of **ποταμός** is representative; replace the stem *ποταμ‑* with any other regular masculine ο‑stem (e.g., *ἀδελφ‑* → *ἀδελφός*) and the endings remain identical.

### 2.2. Dual (poetic, rare)  

| Case | Dual form |
|------|-----------|
| Nominative / Accusative | **ποταμόν** |
| Genitive | **ποταμῶν** |
| Dative | **ποταμοῖν** |

The dual appears mainly in poetry and some inscriptions; it is not used in ordinary prose.

### 2.3. Quick Reference Table  

| Case | Singular ending | Plural ending |
|------|-----------------|---------------|
| Nominative | ‑ός | ‑οι |
| Genitive   | ‑οῦ | ‑ῶν |
| Dative     | ‑ῷ | ‑οῖς |
| Accusative | ‑όν | ‑ούς |
| Vocative (sg.) | ‑ε | — |

## 3. Key Rules & Common Exceptions  

| Rule | Explanation |
|------|-------------|
| **Identify the stem** | Remove the nominative singular ‑ός to obtain the stem (e.g., *ποταμ‑*). |
| **Add the regular endings** | The same set of endings (‑οι, ‑ων, ‑οις, ‑ους) is used for every regular masculine ο‑stem noun. |
| **Accent shift** | The acute on the nominative singular ‑ός becomes a grave (‑ὸς) when the word is not clause‑final, and a circumflex (‑οῦ, ‑ῷ, ‑ῶν, ‑οῖς) in the genitive and dative. |
| **Vocative singular** | Formed by dropping ‑ός and adding ‑ε → *ποταμέ*. |
| **Plural vocative** | Identical to the nominative plural (‑οι). |
| **Neuter nouns** | A few neuter nouns belong to the second declension; they use ‑ον in the singular and ‑α/‑ων in the plural. This lesson focuses on masculine nouns. |
| **Exceptions** | Nouns that look like ‑ος but belong to the first declension (e.g., *ἡ γυνή*) or have irregular genitives (e.g., *ὁ πατήρ, gen. πατρός*) are not part of this pattern. Always verify the genitive; if it does **not** end in ‑ου, the noun is not a regular ο‑stem. |

| Greek | Transliteration | English |
|-------|----------------|---------|
| ὁ **ποταμός** τρέχει ταχέως. | ho *potamós* tréchei tachéōs. | The river flows quickly. |
| τὸν **ἀδελφὸν** ἔβλεψα ἐν τῇ ἀγορᾷ. | tòn *adelfòn* éblepsa en tē̂i agorâi. | I saw the brother in the market. |
| ἔδωκα τὰ **δῶρα** τοῖς **ποταμοῖς**. | édōka tà *dôra* toîs *potamoîs*. | I gave the gifts to the rivers. |
| ἡ **γῆ** ἐστὶν ὑπὲρ τῶν **ποταμῶν**. | hē *gē* estìn hypèr tō̂n *potamôn*. | The land is above the rivers. |

## 5. Notes on Usage and Context  

* **Everyday vocabulary** – Many common nouns (people, places, objects) are masculine ο‑stem, so mastering this declension is essential for reading narrative and historical prose.  
* **Dative of indirect object** – Frequently appears with verbs of giving, sending, or speaking (e.g., *διδῶσι τὰ δῶρα τῷ στρατηγῷ* “they give the gifts to the general”).  
* **Genitive of possession** – Used to express “of” relationships (τὸν ἥλιον τοῦ **ποταμοῦ** – “the sun of the river”).  
* **Literary style** – Poets may vary the accent (grave vs. acute) for metrical reasons, but the underlying forms stay the same.  
* **Dual** – When a poet wants to emphasize a pair (e.g., “two rivers”), the dual forms appear, though they are rare in ordinary prose.  

---  

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology  

The ο‑stem masculine nouns descend from the Proto‑Indo‑European *‑os* thematic class. In Greek the thematic vowel became **‑ο‑**, giving the characteristic nominative singular **‑ος** and genitive singular **‑ου**.  

* The shift from an acute in the nominative to a circumflex in the genitive and dative reflects an ancient lengthening of the vowel when a consonantal case ending followed.  
* The later development of a grave accent on the nominative when the word is not clause‑final marks the loss of the original pitch accent in certain phonological environments.  

These historical changes produced the regular pattern that survives unchanged from Classical Attic through Koine Greek.
