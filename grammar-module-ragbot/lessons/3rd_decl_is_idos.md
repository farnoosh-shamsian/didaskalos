# The Greek 3rd Declension (is_idos)

---

## 1. What Is the 3rd Declension?

The 3rd declension is the most varied and frequently used group of Greek nouns.  
It contains **masculine, feminine, and neuter** nouns that share a common set of endings, but the stem may end in a consonant or in a vowel. Because of this variety, the 3rd declension is often called the *“mixed”* or *“consonant”* declension.

### Why It Matters

- It is the largest declension in Greek, so mastering it gives you access to a huge portion of the vocabulary.
- The same endings are used for all three genders; the gender is determined by the stem, not by the ending.

---

## 2. Forms and Patterns

Below is a **paradigm** that shows the typical endings for each case and gender.  
Replace **X** with the noun’s stem.

| Case | Masculine | Feminine | Neuter |
|------|-----------|----------|--------|
| **Nominative** | X‑ος | X‑α | X‑ον |
| **Genitive** | X‑ου | X‑ας | X‑ου |
| **Dative** | X‑ῳ | X‑ῃ | X‑ῳ |
| **Accusative** | X‑ον | X‑αν | X‑ον |
| **Vocative** | X‑ε | X‑α | X‑ον |

### Example Paradigm

| Case | *πόλις* (city) | *ἔργον* (work) | *ἡμέρα* (day) |
|------|----------------|----------------|---------------|
| **Nom.** | πόλις | ἔργον | ἡμέρα |
| **Gen.** | πόλεως | ἔργου | ἡμέρας |
| **Dat.** | πόλει | ἔργῃ | ἡμέρᾳ |
| **Acc.** | πόλιν | ἔργον | ἡμέραν |
| **Voc.** | πόλιε | ἔργον | ἡμέρα |

> **Tip:** The neuter keeps the same form in nominative, accusative, and vocative.

---

## 3. Key Rules & Exceptions

| Rule | Explanation | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Stem ending** | Most stems end in a consonant; if the stem ends in *‑α* or *‑ο*, the nominative ends in *‑α* or *‑ο* respectively. | *ἔργον* (stem *ἔργ-*) → nominative *ἔργον* |
| **Genitive singular** | Add *‑ου* to the stem. | *πόλεως* |
| **Dative singular** | Add *‑ῳ* (masc./neut.) or *‑ῃ* (fem.). | *πόλει* |
| **Accusative singular** | Add *‑ον* (masc./neut.) or *‑αν* (fem.). | *πόλιν* |
| **Vocative singular** | Masculine: *‑ε*; Feminine: same as nominative; Neuter: same as nominative. | *πόλιε* |
| **Plural** | Add *‑ες* (masc.), *‑αι* (fem.), *‑α* (neut.) to the stem, then apply case endings. | *πόλεις* (nom. masc. pl.) |
| **Accent** | The accent may shift when endings are added; always check the accent in the dictionary. | *πόλεως* (accent on *ε*) |
| **Irregular stems** | Some nouns have irregular genitive or dative forms (e.g., *ἔργον* → *ἔργου* in genitive, *ἔργῃ* in dative). | *ἔργον* |

---

## 4. Example Sentences

| Greek | English |
|-------|---------|
| Ἡ πόλις ἐστὶν μεγάλη. | The city is great. |
| ἔργον ἔχω ὑπὸ τῆς ἡμέρας. | I have work under the day. |
| Ἀκούσατε τὴν ἡμέραν. | Listen to the day. |
| Ἀνέλαβε τὸν πόλιν. | He took the city. |

> In the second sentence, *ἔργον* is in the accusative because it is the object of *ἔχω*.

---

## 5. Notes on Usage and Context

- **Common nouns**: *πόλις* (city), *ἔργον* (work), *ἡμέρα* (day), *ἄνθρωπος* (man), *γυνή* (woman).
- **Adjectives**: Many adjectives belong to the 3rd declension (e.g., *ἅγιος* “holy”).
- **Proper names**: Many Greek proper names are 3rd declension (e.g., *Ἀλέξανδρος*).
- **Neuter nouns**: Often denote abstract concepts (*ἔργον*, *ἡμέρα*).

---

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology

- **Proto‑Indo‑European roots**: The 3rd‑declension stems from PIE nouns with a *‑o* or *‑i* stem, which evolved into Greek consonant stems.
- **Etymology of endings**:
  - *‑ος* (masc.) → from PIE *‑os*.
  - *‑α* (fem.) → from PIE *‑ā*.
  - *‑ον* (neut.) → from PIE *‑on*.
- **Evolution**: Over time, many 3rd‑declension nouns lost their original endings, leaving only the stem plus the case endings. This created the “mixed” nature of the declension.
