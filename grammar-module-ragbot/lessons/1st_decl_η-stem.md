### Introduction to 1st Declension (η-stem)
The 1st declension in Greek, also known as the η-stem declension, is a category of nouns that are primarily feminine in gender. These nouns are characterized by their ending in -η (eta) in the nominative singular form. Understanding the 1st declension is crucial for any student of Greek, as it encompasses a significant number of common nouns.

### Grammatical Concept
In Greek, nouns are categorized into different declensions based on their grammatical endings. The 1st declension includes nouns that end in -η (eta) in the nominative singular form. These nouns are typically feminine, but there are some exceptions. The η-stem declension is used to describe a wide range of concepts, including objects, places, and ideas.

### Forms and Patterns
The η-stem nouns follow a specific pattern of declension across different cases (nominative, genitive, dative, accusative) in both singular and plural forms. The following tables illustrate the declension of typical η-stem nouns:

#### Example 1: γαστὴρ (gastēr) - "stomach" or "belly"
| Case | Singular | Plural |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Nominative | γαστὴρ (gastēr) | γαστῆρες (gastēres) |
| Genitive | γαστῆρος (gastēros) | γαστὴρων (gastērōn) |
| Dative | γαστῆρι (gastēri) | γαστῆρσι (gastērsi) |
| Accusative | γαστῆρα (gastēra) | γαστῆρας (gastēras) |

#### Example 2: χθών (chthōn) - "earth" or "ground"
| Case | Singular | Plural |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Nominative | χθών (chthōn) | χθῶνες (chthōnes) |
| Genitive | χθῶνος (chthōnos) | χθώνων (chthōnōn) |
| Dative | χθῶνι (chthōni) | χθῶσι (chthōsi) |
| Accusative | χθῶνα (chthōna) | χθῶνας (chthōnas) |

#### Example 3: ποταμ-ή (potam-ē) - "river"
| Case | Singular | Plural |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Nominative | ποταμ-ή (potam-ē) | ποταμ-αί (potam-ai) |
| Genitive | ποταμ-ῆς (potam-ēs) | ποταμ-ῶν (potam-ōn) |
| Dative | ποταμ-ῇ (potam-ēi) | ποταμ-αῖς (potam-ais) |
| Accusative | ποταμ-ήν (potam-ēn) | ποταμ-άς (potam-as) |

### Key Rules and Exceptions
The key rules for the 1st declension are as follows:
* The nominative singular form ends in -η (eta).
* The genitive singular form ends in -ης (ēs).
* The dative singular form ends in -ῇ (ēi).
* The accusative singular form ends in -ην (ēn).
* The nominative plural form ends in -αί (ai).
* The genitive plural form ends in -ῶν (ōn).
* The dative plural form ends in -αῖς (ais).
* The accusative plural form ends in -άς (as).
Exceptions to these rules include nouns that have a different stem in the plural form, such as γυνή (gunē) "woman" which becomes γυναικ- (gunaik-) in the plural.

### Notes on Usage and Context
The 1st declension is used to describe feminine nouns, such as γυνή (gunē) "woman", θάλασσα (thálassa) "sea", and ποταμ-ή (potam-ē) "river". The context in which these nouns are used can affect their meaning and grammatical function. Understanding the declension patterns and being able to recognize these nouns in different cases is essential for reading and writing in Greek.

### Historical Development and Etymology
The η-stem declension has its roots in ancient Proto-Indo-European and has evolved over time, influenced by the phonological and morphological changes within the Greek language. Many η-stem nouns have cognates in other Indo-European languages, reflecting a shared ancestral vocabulary. The study of these nouns can provide insights into the history and spread of languages across Europe and Asia. The η-stem declension is believed to have originated from the Proto-Indo-European suffix *-eh2, which was used to form feminine nouns. Over time, this suffix evolved into the η-stem declension in Ancient Greek.
