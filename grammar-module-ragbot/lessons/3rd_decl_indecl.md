### Introduction to 3rd Declension (Indeclinable) Nouns
#### Overview
In the Greek language, nouns are categorized into different declensions based on their grammatical behavior. The 3rd declension is a unique group that includes nouns which do not follow the typical patterns of the first and second declensions. Within the 3rd declension, there is a subgroup known as indeclinable nouns. These nouns do not change their form regardless of the case (nominative, genitive, dative, accusative, ablative) they are in.

### Forms and Patterns
Indeclinable nouns in Greek are typically borrowed words, proper nouns, or nouns that have been adapted from other languages. Since they do not change form, they are relatively straightforward but can be confusing for learners because they do not fit the usual patterns of declension.

The following table illustrates the forms of an indeclinable noun:

|  | Singular |  | Plural | 
| --- | --- | --- | --- | 
| Nominative | ἄνθρωπος | the person | ἄνθρωπος | the person | 
| Genitive | ἄνθρωπος | of the person | ἄνθρωπος | of the person | 
| Dative | ἄνθρωπος | to/for the person | ἄνθρωπος | to/for the person | 
| Accusative | ἄνθρωπος | the person (object) | ἄνθρωπος | the person (object) | 

Another example of indeclinable nouns is:

| Noun | Meaning |
| --- | --- |
| ἄβρος | delicate, dainty |
| ἄγριος | wild |
| ἄρρην | male |

These nouns maintain their form across all cases. For example, the noun "ἄβρος" remains "ἄβρος" whether it is the subject of the sentence (nominative), the object (accusative), or shows possession (genitive).

### Key Rules and Exceptions
The primary rule for indeclinable nouns is that they do not change their form to indicate case. This means that the same form is used for all cases (nominative, genitive, dative, accusative). There are no specific exceptions to this rule within the indeclinable subgroup, as the defining characteristic of these nouns is their lack of declension.

### Example Sentences with Translations
Here are a few example sentences using indeclinable nouns:

* **Ἄβρος ἦν τὸ παιδίον.** - The child was delicate.
* **Ἄγριος ἄνθρωπος ἦν ἐκεῖνος.** - That man was wild.
* **ἄνθρωπος ἦλθεν.** - A person came.
* **τὸν ἄνθρωπον εἶδον.** - I saw the person.
* **ἄνθρωπος ἐστὶν ἀγαθός.** - The person is good.

### Notes on Usage and Context
Indeclinable nouns are used in the same way as other nouns in terms of syntax and sentence structure. However, their invariable form can sometimes make it more challenging to determine the grammatical case without considering the context and the words surrounding them. It is essential to recognize these nouns and understand their behavior to communicate effectively in Greek.

### Historical Development and Etymology
The concept of indeclinable nouns in Greek is closely related to the language's historical development. In ancient Greek, some nouns were borrowed from other languages or were derived from roots that did not follow the typical declension patterns. Over time, these nouns became indeclinable, meaning their form remained unchanged in all cases. Understanding the etymology of these nouns can provide valuable insights into the language's evolution and the cultural exchange that occurred in the ancient world. For example, words related to trade, technology, or foreign places might be indeclinable because they were adopted from languages with different grammatical structures.
