# Lesson: 3rd Declension – ι/εω‑Stem Nouns  

Greek nouns of the 3rd declension are the most varied in form.  
The **ι‑stem** and **εω‑stem** groups are the two most common patterns.  
This lesson explains how they are formed, how they decline, and when to use them.

---

## 1. What is the 3rd Declension?

- **3rd Declension** nouns have stems that end in a consonant or a vowel that is not the typical 1st‑ or 2nd‑declension endings.  
- They are divided into two main sub‑groups:
  - **ι‑stem** nouns (stem ends in *-ι* or *-ις* in the nominative singular)
  - **εω‑stem** nouns (stem ends in *-εω* in the nominative singular)

Both groups share the same set of case endings, but the stem changes slightly in some cases.

---

## 2. Forms and Patterns

### 2.1 ι‑Stem Nouns

| Case | Masculine | Feminine | Neuter |
|------|-----------|----------|--------|
| **Nominative** | *-ις* | *-η* | *-ον* |
| **Genitive** | *-ου* | *-ης* | *-ου* |
| **Dative** | *-ι* | *-ῃ* | *-ῃ* |
| **Accusative** | *-ην* | *-ην* | *-ον* |
| **Vocative** | *-ε* | *-η* | *-ον* |

**Example:**  
- *ἄνθρωπος* (man) → *ἄνθρωπος* (nom.), *ἄνθρωπου* (gen.), *ἄνθρωπῳ* (dat.), *ἄνθρωπον* (acc.), *ἄνθρωπε* (voc.)

### 2.2 εω‑Stem Nouns

| Case | Masculine | Feminine | Neuter |
|------|-----------|----------|--------|
| **Nominative** | *-εω* | *-η* | *-ον* |
| **Genitive** | *-ου* | *-ης* | *-ου* |
| **Dative** | *-εῖ* | *-ῃ* | *-ῃ* |
| **Accusative** | *-εῖν* | *-ην* | *-ον* |
| **Vocative** | *-ε* | *-η* | *-ον* |

**Example:**  
- *ἔργον* (work) → *ἔργον* (nom.), *ἔργου* (gen.), *ἔργῳ* (dat.), *ἔργον* (acc.), *ἔργον* (voc.)

---

## 3. Key Rules & Exceptions

| Rule | Explanation | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Stem ending** | ι‑stem nouns keep the *-ι* in the nominative singular; εω‑stem nouns keep *-εω*. | *ἄνθρωπος* (ι‑stem), *ἔργον* (εω‑stem) |
| **Genitive singular** | Both groups use *-ου* for masculine/neuter and *-ης* for feminine. | *ἄνθρωπου*, *ἔργου*, *ἡμέρας* |
| **Dative singular** | Masculine ι‑stem uses *-ι*; feminine and neuter use *-ῃ*. εω‑stem uses *-εῖ* for masculine, *-ῃ* for feminine/neuter. | *ἄνθρωπῳ*, *ἔργῳ*, *ἡμέρᾳ* |
| **Accusative singular** | Masculine ι‑stem uses *-ην*; feminine uses *-ην*; neuter uses *-ον*. εω‑stem masculine uses *-εῖν*, feminine *-ην*, neuter *-ον*. | *ἄνθρωπον*, *ἔργον*, *ἔργον* |
| **Vocative singular** | Masculine ι‑stem uses *-ε*; feminine uses *-η*; neuter uses *-ον*. εω‑stem masculine uses *-ε*, feminine *-η*, neuter *-ον*. | *ἄνθρωπε*, *ἔργον*, *ἔργον* |
| **Plural** | Plural endings are the same for both groups: *-οι* (nom./voc.), *-ων* (gen.), *-οις* (dat.), *-ους* (acc.). | *ἄνθρωποι*, *ἔργων*, *ἔργοις*, *ἔργους* |
| **Exceptions** | Some nouns have irregular stems (e.g., *ἄνθρωπος* → *ἄνθρωπος* but *ἔργον* → *ἔργον*). | *ἔργον* → *ἔργον* (no change) |

## 5. Usage & Context

- **Common nouns**: *ἔργον* (work), *ἡμέρα* (day), *ἄνθρωπος* (man), *ἔργον* (work), *ἔργον* (work).
- **Abstract vs. concrete**: Many ι‑stem nouns are abstract (e.g., *ἔργον*), while εω‑stem nouns often denote concrete objects (e.g., *ἔργον*).
- **Gender distribution**: Masculine and neuter ι‑stem nouns are frequent; feminine ι‑stem nouns are less common but still present (e.g., *ἡμέρα*).

---

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology

- **ι‑stem**: The *-ι* ending originates from the Proto‑Greek *-i* suffix that marked masculine nouns. Over time, the vowel *i* was retained in the nominative singular but dropped in other cases, giving the characteristic pattern.
- **εω‑stem**: The *-εω* ending comes from the Proto‑Greek *-eō* suffix, a later development that created a distinct class of nouns. The *-εω* is preserved in the nominative singular but changes to *-εῖ* in the dative and *-εῖν* in the accusative.
- Both groups reflect the **consonant‑stem** tradition of Greek, where the stem ends in a consonant or a vowel that is not the typical *-α* or *-ος* endings of the 1st and 2nd declensions.
