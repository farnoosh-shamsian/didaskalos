### Introduction to 1st Declension (α/η-stem)
The 1st declension in Greek is a category of nouns that follow a specific pattern of declension. These nouns typically end in -α (alpha) or -η (eta) in their nominative singular form. Understanding the 1st declension is crucial for reading and writing in Greek, as it encompasses a wide range of nouns, including those related to geography, anatomy, and more.

### Forms and Patterns
The 1st declension nouns follow specific patterns for each case in the singular and plural. The tables below illustrate these forms for typical 1st declension nouns.

### Key Rules and Exceptions
- The 1st declension includes nouns that end in -α or -η in the nominative singular.
- Most 1st declension nouns are feminine, but there are some masculine nouns that follow this pattern.
- The genitive singular of masculine nouns in the 1st declension ends in -ος (os), while feminine nouns end in -ας (as).
- The dative singular of masculine nouns ends in -ι (i), while feminine nouns end in -ᾳ (ā).
- **Nominative Singular**: Ends in -α or -η.
- **Genitive Singular**: Ends in -ας for nouns ending in -α, and -ης for those ending in -η.
- **Dative Singular**: The same as the nominative form.
- **Accusative Singular**: The same as the nominative form.
- **Vocative Singular**: The same as the nominative form.
- **Plural Forms**: Follow the patterns shown in the table, with -αι for nominative and vocative plural, -ων for genitive plural, -αις for dative plural, and -ας for accusative plural.

### Example Sentences with Translations
1. ἡ χώρα ἡμετέρα ἐστὶν ἐν Εὐρώπῃ. (hē chōrā hēmetéra estin en Eurōpē) - Our country is in Europe.
2. ὁ γαστήρ μου πονεῖ. (ho gastēr mou ponei) - My stomach hurts.
3. Ἡ γυναίκα ἔρχεται. (hē gynaika erchetai) - The woman is coming.

### Notes on Usage and Context
1st declension nouns are used in various contexts, including descriptions of people, places, and things. The gender of the noun (feminine for most 1st declension nouns) affects the form of adjectives and other grammatical elements that modify it. Understanding the declension pattern of these nouns is crucial for correct usage in sentences.

### Historical Development and Etymology
The 1st declension nouns in Greek have evolved from ancient Proto-Indo-European roots. The -α and -η endings are characteristic of feminine nouns in Greek, reflecting a pattern seen in other Indo-European languages. The development of these nouns over time has been influenced by the language's phonological and grammatical changes, resulting in the complex but systematic patterns observed in ancient and modern Greek.
