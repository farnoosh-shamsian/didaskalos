# Lesson 3: The Third Declension (r_ros) – Nouns in Greek

Greek nouns are divided into four declensions. The third declension, often called the **r_ros** class, is the most varied and the most common in everyday Greek. It contains the majority of masculine, feminine, and neuter nouns, including many of the most important words in the language.

---

## 1. What Is the Third Declension?

- **Definition**: A group of nouns whose stems end in a consonant or a vowel that is not *-α* or *-η*.  
- **Why it matters**: Most Greek nouns you will encounter belong to this declension, so mastering it gives you a solid foundation for reading and speaking.

---

## 2. Declension Patterns

Below are the standard endings for the third declension. The tables are split by gender because the endings differ for masculine/feminine and neuter nouns.

### 2.1 Masculine / Feminine Nouns

| Case | Singular | Plural |
|------|----------|--------|
| Nominative | -ος | -οι |
| Genitive | -ου | -ων |
| Dative | -ῳ | -οις |
| Accusative | -ον | -ους |
| Vocative | -ε | -οι |

### 2.2 Neuter Nouns

| Case | Singular | Plural |
|------|----------|--------|
| Nominative | -ον | -α |
| Genitive | -ου | -ων |
| Dative | -ῳ | -οις |
| Accusative | -ον | -α |
| Vocative | -ον | -α |

> **Tip** – The genitive singular *-ου* and dative singular *-ῳ* are the same for both genders. The difference appears in the nominative and accusative singular.

---

## 3. Key Rules & Common Patterns

| Rule | Explanation | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Stem ends in a consonant** | Most third‑declension nouns have stems that finish with a consonant (e.g., *λόγος*). | *λόγος* → *λόγου* (genitive) |
| **Stem ends in -α or -η** | These nouns behave like third‑declension nouns but keep the vowel in the stem. | *ἄνθρωπος* (masc.) → *ἄνθρωπου* |
| **Stem ends in -ι** | The stem may change to *-ι-* in some forms. | *ἔργον* (neut.) → *ἔργου* |
| **Vocative singular** | Masculine/feminine nouns use *-ε*; neuter nouns keep *-ον*. | *λόγος* → *λόγε* (vocative) |
| **Plural formation** | Masculine/feminine: *-οι* (nom.), *-ους* (acc.), *-οις* (dat.). Neuter: *-α* (nom./acc.), *-οις* (dat.). | *λόγοι* (nom. pl.) |
| **Genitive plural** | All genders use *-ων*. | *λόγων* |

---

## 4. Exceptions & Special Cases

| Exception | What Happens | Example |
|-----------|--------------|---------|
| **Nouns ending in -ος but with a stem that ends in a vowel** | The stem may change in the genitive (e.g., *Ἀριστοτέλης* → *Ἀριστοτέλους*). | *Ἀριστοτέλης* → *Ἀριστοτέλους* |
| **Nouns with a stem ending in -ι** | The genitive may show a *-ου* but the dative can be *-ῳ* or *-ι* depending on the word. | *ἔργον* → *ἔργου* |
| **Nouns that are actually second‑declension in form** | Some nouns look like third‑declension but are treated as second‑declension (e.g., *ἄνθρωπος* is third‑declension, *Ἀθηναῖος* is second‑declension). | *Ἀθηναῖος* → *Ἀθηναίου* (second‑declension) |

---

## 5. Example Sentences

| Greek | English |
|-------|---------|
| ὁ λόγος ἐν τῇ ἀλήθειᾳ ἐστίν. | The word is in truth. |
| ἔγραψεν τὸν λόγον τῆς ἀρετῆς. | He wrote the word of virtue. |
| ἔργον ἔσται ὁ ἄνθρωπος. | The man will be a work. |
| ἔγραψεν τὸν ἔργον. | He wrote the work. |
| ὁ λόγος ἐν τῇ ἀλήθειᾳ. | The word in truth. |

> **Note** – In the first sentence, *λόγος* is in the nominative singular. In the second, *λόγον* is accusative singular. The dative *τῇ* shows the indirect object.

---

## 6. Usage & Context

- **Common nouns**: *λόγος* (word), *ἔργον* (work), *ἄνθρωπος* (man), *ἡμέρα* (day), *ἔργον* (work).
- **Academic & literary texts**: Many philosophical terms belong to this declension (*ἔργον*, *ἔργον*).
- **Everyday speech**: Most everyday nouns are third‑declension, so learning this class is essential for fluency.

---

## 7. Historical Development & Etymology

- **Proto‑Indo‑European roots**: The third declension in Greek descends from the PIE *-os* and *-on* endings that marked masculine and neuter nouns, respectively.
- **Sound changes**: Over time, the consonant endings of the stems were preserved, while the endings of the cases evolved. For example, the PIE *-os* became Greek *-ος* in the nominative singular.
- **Etymological note**: Many third‑declension nouns are inherited from ancient Greek and have cognates in other Indo‑European languages (e.g., Latin *verbum* < PIE *wérbʰ*).

---

## 8. Summary

- The third declension (r_ros) is the most common noun class in Greek.
- Masculine/feminine nouns share the same endings; neuter nouns have a distinct set.
- Key endings: *-ος/-ον* (nom.), *-ου* (gen.), *-ῳ* (dat.), *-ον/-ους* (acc.),
