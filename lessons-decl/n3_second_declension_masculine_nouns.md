# Second Declension Masculine Nouns (o-stems)

Second declension nouns in **-ος** are the single most common noun type in Greek prose. Most are masculine: λόγος "word", φίλος "friend", ἄνθρωπος "human being". A few are feminine but decline identically — ἡ ὁδός "road", ἡ νῆσος "island" — and only the article reveals their gender.

## How to recognize the class

- Nominative singular in **-ος**, genitive singular in **-ου**.
- Dictionary entry: λόγος, -ου, ὁ.

## Paradigm

λόγος "word":

| Case | Singular | Plural |
|---|---|---|
| Nominative | λόγος | λόγοι |
| Genitive | λόγου | λόγων |
| Dative | λόγῳ | λόγοις |
| Accusative | λόγον | λόγους |
| Vocative | λόγε | λόγοι |

Note the iota subscript in the dative singular (-ῳ): it is silent but always written, and it is often the only thing distinguishing dative λόγῳ from another form.

## Common vocabulary

| Noun | Genitive | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| ἄνθρωπος | ἀνθρώπου | human being |
| θεός | θεοῦ | god |
| νόμος | νόμου | law, custom |
| πόλεμος | πολέμου | war |
| δῆμος | δήμου | people |
| ἵππος | ἵππου | horse |
| ἡ ὁδός | ὁδοῦ | road, way (feminine!) |

## Points to watch

- **Learn each noun with its article.** ὁ λόγος but ἡ ὁδός — the ending alone does not tell you the gender.
- The vocative singular in -ε is distinctive: ὦ φίλε "friend!" (θεός usually keeps θεός in the vocative.)
- Contract nouns like νοῦς "mind" (from νόος) belong here too: νοῦς, νοῦ, νῷ, νοῦν.
- Do not confuse the accusative singular -ον with the neuter nouns of the next lesson; for masculines, nominative -ος and accusative -ον differ, while neuters use -ον for both.

## Examples

- ὁ νόμος τοὺς ἀνθρώπους παιδεύει — "the law educates people"
- ἐν τῇ ὁδῷ — "on the road" (feminine article + dative)
- οἱ τοῦ δήμου λόγοι — "the words of the people"
