# Conjunctions

## 1. What Conjunctions Are

Conjunctions **connect** — words, phrases, or whole clauses. They show addition ("and"), contrast ("but"), alternatives ("or"), cause ("because"), condition ("if"), purpose ("in order that"), and time ("when"). Like particles they are uninflected; unlike most particles they have a clearer logical job.

Greek builds long, articulated sentences out of short clauses, and conjunctions are the joints. Learning the twenty or so common ones pays off immediately.

## 2. Coordinating Conjunctions

These join **equal** elements — two nouns, two verbs, two independent clauses:

| Conjunction | Meaning | Example |
|---|---|---|
| καί | and; also, even | ὁ πατὴρ καὶ ἡ μήτηρ "the father and the mother" |
| τε (enclitic) | and (attaches to its word) | ἄνδρες γυναῖκές τε "men and women" |
| δέ | and, but (postpositive) | ὁ δὲ ἀπῆλθεν "and he went away" |
| ἀλλά | but (strong contrast, correction) | οὐ λόγων ἀλλ᾿ ἔργων "not words but deeds" |
| ἤ | or; than | νῦν ἢ ὕστερον "now or later" |
| οὐδέ / μηδέ | and not, not even | οὐδὲ εἷς "not even one" |
| γάρ | for (introduces a reason; postpositive) | φεύγωμεν· ὁ γὰρ πολέμιος ἐγγύς. "Let's flee — for the enemy is near." |
| οὖν | therefore (postpositive) | λέγωμεν οὖν "let us speak, then" |

Useful pairs: **τε … καί / καὶ … καί** "both … and"; **ἢ … ἤ** "either … or"; **οὔτε … οὔτε** "neither … nor".

Note also that **καί** before a single word means "also, even": καὶ σύ "you too".

## 3. Subordinating Conjunctions

These introduce **dependent clauses** and tell you exactly what kind:

| Conjunction | Introduces | Example |
|---|---|---|
| ὅτι / ὡς | statement: "that"; cause: "because" | λέγει ὅτι ἥκει "he says that he has come" |
| εἰ | condition: "if" | εἰ θεοὶ εἰσίν "if there are gods" |
| ἐάν (= εἰ + ἄν) | condition with subjunctive: "if (ever)" | ἐὰν ζητῇς, εὑρήσεις "if you seek, you will find" |
| ἐπεί / ἐπειδή | "when, since, after" | ἐπεὶ ταῦτα ἤκουσεν "when he heard this" |
| ὅτε | "when" | ὅτε νέος ἦν "when he was young" |
| ὅταν (= ὅτε + ἄν) | "whenever" | ὅταν βούλῃ "whenever you wish" |
| ἵνα / ὅπως | purpose: "in order that" | μανθάνομεν ἵνα σοφοὶ ὦμεν "we learn in order to be wise" |
| ὥστε | result: "so that, with the result that" | οὕτω σοφὸς ἦν ὥστε πάντες ἐθαύμαζον "he was so wise that all marveled" |
| πρίν | "before, until" | πρὶν ἐλθεῖν "before coming" |
| ἕως | "while, until" | ἕως ἔξεστιν "while it is possible" |
| καίπερ | concession, with participle: "although" | καίπερ σοφὸς ὤν "although being wise" |

The conjunction is your **advance warning** of the clause's grammar: ἵνα and ὅπως point to a subjunctive (or optative), ὥστε often to an infinitive, ἐάν and ὅταν always to a subjunctive. Reading Greek well means letting these little words set your expectations.

## 4. Position

Most conjunctions stand **first in their clause**: καί, ἀλλά, εἰ, ὅτι, ἐπεί, ἵνα…

The postpositives — δέ, γάρ, οὖν, τε — come **second** (or attach to the first word). This mix is a feature, not a bug: a Greek clause almost always announces its logical relationship within its first two words.

## 5. Conjunctions vs. Particles

The boundary is soft. δέ, γάρ, and οὖν are traditionally counted as particles yet function as clause connectors; treebanks and grammars draw the line differently. Do not worry about the label — learn what each word *does*.

## 6. Points to Watch

- **ἤ** means both "or" and "than" (after a comparative): μείζων ἢ ἐγώ "bigger than I".
- **ὡς** is heavily overloaded: "that", "as", "when", "in order that", "how" — the mood of the verb and the context decide.
- Negative in the subordinate clause follows its type: factual ὅτι-clauses take οὐ, purpose and conditional clauses take μή: ἵνα μὴ ἁμάρτῃ "so that he may not err".
- After verbs of saying, Greek often prefers an **infinitive construction** instead of ὅτι — both are common.

## 7. Example Sentences

- λέγουσιν ὅτι ὁ βασιλεὺς ἥκει. — "They say that the king has come."
- ἐὰν τοῦτο ποιῇς, χαιρήσεις. — "If you do this, you will be glad."
- μανθάνομεν ἵνα σοφώτεροι γιγνώμεθα. — "We learn so that we may become wiser."
- οὕτω ταχέως ἔδραμεν ὥστε πάντας νικῆσαι. — "He ran so fast that he beat everyone."
