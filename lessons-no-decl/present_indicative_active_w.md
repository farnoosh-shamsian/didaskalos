# Lesson: The Present Indicative Active of ‑ω Verbs

*Target audience: beginner–intermediate students who already know the Greek alphabet and basic noun declension.*

---

## 1. What the Present Indicative Active Is

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Present** | The action is going on **now** or habitually, or states a permanent truth. |
| **Indicative** | The "statement" mood — for factual or neutral assertions (as opposed to subjunctive, optative, imperative). |
| **Active** | The subject **performs** the action (as opposed to middle or passive). |
| **‑ω** | The ending of the **1st person singular** present indicative active in most verbs. All the other forms are built on the same stem. |

The present indicative active is therefore the "default" form of the verb, and the one you will meet most often in both prose and verse: *λέγω* "I say," *γράφω* "I write," *παιδεύω* "I educate."

---

## 2. Forms and Patterns

Verbs ending in ‑ω fall into two main groups in the present system:

1. **Plain thematic verbs** — a linking vowel (ε/ο) stands between stem and ending, and nothing else happens: λύω, γράφω, πίνω.
2. **Contract verbs** — the stem ends in α, ε, or ο, and that vowel merges with the linking vowel and ending: τιμάω, φιλέω, δηλόω.

Irregular verbs (such as *εἰμί*) are treated under "Exceptions" below.

### 2.1 Plain Thematic Verbs (model: **λύω** "to loose")

| Person | Ending | Form | Gloss |
|--------|--------|------|-------|
| 1st sg. | ‑ω | **λύω** | I loose |
| 2nd sg. | ‑εις | **λύεις** | you (sg.) loose |
| 3rd sg. | ‑ει | **λύει** | he/she/it looses |
| 1st pl. | ‑ομεν | **λύομεν** | we loose |
| 2nd pl. | ‑ετε | **λύετε** | you (pl.) loose |
| 3rd pl. | ‑ουσι(ν) | **λύουσι(ν)** | they loose |

*Note:* the thematic vowel appears as **ο** before μ and ν (λύ‑ο‑μεν) and as **ε** elsewhere (λύ‑ε‑τε); in ‑εις and ‑ει it has already fused with the old personal ending.

### 2.2 Contract Verbs

The final vowel of the stem merges with the ending. There are three sub-types:

| Sub-type | Example | Present indicative active forms |
|----------|---------|---------------------------------|
| **α‑contract** | **τιμάω** "to honour" (stem *τιμα‑*) | τιμῶ, τιμᾷς, τιμᾷ, τιμῶμεν, τιμᾶτε, τιμῶσι(ν) |
| **ε‑contract** | **φιλέω** "to love" (stem *φιλε‑*) | φιλῶ, φιλεῖς, φιλεῖ, φιλοῦμεν, φιλεῖτε, φιλοῦσι(ν) |
| **ο‑contract** | **δηλόω** "to make clear" (stem *δηλο‑*) | δηλῶ, δηλοῖς, δηλοῖ, δηλοῦμεν, δηλοῦτε, δηλοῦσι(ν) |

*In real texts the ε‑contracts (φιλέω, ποιέω) and α‑contracts (τιμάω, ὁράω) are by far the most common.*

## 3. Key Rules & Common Exceptions

| Rule | Explanation | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **1. The 1st sg. always ends in ‑ω** | This is the "base" form recorded by dictionaries. | *γράφω* "I write" |
| **2. The 2nd sg. ending is ‑εις** (α‑contract: ‑ᾷς) | In contract verbs the stem vowel has merged into the ending. | *λύεις* vs. *τιμᾷς* |
| **3. The 3rd sg. ending is ‑ει** (α‑contract: ‑ᾷ) | The same contraction rule. | *λύει* vs. *τιμᾷ* |
| **4. The 1st pl. ending is ‑ομεν** | The thematic vowel ο appears before μ. | *γράφομεν*, *πίνομεν* |
| **5. The 2nd pl. ending is ‑ετε** | Thematic vowel ε. | *γράφετε* |
| **6. The 3rd pl. ending is ‑ουσι(ν)** | The final **ν** is the **movable ν**: it appears before a vowel and at the end of a sentence. | *γράφουσι(ν)* |
| **7. Contract verbs merge the stem vowel with the ending** | α + ω → ω; ε + ο → ου; ε + ε → ει; ο + ε/ο → ου, and so on. | *τιμῶ* (from *τιμα‑ + ω*), *φιλοῦμεν* (from *φιλε‑ + ομεν*) |
| **8. Irregular verbs** | A few very common verbs have special stems or endings. | *εἰμί* "I am", *φημί* "I say" (both belong to the ‑μι family) |
| **9. Accent** | The accent of finite verb forms is **recessive** — as far from the end of the word as the rules allow (γράφομεν, not *γραφόμεν*). | *παιδεύομεν* |

### 3.1 Irregular Verbs Worth Knowing

| Verb | 1st sg. | Conjugation (selected) | Note |
|------|---------|------------------------|------|
| **εἰμί** "to be" | **εἰμί** | εἰμί, εἶ, ἐστί(ν), ἐσμέν, ἐστέ, εἰσί(ν) | Suppletive stem and irregular forms; most of its forms are enclitic. |
| **ἔχω** "to have" | **ἔχω** | ἔχω, ἔχεις, ἔχει, ἔχομεν, ἔχετε, ἔχουσι(ν) | The endings are perfectly regular. |
| **ὁράω** "to see" (α‑contract) | **ὁρῶ** | ὁρῶ, ὁρᾷς, ὁρᾷ, ὁρῶμεν, ὁρᾶτε, ὁρῶσι(ν) | A contract verb; the long ω results from α + ω. |
| **ἔρχομαι** "to come" | — | — | Middle-only (deponent); treated in the middle lessons. |

## 4. Example Sentences

| Greek (present indicative active) | Transliteration | English |
|-----------------------------------|-----------------|---------|
| **λέγω τὸν νόμον.** | *légō ton nómon* | I recite the law. |
| **φιλεῖς τὸν φίλον σου.** | *phileîs ton phílon sou* | You love your friend. |
| **πίνουσιν οἶνον ἐν τῇ ἀγορᾷ.** | *pínousin oînon en têi agorâi* | They drink wine in the marketplace. |
| **τιμῶ τὸν πατέρα μου.** | *timô ton patéra mou* | I honour my father. |
| **ἐγὼ ἔχω τὰ βιβλία.** | *egṓ ékhō ta biblía* | I have the books. |
| **ὁ ἀνὴρ ὁρᾷ τὸν οὐρανόν.** | *ho anḗr horâi ton ouranón* | The man sees the sky. |

*Notice the verb endings:* **‑ω**, **‑εις**, **‑ει**, **‑ομεν**, **‑ετε**, **‑ουσι(ν)**.

---

## 5. Usage & Context

1. **Narrative prose** — the present indicative is the default tense for describing actions that happen **at the time of narration**.
2. **Historical present** — historians often use the present for past events to make the scene vivid and immediate.
3. **General truths** — the present states permanent facts: *ὁ ἥλιος ἀνατέλλει* "the sun rises."
4. **Habitual actions** — regularly repeated activity is also expressed by the present: "he writes every day."

---

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology

- The thematic conjugation, with its linking vowel ε/ο, is part of Greek's Indo-European inheritance; the ending ‑ω continues the old first-person-singular ending of the parent language.
- The **contract verbs** are not a separate conjugation but ordinary thematic verbs whose stems happened to end in a vowel; the contractions are a regular Attic sound change, and Homeric Greek often preserves the uncontracted forms (*ὁράω* beside Attic *ὁρῶ*).
- The ω‑verb pattern proved remarkably stable, and in later Greek the older μι‑verbs gradually migrated into it.
- Many ω‑verbs survive in English loanwords: *graph* and *graphic* come from the same root as γράφω.
