### Introduction to ω-Verbs
ω-Verbs are the largest category of Greek verbs, and knowing them is essential for reading and understanding Greek texts. This lesson focuses on the present tense, the participle, and the active voice of these verbs.

### Explanation of the Grammatical Concept
ω-Verbs are identified by the ending ‑ω in the first person singular of the present. The present tense expresses an action in progress or a general truth. The **participle** is a verb form that behaves like an adjective: it describes a noun and agrees with it in gender, number, and case. The **active voice** shows that the subject of the sentence performs the action.

### Forms and Patterns
The present indicative active of ω-verbs is built on the present stem with these endings:

| Person | Singular | Plural |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1st | ‑ω | ‑ομεν |
| 2nd | ‑εις | ‑ετε |
| 3rd | ‑ει | ‑ουσι(ν) |

For example, the verb γράφω, "to write":

| Person | Singular | Plural |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1st | γράφω | γράφομεν |
| 2nd | γράφεις | γράφετε |
| 3rd | γράφει | γράφουσι(ν) |

The present active participle is formed on the present stem with these markers:
- **‑ων** (masculine), **‑ουσα** (feminine), **‑ον** (neuter)

Using γράφω as an example, in the nominative:

| Gender | Singular | Plural |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Masculine | γράφων | γράφοντες |
| Feminine | γράφουσα | γράφουσαι |
| Neuter | γράφον | γράφοντα |

The masculine and neuter decline like third-declension nouns (ντ-stem; genitive singular γράφοντος), while the feminine declines like a first-declension noun (genitive singular γραφούσης).

Another example, with the verb λέγω, "to say," in the present indicative:

| Person | Singular | Plural |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1st | λέγω | λέγομεν |
| 2nd | λέγεις | λέγετε |
| 3rd | λέγει | λέγουσι(ν) |

And its present active participle:

| Gender | Participle form |
| --- | --- |
| Masculine | λέγων |
| Feminine | λέγουσα |
| Neuter | λέγον |

### Key Rules and Exceptions
- **Agreement**: the participle must agree with the noun it describes in gender, number, and case.
- **Contract verbs**: the final vowel of the stem contracts with the participle marker: ποιέων → **ποιῶν, ποιοῦσα, ποιοῦν** "making"; τιμάων → **τιμῶν, τιμῶσα, τιμῶν**; δηλόων → **δηλῶν, δηλοῦσα, δηλοῦν**.
- **The verb εἰμί**: its present participle is irregular: **ὤν, οὖσα, ὄν** "being."
- **Dative plural masculine/neuter**: the ντ-stem simplifies before σι: γράφουσι(ν) — spelled the same as the 3rd person plural of the verb, but distinguished by context.

### Example Sentences with Translations
1. **ὁ ἀνὴρ γράφει ἐπιστολήν.** — The man is writing a letter.
2. **ἡ γυνὴ ἡ γράφουσα βιβλίον σοφή ἐστιν.** — The woman who is writing a book is wise.
3. **οἱ ἄνθρωποι λέγουσι τὴν ἀλήθειαν.** — The people are speaking the truth.
4. **ἡ γυνὴ ἡ λέγουσα τὴν ἀλήθειαν εὐτυχής ἐστιν.** — The woman who speaks the truth is happy.
5. **γράφων τὴν ἐπιστολήν, ὁ παῖς χαίρει.** — While writing the letter, the boy rejoices.

### Notes on Usage and Context
The present participle expresses an action **simultaneous** with the main verb. It has three main uses: **attributive** with the article (ὁ γράφων ἀνήρ "the man who is writing"), **substantive** with the article alone (ὁ γράφων "the writer, the one writing"), and **circumstantial**, expressing time, cause, or manner (γράφων "while writing / because he was writing"). Instead of piling up subordinate clauses, Greek very often prefers these compact participles.

### Historical Development and Etymology
The marker ‑ντ‑ of the present active participle is a direct inheritance from Proto-Indo-European and appears in the related languages as well; Latin *amāns, amantis* "loving" preserves the same suffix. The declension of these participles — masculine and neuter after the third declension, feminine after the first — is an ancient pattern that was fully established in Classical Greek, and recognizing it is a key to reading both prose and poetry.
