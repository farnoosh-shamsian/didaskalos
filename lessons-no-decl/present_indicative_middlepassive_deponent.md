# Lesson: The Present Indicative Middle/Passive — Deponent Verbs

---

## 1. What the Present Indicative Middle/Passive Is

| Term | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| **Present** | An action or state occurring now or habitually. |
| **Indicative** | The "statement" mood — for facts and ordinary questions. |
| **Middle** | The subject is both actor and beneficiary of the action, or acts on itself: "I wash myself." |
| **Passive** | The subject **receives** the action; the agent can be added with ὑπό + genitive. |
| **Deponent** | A verb that has **only middle/passive forms** but an **active meaning** (e.g., "I fear," "I come"). |

In the present indicative, middle and passive share **the same endings**. Deponent verbs use exactly these endings, but they always **translate actively**.

---

## 2. Forms and Patterns

### 2.1 Present Indicative Middle/Passive Endings

| Person | Active ending (for comparison) | Middle/Passive ending |
|--------|---------------|------------------------|
| 1st sg. | ‑ω | **‑ομαι** |
| 2nd sg. | ‑εις | **‑ῃ / ‑ει** |
| 3rd sg. | ‑ει | **‑εται** |
| 1st pl. | ‑ομεν | **‑όμεθα** |
| 2nd pl. | ‑ετε | **‑εσθε** |
| 3rd pl. | ‑ουσι(ν) | **‑ονται** |

### 2.2 A Regular Verb for Comparison: **λύω** "to loose" → **λύομαι**

| Person | Active | Middle/Passive |
|--------|------------------------|----------------|
| 1 sg. | λύω | **λύομαι** |
| 2 sg. | λύεις | **λύῃ / λύει** |
| 3 sg. | λύει | **λύεται** |
| 1 pl. | λύομεν | **λυόμεθα** |
| 2 pl. | λύετε | **λύεσθε** |
| 3 pl. | λύουσι(ν) | **λύονται** |

### 2.3 A Contract Deponent: **φοβέομαι → φοβοῦμαι** "to fear"

| Person | Middle/Passive (active meaning) |
|--------|---------------------------------|
| 1 sg. | **φοβοῦμαι** "I fear" |
| 2 sg. | **φοβῇ / φοβεῖ** |
| 3 sg. | **φοβεῖται** |
| 1 pl. | **φοβούμεθα** |
| 2 pl. | **φοβεῖσθε** |
| 3 pl. | **φοβοῦνται** |

*The stem φοβε‑ contracts with the thematic vowel and ending (ε + ο → ου, ε + ε → ει); the endings are the ordinary middle/passive set.*

### 2.4 Common Deponents Worth Knowing

| Verb | Meaning | Note |
|------|------|------|
| **ἔρχομαι** | to come | Middle forms only; active meaning. |
| **βούλομαι** | to want, wish | |
| **γίγνομαι** | to become, happen | |
| **δέχομαι** | to receive, accept | |
| **ἀποκρίνομαι** | to answer | Takes the dative. |
| **μάχομαι** | to fight | Takes the dative. |
| **αἰσθάνομαι** | to perceive | Takes the genitive. |
| **ἡγέομαι** | to lead; to consider | ε-contract. |

## 3. Key Rules & Common Exceptions

| Rule | Explanation | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **1. No augment in the present** | The augment ἐ‑ belongs to the imperfect and aorist only. | Present: λύομαι; imperfect: ἐλυόμην |
| **2. Middle = reflexive / self-benefit** | When the subject acts on or for itself. | λούομαι "I wash myself" |
| **3. Passive = subject acted upon** | Often with ὑπό + genitive for the agent. | γράφεται ὑπὸ τοῦ γραφέως "it is written by the scribe" |
| **4. Deponents lack active forms** | Middle/passive form, active translation. | ἔρχομαι "I come" |
| **5. Contract verbs** | The stem vowel merges with the ending. | φοβέομαι → φοβοῦμαι |
| **6. Two spellings in the 2nd sg.** | ‑ῃ is the more classical; ‑ει is also common. | φοβῇ / φοβεῖ |
| **7. Some verbs are deponent only in certain tenses** | "Semi-deponents": active present, middle future. | ἀκούω "I hear" → future ἀκούσομαι |
| **8. Learn the meaning from the dictionary** | If the headword ends in ‑ομαι, the verb is deponent. | βούλομαι, γίγνομαι |

## 4. Example Sentences

| Greek | Transliteration | Translation |
|----------------------------|----------------|-------------|
| **ὁ μαθητὴς φοβεῖται τὸν διδάσκαλον.** | ho mathētḕs phobeîtai tòn didáskalon | The student fears the teacher. (deponent, active meaning) |
| **ἔρχομαι εἰς τὴν πόλιν.** | érkhomai eis tḕn pólin | I am coming into the city. (deponent) |
| **ὁ στρατιώτης ἀποκρίνεται τῷ στρατηγῷ.** | ho stratiṓtēs apokrínetai tôi stratēgôi | The soldier answers the general. (deponent) |
| **ὁ διδάσκαλος φιλεῖται ὑπὸ τῶν μαθητῶν.** | ho didáskalos phileîtai hypò tôn mathētôn | The teacher is loved by the students. (true passive) |
| **παιδευόμεθα ὑπὸ τῶν πατέρων.** | paideuómetha hypò tôn patérōn | We are being educated by our fathers. (true passive) |

## 5. Usage & Context

1. **Middle vs. passive** — in narrative prose the middle often carries a reflexive or "for one's own benefit" nuance, while the passive marks an action done to the subject; the forms are identical, so context is the final judge.
2. **Deponents are very common in Classical Greek**, especially among verbs of feeling, perception, and motion (ἔρχομαι, φοβοῦμαι, αἰσθάνομαι, ἀποκρίνομαι). Trust the meaning, not the shape: the form is middle, the translation active.
3. **Literary style** — poets favor the middle for a more personal tone; historians use the passive for objective reporting.
4. **Always check the dictionary**: only the headword tells you whether a form is deponent (βούλομαι) or the middle/passive of an active verb (λύομαι from λύω).

---

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **The middle voice** | Continues the Proto-Indo-European mediopassive, which combined reflexive, reciprocal, and passive functions; in Greek the sense "action in the subject's interest" remained strong. |
| **The term "deponent"** | From Latin *dēpōnere* "to lay aside": these verbs have, as it were, "laid aside" their active forms. |
| **Origin of deponents** | Originally middle-voice verbs whose active meaning prevailed while their active paradigm fell out of use or never existed. |
| **The endings** | ‑ομαι combines the old middle suffix with the thematic vowel ‑ο‑; the middle/passive infinitive shows the same suffix in ‑εσθαι. |
