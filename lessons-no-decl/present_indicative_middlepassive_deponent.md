# Lesson: The Present‑Indicative Middle/Passive (including Deponent Verbs)

---

## 1. What the Present‑Indicative Middle/Passive Is  

| Term | Meaning in Greek | English Approximation |
|------|------------------|-----------------------|
| **Present** | Action or state that is occurring **now** or habitually. | “I am …”, “I do …” |
| **Indicative** | The “statement” mood – used for ordinary facts, questions, or commands that are not wishes or possibilities. | “He walks.” |
| **Middle** | The subject is both **actor and beneficiary** of the action, or the action is performed **for oneself**. | “I wash **myself**.” |
| **Passive** | The subject **receives** the action; the agent may be omitted or expressed with a *by‑phrase*. | “The house is built (by the workers).” |
| **Deponent** | Verbs that have **only middle/passive forms** but **carry an active meaning** (e.g., “I fear”). They are historically middle‑voice verbs that lost their active paradigm. |

In the present indicative we therefore have **two voice series**:

1. **Middle** – endings that show the subject acting for its own benefit or in a reflexive way.  
2. **Passive** – endings that show the subject being acted upon.

Both series share the same set of endings (the “middle/passive” endings). Deponent verbs use exactly these endings, but they translate **actively**.

## 2. Forms and Patterns  

### 2.1. Regular Present‑Indicative Middle/Passive Endings  

| Person | Active Ending | Middle/Passive Ending* |
|--------|---------------|------------------------|
| 1st sg. | ‑ω, ‑εω (contract) | **‑ομαι** |
| 2nd sg. | ‑εις, ‑εῖς | **‑ῃ/‑ει** (often ‑ε/‑η) |
| 3rd sg. | ‑ει, ‑ει | **‑εται** |
| 1st pl. | ‑ομεν | **‑όμεθα** |
| 2nd pl. | ‑ετε | **‑εσθε** |
| 3rd pl. | ‑ουσι(ν) | **‑ονται** |

\*The middle and passive share the same endings; the difference is semantic (reflexive vs. true passive).  

> **Note:** In Attic Greek the 2nd‑person singular middle ending is usually **‑ῃ** (spelled ‑ει after a vowel) and the 3rd‑person singular is **‑εται** (‑εται).  

### 2.2. Conjugation of a Regular Verb: **παίω** “to strike, beat” (active) → **παίομαι** “to strike oneself, be struck”  

| Person | Active (for comparison) | Middle/Passive |
|--------|------------------------|----------------|
| 1 sg. | παίω | **παίομαι** |
| 2 sg. | παίεις | **παίῃ** / **παίει** |
| 3 sg. | παίει | **παίεται** |
| 1 pl. | παίομεν | **παίομεθα** |
| 2 pl. | παίετε | **παίεσθε** |
| 3 pl. | παίουσι(ν) | **παίονται** |

### 2.3. Conjugation of a **Deponent** Verb: **φοβέομαι** → **φοβοῦμαι** “to fear”  

| Person | Middle/Passive (active meaning) |
|--------|---------------------------------|
| 1 sg. | **φοβοῦμαι** |
| 2 sg. | **φοβῇ** / **φοβεί** |
| 3 sg. | **φοβείται** |
| 1 pl. | **φοβούμεθα** |
| 2 pl. | **φοβεῖσθε** |
| 3 pl. | **φοβούνται** |

*The stem changes (ε → ου) because **φοβέω** contracts in the present. The deponent uses the same middle/passive endings.*

### 2.4. Contract Verbs (e‑contract) – Example **φιλέω** “to love” → **φιλοῦμαι** “to love (for oneself), be loved”  

| Person | Middle/Passive |
|--------|----------------|
| 1 sg. | **φιλοῦμαι** |
| 2 sg. | **φιλεῖ** / **φιλεῖς** (rare) |
| 3 sg. | **φιλεῖται** |
| 1 pl. | **φιλοῦμεθα** |
| 2 pl. | **φιλεῖσθε** |
| 3 pl. | **φιλοῦνται** |

*The long vowel **ου** appears before the middle/passive endings.*

## 3. Key Rules & Common Exceptions  

| Rule | Explanation | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **No augment** in the present | The augment (ἐ‑) is only used in the **imperfect** and **aorist**. | Present: **λύω** “I loosen”; Imperfect: **ἔλυον** |
| **Middle = Reflexive** | When the meaning is “to do something to oneself” or “for oneself”. | **ἀποκρίνεσθαι** → **ἀποκρίνεσθαι** “to answer (for oneself)”. |
| **Passive = True passive** | The subject receives the action; often the agent is expressed with a *by‑phrase* (ὑπὸ). | **γράφεται** “it is written (by the scribe)”. |
| **Deponent verbs lack active forms** | Use only middle/passive endings, but translate actively. | **ἔρχομαι** “I come”. |
| **Contract verbs** | Vowel‑contracting stems (e‑, o‑, a‑) change before the endings. | **φιλέω → φιλοῦμαι**, **διδάσκω → διδάσκομαι** (no contraction). |
| **Spurious diphthongs** (ει, ου) | In some verbs they are **true diphthongs** (long) and affect the length of the preceding vowel. | **πείθω → πείθομαι** (spurious diphthong). |
| **Irregular stems** | Some verbs have a different present stem from the infinitive (e.g., **ἔρχομαι** from *ἔρχο-*, **γίγνομαι** from *γίγ*). | **γίγνομαι** “I become”. |
| **Middle‑passive endings in the 2nd sg.** | The form **‑ῃ** is the “long” version; after a vowel it appears as **‑ει**. | **παίῃ** vs. **παίει**. |
| **Passive of deponent verbs** | Rare, but some deponents have a true passive meaning (e.g., **ἀποκρίνομαι** can be passive in later Greek). | Not common in Classical Attic. |

## 4. Example Sentences  

| Greek (Present‑Indicative) | Transliteration | Translation |
|----------------------------|----------------|-------------|
| **1.** Ἀνὴρ **παίεται** τὸν ἥλιον. | Anēr **paietai** ton hēlion. | The man **is struck** by the sun. (Passive) |
| **2.** Ἡ γυνὴ **παίεται** τὸν ἱματισμόν της. | Hē gynē **paietai** ton himatismon tēs. | The woman **is dressed** (by someone). |
| **3.** Ὁ μαθητὴς **φοβοῦται** τὸν διδάσκαλον. | Ho mathētēs **phoboutai** ton didaskalon. | The student **fears** the teacher. (Deponent, active meaning) |
| **4.** Ἐγὼ **φιλοῦμαι** τὸν πατέρα μου. | Egō **philoumai** ton patera mou. | I **love** my father (reflexive sense “love for”). |
| **5.** Ἀγγέλλομεν **ὑμῖν** ὅτι **παιδεύομεθα** ἀπὸ τῶν πατέρων. | Angelomen hymin hoti **paideuometha** apo tōn paterōn. | We tell you that **we are being educated** by our fathers. |

## 5. Usage & Context  

1. **Middle vs. Passive** – In narrative prose the middle often carries a **reflexive** or **benefactive** nuance, while the passive is used for actions done *to* the subject.  
2. **Deponent verbs** are common in Classical Greek, especially for verbs of feeling, perception, and movement (e.g., **ἔρχομαι**, **ὁρμᾶσθαι**, **ἀποκρίνεσθαι**). They are treated as **active** in translation, so remember the meaning, not the form.  
3. **Literary style** – Poets may prefer the middle for a more “personal” tone, while historians often use the passive for objective reporting.  
4. **Verb‑type** – Most verbs follow the regular pattern, but always check a dictionary for irregular stems (e.g., **ἔρχομαι**, **γίγνομαι**, **ἀποκρίνομαι**).

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology  

### 6.1. The Middle Voice  
- Originates from the Proto‑Indo‑European (PIE) **mediopassive** voice, which combined reflexive, reciprocal, and passive functions.  
- In Greek the middle retained a strong sense of “the subject acting for its own interest” and later developed a **passive** meaning when the agent was omitted.

### 6.2. Deponent Verbs  
- The term **deponent** comes from Latin *deponere* “to lay aside”, because these verbs have **“laid aside”** their active forms.  
- Historically, many deponents were originally **middle** verbs whose active forms fell out of use, leaving only the middle/passive paradigm.  
- Over time, some deponents acquired a **true passive** sense (e.g., **ἀποκρίνομαι** “to be answered”), but the majority kept an active meaning.

### 6.3. Morphological Change  
- The **‑ομαι** ending derives from the PIE *‑h₂e‑* (middle suffix) plus the thematic vowel *‑o‑*.  
- The **‑εσθαι** infinitive (e.g., *φιλεῖσθαι*) shows the same middle suffix attached to the infinitive marker *‑σθαι*.

### Bottom Line  

- The **present‑indicative middle/passive** uses a single set of endings for both voices.  
- **Middle** = reflexive/benefactive; **Passive** = acted upon.  
- **Deponent verbs** employ these endings but translate **actively**.  
- Master the endings, watch for stem changes (contract verbs, irregular stems), and always check the lexical meaning to know whether a verb is deponent.  

Now you have a complete picture of how to form and understand the present indicative middle/passive in Ancient Greek!
