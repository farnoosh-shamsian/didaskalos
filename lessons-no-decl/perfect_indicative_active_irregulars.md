# Lesson: The Perfect Indicative Active (Irregular Verbs)  

*Target level: Beginner‑Intermediate*  
*Prerequisites: Greek alphabet, basic knowledge of present‑tense conjugation.*

---

## 1. What the Perfect Indicative Active Means  

| Term | Meaning in English |
|------|--------------------|
| **Perfect** | An action that **has been completed** and whose **result still holds** at the time of speaking. |
| **Indicative** | The “real” mood – statements of fact, not wishes or commands. |
| **Active** | The subject **does** the action (as opposed to passive, where the subject receives it). |

The perfect therefore answers the question *“What has the subject done, and what is the state now?”*  

> **ἔγραψα τὸν ἰ̓̔̔ παιδεῦ** – “I have written the letter (and it is now written).”

---

## 2. How the Perfect is Built (Regular Pattern)

1. **Take the perfect stem** – usually the **present stem + reduplication** (συ‑, ε‑, or a vowel‑lengthening).  
2. **Add the perfect active endings** (the same as the aorist active endings).  

### 2.1 Perfect Active Endings (Indicative)

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|--------|----------|--------|
| 1st    | ‑α (‑α)   | ‑αμεν |
| 2nd    | ‑ας      | ‑ατε |
| 3rd    | ‑ε(ν)    | ‑ασι(ν) |

*The “‑α” ending is the same for 1st sg, 2nd sg, 3rd sg (‑ε/‑εν) and the plural forms.*

### 2.2 Regular Paradigm: **λῡ́ω** “to loose / release”

|          | Singular | Plural |
|----------|----------|--------|
| **1st**  | **λέλῡα** | **λελύαμεν** |
| **2nd**  | **λέλῡας**| **λελύατε** |
| **3rd**  | **λέλῡε(ν)**| **λελύασι(ν)** |

*Stem = **λελυ‑** (reduplicated *λῡ‑* → *λελυ‑*).*

## 3. Irregular Perfects  

Some of the most common verbs do **not** follow the simple reduplication rule. They either use a **different stem**, **suppletive forms**, or **vowel‑changing patterns**. Below are the principal irregular perfects you will meet early on.

### 3.1 ἔρχομαι – “to come, to go”

| Person | Form | Literal meaning |
|--------|------|-----------------|
| 1st sg | **ἐλήλυθα** | “I have come / I am come” |
| 2nd sg | **ἐλήλυθας** | “you have come” |
| 3rd sg | **ἐλήλυθε(ν)** | “he/she/it has come” |
| 1st pl | **ἐλήλυθαμεν** | “we have come” |
| 2nd pl | **ἐλήλυθατε** | “you (pl.) have come” |
| 3rd pl | **ἐλήλυθασι(ν)** | “they have come” |

*Stem = **ἐληλυθ‑** (suppletive; derived from the aorist **ἦλθον**). No regular reduplication.*

---

### 3.2 εἰμί – “to be”

| Person | Form | Literal meaning |
|--------|------|-----------------|
| 1st sg | **ἐστί(ν)** | “I am / I have been” |
| 2nd sg | **ἐστί(ν)** (same) | “you are / have been” |
| 3rd sg | **ἐστί(ν)** | “he/she/it is / has been” |
| 1st pl | **ἐσμέν** | “we are / have been” |
| 2nd pl | **ἐστέ** | “you (pl.) are / have been” |
| 3rd pl | **εἰσίν** | “they are / have been” |

*The perfect of *εἰμί* is essentially the same as the present; it is a **suppletive** form that does not use the regular endings.*

---

### 3.3 ἔχω – “to have, to hold”

| Person | Form | Literal meaning |
|--------|------|-----------------|
| 1st sg | **ἔσχηκα** | “I have (possessed) / I am having” |
| 2nd sg | **ἔσχης** | “you have” |
| 3rd sg | **ἔσχεν** | “he/she/it has” |
| 1st pl | **ἐσχέμεν** | “we have” |
| 2nd pl | **ἐσχέτε** | “you (pl.) have” |
| 3rd pl | **ἔσχον** | “they have” |

*Stem = **ἐσχ‑** (suppletive, from the aorist **ἔσχον**). The endings are the regular perfect ones.*

---

### 3.4 φημί – “to say”

| Person | Form | Literal meaning |
|--------|------|-----------------|
| 1st sg | **πέφην** | “I have said” |
| 2nd sg | **πέφησας** | “you have said” |
| 3rd sg | **πέφησεν** | “he/she/it has said” |
| 1st pl | **πεφῆμεν** | “we have said” |
| 2nd pl | **πεφῆτε** | “you (pl.) have said” |
| 3rd pl | **πέφησαν** | “they have said” |

*Stem = **π-εφ‑** (reduplication with *π‑* plus a vowel change).*

---

### 3.5 γιγνώσκω – “to know, to recognize”

| Person | Form | Literal meaning |
|--------|------|-----------------|
| 1st sg | **ἔγνων** | “I have known / I know” |
| 2nd sg | **ἔγνως** | “you have known” |
| 3rd sg | **ἔγνως** (same) | “he/she/it has known” |
| 1st pl | **ἐγνώκαμεν** | “we have known” |
| 2nd pl | **ἐγνώκατε** | “you (pl.) have known” |
| 3rd pl | **ἔγνωσαν** | “they have known” |

*Stem = **γν‑** with a **suppletive aorist‑derived perfect** (no reduplication).*

---

## 4. Key Rules & Exceptions  

| Rule | Explanation | Exception(s) |
|------|-------------|--------------|
| **4.1 Reduplication** – most verbs double the initial consonant or lengthen the vowel (συ‑, ε‑, or vowel‑lengthening). | Regular verbs like **λῡ́ω → λέλυ‑**. | Irregular verbs use **suppletive stems** (ἔρχομαι → ἐλήλυθ‑, εἰμί → ἐσ‑). |
| **4.2 Perfect Stem + Endings** – after the stem, attach **‑α, ‑ας, ‑ε(ν), ‑αμεν, ‑ατε, ‑ασι(ν)**. | Works for regular and many irregular stems. | Some irregulars have **different vowel quality** in the endings (e.g., φημί → **‑ησ‑**). |
| **4.3 Contracted Forms** – when the stem ends in a vowel, the ending may contract (e.g., **ἔσχον → ἔσχον** retains -ον). | Mostly a phonological issue, not a morphological one. | None critical for the perfect active. |
| **4.4 Suppletion** – the perfect may come from a completely different lexical root. | ἔρχομαι → ἐλήλυθα, εἰμί → ἐστί. | These are memorised as lexical items. |
| **4.5 Accent** – the accent normally falls on the **penult** if it is long; otherwise on the **ultima**. | λῡ́ω → **λέλῡα** (penult long). | Irregulars may keep the original accent (e.g., **ἔσχεν**). |

### Tips for Memorising Irregular Perfects  

1. **Link the perfect to the aorist** – many irregular perfect stems are derived from the aorist (e.g., ἐλήλυθ‑ from ἦλθον).  
2. **Group by pattern** – φημί, γιγνώσκω, and εἰμί each have a distinct “type” of suppletion.  
3. **Practice the endings** – once you know the stem, the endings are always the same.

## 5. Example Sentences  

| Greek (Perfect, Indicative, Active) | Translation |
|--------------------------------------|-------------|
| **ἔληλυθα εἰς τὴν ἀγορὰν.** | “I have come to the market.” |
| **ἔσχηκα τὸν λόγον.** | “I have taken the word (I have spoken).” |
| **πέφην ὅτι ἔρχεται.** | “I have said that he is coming.” |
| **ἔγνων τὸν ἥρωα.** | “I have known the hero (I recognize him).” |
| **ἐσμέν ἐν τῇ οἰκίᾳ.** | “We are (have been) in the house.” |

Notice the **present‑time relevance**: the speaker can still act on the result (the hero is still known, the word is still taken, etc.).

## 6. Usage & Context  

| Situation | Typical Perfect Use | Why the Perfect? |
|-----------|--------------------|------------------|
| **Resultant state** | *ἔληλυθα* “I have come (and I am now here).” | Emphasises the *present* state that follows the completed action. |
| **Narrative contrast** | *ἔγραψα τὸν λόγον, ἀλλ’ οὐκ ἀνέγνω.* “I wrote the speech, but it has not been read.” | Shows a completed action whose effect is still relevant. |
| **With adverbs** | *ἤδη* (already), *πάλαι* (long ago), *οὔπω* (not yet) often accompany perfects. | Marks temporal nuance. |
