# Lesson: The Aorist Indicative Active of **‑μι** Verbs  
*(e.g., φημι, τιθημι, ἵστημι, δίδωμι, etc.)*  

---

## 1. What the Aorist Indicative Active Is  

| Term | Meaning in Greek grammar | English‑language analogue |
|------|--------------------------|---------------------------|
| **Aorist** | A past‑time (perfective) aspect that presents an action as a simple whole, without reference to its duration or repetition. | “He **went**,” “She **said**,” “I **gave**.” |
| **Indicative** | The mood used for statements of fact or real events. | The ordinary “statement” mood. |
| **Active** | The subject of the verb performs the action (as opposed to middle or passive). | “I **saw** the dog.” |
| **‑μι** | A class of irregular verbs whose present‑stem ends in **‑μι** (instead of the regular **‑ω**). | φη‑μι, τι‑θη‑μι, ἵ‑στη‑μι, διδ‑ω‑μι, … |

The **aorist indicative active** of a ‑μι verb therefore tells us **that the subject performed a single, completed action in the past**. Because ‑μι verbs are irregular, their aorist forms do **not** follow the regular ‑ω pattern; they have their own set of stems and endings.

---

## 2. Forms & Patterns – Paradigms  

Below are three representative verbs that illustrate the three main aorist‑stem patterns found in ‑μι verbs.

### 2.1. Verb **φημι** “to say, to speak”  

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|--------|----------|--------|
| **1st** | ἔφησα | ἐφάμεν |
| **2nd** | ἔφης | ἐφάτε |
| **3rd** | ἔφη | ἔφασαν |

*Stem*: **φῆ‑** → aorist stem **φῆσ‑** (with augment **ε‑** and the regular active aorist ending **‑α**).  
*Note*: The 3rd‑person plural uses the regular **‑σαν** ending (instead of **‑σαν** → **‑σαν**).

---

### 2.2. Verb **τιθημι** “to place, to put”  

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|--------|----------|--------|
| **1st** | ἔθηκα | ἐθέμεν |
| **2nd** | ἔθηκας | ἐθέτε |
| **3rd** | ἔθηκεν | ἔθεσαν |

*Stem*: **τιθ‑** → aorist stem **θ‑** (the **t** drops, leaving **θ**). The aorist is formed with the **‑κα** suffix (the “α‑aorist”) plus the augment **ε‑**.

---

### 2.3. Verb **ἵστημι** “to set up, to stand”  

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|--------|----------|--------|
| **1st** | ἔστησα | ἐστήσαμεν |
| **2nd** | ἔστησας | ἐστήσατε |
| **3rd** | ἔστησεν | ἔστησαν |

*Stem*: **στη‑** → aorist stem **στησ‑** (the **‑σ‑** is added). The aorist uses the regular **‑α** ending (the “α‑aorist”) with augment **ε‑**.

---

### 2.4. Summary of the Three Patterns  

| Pattern | Typical aorist suffix | Example verb | Example aorist |
|---------|-----------------------|--------------|----------------|
| **α‑aorist** (with **‑α**) | **‑α** (or **‑κα** after a consonant) | φημι → ἔφησα | ἔφησα |
| **α‑aorist** (with **‑κα**) | **‑κα** (after a vowel‑initial stem) | τιθημι → ἔθηκα | ἔθηκα |
| **σ‑aorist** (with **‑σ‑**) | **‑σ‑** + **‑α** | ἵστημι → ἔστησα | ἔστησα |

All three share the same **augment** (prefixed **ε‑**) and the same set of active indicative endings:

| Person | Ending (after augment + stem) |
|--------|------------------------------|
| 1 sg. | **‑α** (or **‑κα**) |
| 2 sg. | **‑ας** / **‑κας** |
| 3 sg. | **‑ε(ν)** |
| 1 pl. | **‑αμεν** / **‑καμεν** |
| 2 pl. | **‑ατε** / **‑κατε** |
| 3 pl. | **‑αν** |

---

## 3. Key Rules & Exceptions  

| Rule | Explanation | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Augment** | All aorist indicative forms receive a prefixed **ε‑** (the “augment”) unless the verb begins with a vowel, in which case the augment lengthens the initial vowel (e.g., **ἔ** + **α** → **ἠ**). | φημι → **ἔ** + φῆσ‑ → **ἔφησα** |
| **Stem change** | The aorist stem of a ‑μι verb is **not** the present stem; it must be memorised. Three main stems: **‑σ‑**, **‑κ‑**, or a simple vowel‑stem. | ἵστημι → stem **στησ‑**; τιθημι → stem **θ‑** |
| **‑α vs. ‑κα** | If the aorist stem ends in a vowel, the aorist suffix is **‑α**; if it ends in a consonant, the suffix is **‑κα** (the “α‑aorist”). | τιθημι (stem **θ‑**) → **θ** + **‑κα** = **ἔθηκα** |
| **3rd‑person plural** | The regular ending is **‑σαν** (or **‑σαν** after a vowel). Some verbs show a contracted form **‑σαν** → **‑σαν** (no change). | ἔφησαν, ἔθεσαν |
| **Irregularities** | A few ‑μι verbs have **mixed** aorist forms (e.g., **διδωμι** → **ἔδωκα** (α‑aorist) but also **ἔδωκον** in poetry). | διδωμι → ἔδωκα (regular) but poetic **ἔδωκον** |
| **Passive & Middle** | The aorist **active** forms described here are distinct from the aorist **middle/passive** forms, which use different endings (‑θην, ‑θη, ‑θην, ‑θημεν, ‑θητε, ‑θησαν). | Not covered in this lesson. |

## 4. Example Sentences  

| Greek (aorist active) | Gloss | Translation |
|-----------------------|-------|-------------|
| **ἔφησα** τὸν λόγον· **ἔστησα** τὸν βωμὸν. | ephēsa / estēsa | “I said the word; I set up the altar.” |
| **ἔθηκα** τὸν δακτύλιον ἐπὶ τὴν χεῖρα. | ethēka | “I placed the ring on the hand.” |
| **ἔδωκαν** τὰ δῶρα τοῖς θεοῖς. | edōkan | “They gave the gifts to the gods.” |
| **ἔφησαν** ὅτι ἡ πόλις ἀσφαλής ἐστίν. | ephēsan | “They said that the city is safe.” |

*Note*: In each sentence the aorist marks a **single, completed action** in the past.

---

## 5. Usage & Context  

1. **Narrative past** – The aorist is the default past tense in storytelling, especially for actions that move the plot forward.  
2. **Result‑oriented statements** – When the speaker wants to stress the *completion* of an act (e.g., “I **said** it” = ἔφησα).  
3. **Contrast with imperfect** – The imperfect (παρά imperfect) would describe an ongoing or repeated past action (e.g., ἔλεγε “he was saying”).  
4. **Literary style** – Poets sometimes use the aorist for dramatic effect, even when the action is not strictly punctual.  

Because ‑μι verbs are frequent in classical prose and poetry (e.g., φημι “to say”), mastering their aorist forms is essential for reading and translating ancient Greek texts.

---

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology  

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Proto‑Indo‑European origin** | The ‑μι verbs descend from the PIE *‑mi* present‑stem suffix (first‑person singular). The aorist stems often reflect older *‑s‑* or *‑k‑* extensions that developed in Greek. |
| **α‑aorist vs. σ‑aorist** | The **α‑aorist** (with ‑α/‑κα) is the older, more productive formation in Greek, while the **σ‑aorist** (with ‑σ‑) appears in verbs that historically added a **‑s‑** suffix to mark a simple past. |
| **Phonological change** | The augment **ε‑** originates in the early Greek perfect‑aspect system, where a past tense was marked by lengthening or adding **ε‑** to the verb stem. |
| **Lexicalization** | Many of the most common verbs in Greek are ‑μι verbs (φημι, ἵστημι, δίδωμι, τιθημι). Their irregular aorist forms were preserved because they were used so often that speakers resisted regularization. |
| **Later Greek** | In Koine and Byzantine Greek, the aorist of many ‑μι verbs became regularized to **‑σα** (e.g., **ἔφησα** → **εἶπον** in later colloquial speech), but the classical forms remain the standard in literary study. |
