# Lesson: The Present Tense, Present Infinitive, and Middle/Passive (Deponent) Forms  

*Target level: Beginner‑Intermediate*  
*Prerequisites: Greek alphabet, basic noun declension, concept of “stem”*  

---

## 1. What the Present Tense and Present Infinitive Express  

| Aspect | Meaning in English | Typical English equivalents |
|--------|-------------------|-----------------------------|
| **Present indicative (active)** | Ongoing or habitual action, general truth, simple present | “I love”, “the sun rises” |
| **Present indicative (middle)** | The subject acts on or for itself, or the action is reciprocal or reflexive | “I wash **myself**”, “they meet each other” |
| **Present indicative (passive)** | The subject receives the action | “the city is built” |
| **Present infinitive** | The “to‑verb” form used after certain verbs, adjectives, or nouns; also used in purpose clauses, indirect statements, etc. | “to love”, “to be built” |
| **Deponent verbs** | Verbs that have only middle/passive forms but a **active** meaning (e.g., “I pray”, “he fears”). They behave like middle/passive in morphology but like active in translation. | “I pray”, “he fears” |

> **Why study them together?**  
All of these forms share the same set of endings (active, middle, passive) and the same **present stem**. Mastering the paradigm once lets you recognise and produce all three functions.

---

## 2. The Present Stem  

A verb’s **present stem** is obtained by removing the **‑ω** of the first‑person singular present active indicative.  

| Example | Dictionary entry | Present stem |
|---------|------------------|--------------|
| λύω   | λύω “to loose, to release” | λυ‑ |
| παίζω | παίζω “to play” | παιζ‑ |
| φῆμι  | φῆμι “to say” (irregular) | φη‑ |
| ἀποκρίνομαι | ἀποκρίνομαι “to answer” (middle) | ἀποκρίνο‑ |

*Irregular verbs* (e.g., **φῆμι**, **εἰμί**) have their own special paradigms; they are treated separately.

---

## 3. Endings for the Present Tense  

The endings are attached to the present stem. The pattern is the same for **active**, **middle**, and **passive** (the latter shares the middle endings in the indicative).  

### 3.1 Active Endings (Indicative)

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|--------|----------|--------|
| 1st | ‑ω | ‑ομεν |
| 2nd | ‑εις | ‑ετε |
| 3rd | ‑ει(ν) | ‑ουσι(ν) |

*Note:* In Attic Greek the 3rd‑person singular often appears as **‑ει** (no final **ν**) and the 3rd‑person plural as **‑ουσι(ν)** (the **ν** is optional in poetry).

### 3.2 Middle Endings (Indicative)

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|--------|----------|--------|
| 1st | ‑ομαι | ‑ομεθα |
| 2nd | ‑ῃ/‑ῃς (often ‑ῃ) | ‑εσθε |
| 3rd | ‑εται | ‑ονται |

*The middle often shows a **‑ο‑** before the ending (e.g., **λυ‑ομαι** → **λῦομαι** after contraction).*

### 3.3 Passive Endings (Indicative)

The passive uses the **same endings as the middle** in the indicative:

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|--------|----------|--------|
| 1st | ‑ομαι | ‑ομεθα |
| 2nd | ‑ῃ/‑ῃς | ‑εσθε |
| 3rd | ‑εται | ‑ονται |

In the **aorist** and **future** the passive has distinct endings, but in the present they are identical to the middle.

### 3.4 Present Infinitive Endings  

| Voice | Ending |
|-------|--------|
| Active | ‑ειν |
| Middle | ‑εσθαι |
| Passive | ‑εσθαι (identical to middle) |

> **Result:** The infinitive **‑εσθαι** can be either middle or passive; context decides which voice is meant.

---

## 4. Full Paradigm Example  

Below is the complete present indicative and infinitive for the regular verb **παίζω** “to play”.  

| **Active** | Singular | Plural |
|------------|----------|--------|
| 1st | **παίζω** | **παίζομεν** |
| 2nd | **παίζεις** | **παίζετε** |
| 3rd | **παίζει** | **παίζουσι(ν)** |

| **Middle / Passive** | Singular | Plural |
|----------------------|----------|--------|
| 1st | **παίζομαι** | **παίζομεθα** |
| 2nd | **παίζῃ** / **παίζῃς** | **παίζεσθε** |
| 3rd | **παίζεται** | **παίζονται** |

| **Infinitive** | Form |
|----------------|------|
| Active | **παίζειν** |
| Middle / Passive | **παίζεσθαι** |

## 5. Deponent Verbs  

### 5.1 What Is a Deponent?  

- **Morphology:** Only middle/passive forms (active endings never appear).  
- **Semantics:** Translated with an **active** meaning.  

| Example | Dictionary entry | Meaning (English) |
|---------|------------------|-------------------|
| φοβοῦμαι | φοβέω “to fear” (deponent) | “I fear” |
| ἀποκρίνομαι | ἀποκρίνεσθαι “to answer” (deponent) | “I answer” |
| δέχομαι | δέχομαι “to receive, accept” (deponent) | “I receive” |

### 5.2 Deponent Paradigm (using **φοβοῦμαι**)  

| **Middle / Passive** (active meaning) | Singular | Plural |
|----------------------------------------|----------|--------|
| 1st | **φοβοῦμαι** | **φοβούμεθα** |
| 2nd | **φοβῇ** / **φοβῇς** | **φοβεῖσθε** |
| 3rd | **φοβῆται** | **φοβοῦνται** |

| **Infinitive** | Form |
|----------------|------|
| Middle / Passive | **φοβῆσθαι** |

*Note:* The **present infinitive** of a deponent is **‑εσθαι** (e.g., **φοβῆσθαι**). It is still translated actively: “to fear”.

### 5.3 Common Deponent Verbs (list for reference)

| Verb (dictionary) | Meaning |
|-------------------|---------|
| ἀποκρίνομαι | to answer |
| ἀγωνίζομαι | to struggle, contend |
| ἁμαρτάνω (rarely deponent) | to err |
| δέχομαι | to receive, accept |
| ἐλπίζω (active) – **not** deponent |
| ἔρχομαι | to come (middle) |
| ἁρπάζω (active) – **not** deponent |
| ὁρμᾶσθαι | to set out (deponent) |
| πείθω (active) – **not** deponent |

## 6. Key Rules & Exceptions  

| Rule | Explanation | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **1. Present stem + endings** | The present stem is the base for all present forms. | λυ‑ + ‑ω → **λύω** |
| **2. Contraction** | When a vowel‑ending stem meets a vowel in the ending, they contract (e.g., **λυ‑ομαι → λῦομαι**). | λυ‑ + ‑ομαι → **λῦομαι** |
| **3. Vowel length in endings** | The 2nd‑person singular middle ending is **‑ῃ** (long iota) after a long vowel, otherwise **‑ει**. | **παίζῃ** (after short stem) vs. **παίζῃ** (same) – the difference is phonological, not orthographic. |
| **4. The 3rd‑person singular middle/passive often drops **ν** in poetry** | **‑εται** → **‑ετα** in lyrical verse. | **παίζετα** (poetic) |
| **5. Deponent verbs never have active endings** | If you see a middle/passive form, treat it as active in translation. | **φοβοῦμαι** = “I fear”, not “I am feared”. |
| **6. The infinitive **‑εσθαι** can be either middle or passive** | Context decides: *ἔρχομαι* (middle) → **ἔρχεσθαι** “to come”; *γράφεται* (passive) → **γραφέσθαι** “to be written”. |
| **7. Irregular verbs** | Some verbs (e.g., **φῆμι**, **εἰμί**) have unique present stems and endings. Treat them as lexical items. | **φῆμι** → **φά̄ναι** (infinitive) |

## 7. Example Sentences  

| Greek (with transliteration) | Translation | Notes |
|------------------------------|-------------|-------|
| **1.** λύω τὸν δεσμὸν. (lu‑ō ton desmon) | “I loose the bond.” | Active present indicative, 1st sg. |
| **2.** λύομαι ἀπὸ τῆς ἀγνοίας. (lu‑omai apo tēs agnoias) | “I am freed from ignorance.” | Middle form used with passive meaning (reflexive). |
| **3.** λύεσθαι δεῖ τὸν ἄνθρωπον. (lu‑esthai dei ton anthrōpon) | “The man must be loosened.” | Present infinitive passive (‑εσθαι). |
| **4.** φοβοῦμαι τῶν θηρίων. (phoboumai tōn thērion) | “I fear the beasts.” | Deponent verb, middle form, active meaning. |
| **5.** ἔρχομαι εἰς τὴν πόλιν. (erchomai eis tēn polin) | “I am coming to the city.” | Middle present indicative, active sense of motion. |
| **6.** παίζεσθαι ἀγαπῶ. (paizesthai agapō) | “I love to play.” | Infinitive middle (‑εσθαι) after a verb of liking. |

## 8. Usage & Context  

1. **Narrative prose** – The present indicative is used for vivid, immediate narration (historical present).  
2. **Poetry** – Contractions and loss of **ν** in 3rd‑person plural are common; the middle/passive may be used for reflexive actions.  
3. **Philosophical/Legal language** – Deponent verbs are frequent (e.g., **ὁρμᾶσθαι** “to be inclined”, **ἀποκρίνεσθαι** “to answer”).  
4. **Infinitive** – Appears after verbs of desire, perception, necessity, and after certain prepositions (e.g., **πρὸ** + infinitive).  
5. **Middle vs. Passive** – Distinguish by meaning:  
   - **Middle** = subject acts for itself, reciprocally, or with a sense of “self‑interest”.  
   - **Passive** = subject receives the action.  
   In many cases the same form serves both; the verb’s lexical entry tells you which voice is intended.

## 9. Historical Development & Etymology  

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Proto‑Indo‑European (PIE) origin** | The *present* system derives from the PIE *present* (or *present‑stem*) formed with a *‑‑* suffix. The middle voice originates from a reflexive *‑‑* suffix that later grammaticalised. |
| **Greek evolution** | Early Greek (Homeric) showed a richer set of *present* endings, including **‑μι** for the 1st‑person singular (e.g., **φιλεῖμι**). By Classical Attic, the **‑ω** ending became standard for the active. |
