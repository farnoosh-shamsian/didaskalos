# Lesson: The Aorist Optative Middle of Deponent Verbs

*Target level: Intermediate*
*Prerequisites: Deponent verbs, aorist indicative middle, present optative*

---

## 1. What This Form Is

**Deponent verbs** (citation form in **-μαι**) have no active forms: their middle forms carry active meaning. The **aorist optative middle** of a deponent therefore expresses a wish or possibility about a single, complete action: γένοιτο "may it happen!"

This is a high-value form: the single word **γένοιτο** (from γίγνομαι "become, happen") is among the most famous optatives in Greek — it survives in the phrase μὴ γένοιτο "may it not be!" and closes countless prayers.

## 2. Morphology

Two formations, depending on the verb's aorist type:

### 2.1 Second (thematic) aorist deponents — endings -οίμην, -οιο, -οιτο

**γίγνομαι "become, happen"** (aorist stem γεν-):

| Person | Form | Gloss |
|---|---|---|
| 1 sg | **γενοίμην** | may I become |
| 2 sg | **γένοιο** | may you become |
| 3 sg | **γένοιτο** | may it happen |
| 1 pl | **γενοίμεθα** | may we become |
| 2 pl | **γένοισθε** | may you (pl.) become |
| 3 pl | **γένοιντο** | may they become |

Same pattern: πυνθάνομαι "learn by inquiry" → **πυθοίμην, πύθοιο, πύθοιτο**; ἀφικνέομαι "arrive" → **ἀφικοίμην, ἀφίκοιτο**; (poetic) ἱκνέομαι "come, reach" → **ἱκοίμην, ἵκοιτο**.

### 2.2 First (sigmatic) aorist deponents — endings -αίμην, -αιο, -αιτο

**ἡγέομαι "lead; believe"** (aorist ἡγησάμην):

| Person | Form |
|---|---|
| 1 sg | **ἡγησαίμην** |
| 2 sg | **ἡγήσαιο** |
| 3 sg | **ἡγήσαιτο** |
| 1 pl | **ἡγησαίμεθα** |
| 2 pl | **ἡγήσαισθε** |
| 3 pl | **ἡγήσαιντο** |

Same pattern: δέχομαι "receive" → **δεξαίμην, δέξαιτο**; αἰσθάνομαι "perceive" → **αἰσθοίμην** (2nd aor.) but θεάομαι "behold" → **θεασαίμην** (1st aor.).

## 3. Key Rules & Common Exceptions

| Rule | Explanation | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Know the verb's aorist stem** | γίγνομαι has 2nd aorist ἐγενόμην → γενοίμην; ἡγέομαι has 1st aorist ἡγησάμην → ἡγησαίμην. The optative follows the indicative's stem type. | |
| **2. No augment** | The augment belongs to the indicative only: ἐγένετο but γένοιτο. | |
| **3. 2 sg ends in -οιο / -αιο** | From -οι-σο with loss of σ: γένοιο "may you become". | ὄλβιος γένοιο |
| **4. Epic/Ionic 3 pl -οίατο** | In Homer and Herodotus, γενοίατο = γένοιντο. | |
| **5. Doric/poetry 1 sg -οίμαν** | In choral lyric and tragedy: γενοίμαν. | (Sophocles, chorus) |

## 4. Example Sentences

- μὴ γένοιτο. — "May it not happen!" / "God forbid!"
- ὄλβιος γένοιο, φίλε. — "May you be blessed, friend."
- εἰ τοῦτο γένοιτο, πάντες ἂν σωθεῖμεν. — "If this should happen, we would all be saved." (future-less-vivid condition)
- ἔπεμψε κατάσκοπον ὅπως πύθοιτο τὰ τῶν πολεμίων. — "He sent a scout so that he might learn the enemy's situation." (purpose clause in past sequence)
- οὐκ ἂν ἡγήσαιτο τοῦτο δίκαιον εἶναι. — "He would not consider this to be just." (potential optative)

## 5. Usage & Context

1. **Wishes:** the bare optative (often with εἴθε or εἰ γάρ) expresses a wish for the future: γένοιτο "so may it be" — the Greek "amen".
2. **Purpose and fear clauses in past sequence:** after a past main verb, ἵνα/ὅπως/μή take the optative: ἦλθεν ἵνα πύθοιτο "he came in order to find out".
3. **Future-less-vivid conditions:** εἰ + optative ... ἄν + optative: "if X should happen, Y would happen".
4. **Curse and blessing formulas** love this form — deponents of becoming, receiving, and perceiving in the optative are the backbone of Greek prayers.
