# Lesson: The Present Optative Active (‑ω Verbs)

*Level: beginner–intermediate*
*Prerequisites: the Greek alphabet, basic noun declension, the present indicative active endings*

---

## 1. What the Optative Mood Is

| Feature | Indicative | Subjunctive | **Optative** |
|---------|------------|-------------|--------------|
| **Function** | States a fact or asks a question | Possibility, purpose, exhortation (often in subordinate clauses) | Wish, remote possibility, a "softened" statement (especially in indirect discourse after a past tense) |
| **Rough English equivalent** | "he writes" | "(that) he may write" | "may he write," "he might write" |
| **Aspect in this lesson** | — | — | **Present** (a wish or possibility in progress) as against **aorist** (a single occurrence) |
| **Voice** | active/middle/passive | active/middle/passive | here: **active** |

The **present optative active** expresses a wish, a possibility, or a softened statement, with imperfective aspect and in the active voice.

> **The two formulas to know**: *εἴθε / εἰ γάρ* + optative = "if only…"; optative + *ἄν* = "he might / one could…".

---

## 2. Morphology — the Present Optative Active Pattern

The optative is built on the **present stem**. Its marker is the suffix **‑οι‑** (in contract verbs the singular is extended to ‑οίη‑).

### 2.1 Endings of the Present Optative Active

| Person | Ending | Transliteration |
|--------|--------|-----------------|
| 1st sg. | **‑οιμι** | ‑oimi |
| 2nd sg. | **‑οις** | ‑ois |
| 3rd sg. | **‑οι** | ‑oi |
| 1st pl. | **‑οιμεν** | ‑oimen |
| 2nd pl. | **‑οιτε** | ‑oite |
| 3rd pl. | **‑οιεν** | ‑oien |

> **Note:** the 1st singular ends in **‑οιμι**, not ‑ω. The diphthong **‑οι‑** replaces the thematic vowel of the indicative and is the reliable sign of the optative.

### 2.2 Full Paradigm — a Regular Thematic Verb

The verb **λύω** "to loose":

| Person | Optative active | Transliteration | Meaning |
|--------|-----------------|-----------------|---------|
| 1st sg. | **λύοιμι** | *lýoimi* | "may I loose" |
| 2nd sg. | **λύοις** | *lýois* | "may you loose" |
| 3rd sg. | **λύοι** | *lýoi* | "may he/she loose" |
| 1st pl. | **λύοιμεν** | *lýoimen* | "may we loose" |
| 2nd pl. | **λύοιτε** | *lýoite* | "may you (pl.) loose" |
| 3rd pl. | **λύοιεν** | *lýoien* | "may they loose" |

### 2.3 Contract Verb — **φιλέω** "to love"

In contract verbs the singular prefers the extended suffix **‑οίη‑**:

| Person | Optative active | Meaning |
|--------|-----------------|---------|
| 1st sg. | **φιλοίην** | "may I love" |
| 2nd sg. | **φιλοίης** | "may you love" |
| 3rd sg. | **φιλοίη** | "may he/she love" |
| 1st pl. | **φιλοῖμεν** | "may we love" |
| 2nd pl. | **φιλοῖτε** | "may you (pl.) love" |
| 3rd pl. | **φιλοῖεν** | "may they love" |

*(Longer plural forms such as φιλοίημεν also occur, but the short forms are commoner.)*

### 2.4 Irregular Verb — **εἰμί** "to be"

| Person | Optative active | Meaning |
|--------|-----------------|---------|
| 1st sg. | **εἴην** | "may I be" |
| 2nd sg. | **εἴης** | "may you be" |
| 3rd sg. | **εἴη** | "may he/she be" |
| 1st pl. | **εἶμεν / εἴημεν** | "may we be" |
| 2nd pl. | **εἶτε / εἴητε** | "may you (pl.) be" |
| 3rd pl. | **εἶεν / εἴησαν** | "may they be" |

## 3. Key Rules & Common Exceptions

| Rule | Explanation | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **1. Build on the present stem** | The same stem as the present indicative (λυ‑ from λύω). | λυ‑ → λύοιμι |
| **2. Add the diphthong ‑οι‑** | The present optative always has ‑οι‑ (contract verbs: ‑οίη‑ in the singular). | λύ‑οι‑μι |
| **3. Contract verbs** | The stem vowel merges with οι: φιλε‑ + οίη → φιλοίη. | φιλοίην |
| **4. Accent** | Final ‑οι counts as **long** in the optative: λύοι (not *λῦοι). | — |
| **5. Irregulars** | εἰμί builds special forms from the stem εἰ‑/ἐσ‑. | εἴην |
| **6. Historical fate** | After the classical period the optative fades; in Koine it survives mainly in fixed expressions (μὴ γένοιτο "may it not be!"). | — |

## 4. Example Sentences

| Greek (optative) | Transliteration | English |
|------------------|-----------------|---------|
| **εἴθε ἀεὶ τὴν ἀλήθειαν λέγοις.** | *eíthe aeì tēn alḗtheian légois* | "If only you would always speak the truth." |
| **εἴθε εὐδαίμων εἴης.** | *eíthe eudaímōn eíēs* | "May you be happy." |
| **λέγοι ἄν τις ὅτι χαλεπόν ἐστιν.** | *légoi án tis hóti khalepón estin* | "Someone might say that it is difficult." (potential optative with ἄν) |
| **ἔλεγεν ὅτι γράφοι.** | *élegen hóti gráphoi* | "He said that he was writing." (optative in indirect discourse after a past tense) |

*Note the two main uses: a wish with εἴθε, and possibility with ἄν.*

---

## 5. Usage & Context

1. **Wish** — the particle **εἴθε** or **εἰ γάρ** + present optative expresses a wish for the present or future with imperfective aspect: εἴθε λέγοις "if only you would (keep) speak(ing)."
2. **Potential optative** — optative + **ἄν** makes a soft, cautious assertion: λέγοι ἄν "he might say / one could say." This is the most tactful way of stating an opinion in Attic prose.
3. **Indirect discourse after a past tense** — after a past main verb, the indicative of the direct speech may be changed to the optative: ἔλεγεν ὅτι γράφοι.
4. **Remote (future less vivid) conditions** — εἰ + optative, ἄν + optative: εἰ ταῦτα λέγοις, ἁμαρτάνοις ἄν "if you were to say this, you would be mistaken."
5. **Subordinate clauses after a past tense** — purpose and fear clauses after a past verb often take the optative: ἵνα γράφοι "so that he might write."

---

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Indo-European origin** | The optative was formed with the suffix *‑yeh₁‑/‑ih₁‑; Greek ‑οι‑ (thematic vowel + *‑ih₁‑) and ‑ίη‑ (the full grade of the suffix) both continue it. Sanskrit *bháret* and Old Latin *siet* (later *sit*) are cognate formations. |
| **Early Greek** | In Homer the optative is frequent and versatile. |
| **Classical period** | Attic prose exploits the optative fully for the nuances of indirect narration and politeness. |
| **Koine and later** | The optative dies out rapidly; in the New Testament it is rare outside formulas such as μὴ γένοιτο, and it has disappeared entirely from Modern Greek. |
