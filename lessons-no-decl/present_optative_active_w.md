# Lesson: The Present Optative Active (‑ω)  

*Target level: Beginner → Intermediate*  
*Prerequisites: Greek alphabet, basic noun declension, present‑indicative active endings*  

---

## 1. What is the Optative Mood?

| Feature | Indicative | Subjunctive | **Optative** |
|---------|------------|-------------|--------------|
| **Function** | States facts or asks questions | Expresses potential or “may‑” meaning (often in dependent clauses) | Conveys a wish, a polite request, or a “softened” potential (often in indirect speech) |
| **Typical English equivalents** | “he **writes**”, “did you **see**?” | “may he **write**”, “let him **write**” | “may he **write** (hopefully)”, “if only he **would write**”, “I wish he **would write**” |
| **Tense‑aspect** | Present, imperfect, aorist, perfect, pluperfect | Present, aorent (future‑like) | **Present** (continuous or habitual wish) and **Aorist** (single‑time wish) |
| **Voice** | Active, middle, passive | Active, middle, passive | Active, middle, passive (here we study **Active**) |

The **present optative active** therefore expresses a *present‑time* wish, possibility, or polite request, using the **active voice**.

> **Typical formula**: *εἴθε* + optative = “may it be that …”, *γένοιτο* + optative = “let it happen”.

---

## 2. Morphology – The Present Optative Active Paradigm  

The optative is built on the **present stem** (the same stem used for the present indicative).  
Two main classes:

| Class | Stem type | Optative formation |
|-------|-----------|--------------------|
| **Thematic (‑ο‑) verbs** | Present stem ends in a vowel (‑ο‑) | **Stem + ‑οι‑ + personal endings** |
| **Contract verbs** | Stem ends in ‑α, ‑ε, or ‑ο (contracted with the thematic vowel) | **Contracted stem + ‑οι‑ + endings** |
| **Irregular verbs** (εἰμί, εἶναι, etc.) | Special stems | Irregular forms (see § 4) |

### 2.1 Personal Endings of the Present Optative Active  

| Person | Ending | Transliteration |
|--------|--------|-----------------|
| 1st sg. | **‑οιμι** | ‑oimi |
| 2nd sg. | **‑οις**  | ‑ois |
| 3rd sg. | **‑οι**   | ‑oi |
| 1st pl. | **‑οιμεν**| ‑oimen |
| 2nd pl. | **‑οιτε** | ‑oite |
| 3rd pl. | **‑οιεν** | ‑oien |

> **Note:** In the **first‑person singular** the ending looks like **‑οιμι**, not **‑ω**.  
> The “‑ω” you see in many textbooks refers to the **present‑indicative** first‑person singular (e.g., *λύω* “I loosen”). In the optative, the vowel **‑οι‑** replaces the **‑ω‑** of the indicative.

### 2.2 Full Paradigm – Regular Thematic Verb  

Take the verb **λύω** “to loosen / to release”.

| Person | Optative Active | Transliteration | Gloss |
|--------|----------------|-----------------|-------|
| 1st sg. | **λύοιμι** | ly‑oimi | “may I loosen” |
| 2nd sg. | **λύοις**  | ly‑ois  | “may you loosen” |
| 3rd sg. | **λύοι**   | ly‑oi   | “may he/she/it loosen” |
| 1st pl. | **λύοιμεν**| ly‑oimen| “may we loosen” |
| 2nd pl. | **λύοιτε** | ly‑oite | “may you (pl.) loosen” |
| 3rd pl. | **λύοιεν** | ly‑oien | “may they loosen” |

### 2.3 Contract Verb Example – **φιλέω** “to love”

| Person | Optative Active | Transliteration | Gloss |
|--------|----------------|-----------------|-------|
| 1st sg. | **φιλοίμην**   | philo‑imēn | “may I love” |
| 2nd sg. | **φιλοίης**    | philo‑iēs | “may you love” |
| 3rd sg. | **φιλοίη**     | philo‑iē | “may he/she love” |
| 1st pl. | **φιλοίημεν**  | philo‑iēmen | “may we love” |
| 2nd pl. | **φιλοίητε**   | pholo‑iēte | “may you (pl.) love” |
| 3rd pl. | **φιλοίησαν**  | pholo‑iēsan* (rare) – more common is **φιλοίησαν** (optative pl.) |

*The 3rd‑pl. ending **‑ησαν** is a variant of **‑οιεν** used especially in poetry.

### 2.4 Irregular Verb – **εἰμί** “to be”

| Person | Optative Active | Transliteration | Gloss |
|--------|----------------|-----------------|-------|
| 1st sg. | **εἴην**       | ei‑ēn | “may I be” |
| 2nd sg. | **εἴης**       | ei‑ēs | “may you be” |
| 3rd sg. | **εἴη**        | ei‑ē | “may he/she/it be” |
| 1st pl. | **εἴημεν**     | ei‑ēmen | “may we be” |
| 2nd pl. | **εἴητε**      | ei‑ēte | “may you (pl.) be” |
| 3rd pl. | **εἴησαν**     | ei‑ēsan | “may they be” |

## 3. Key Rules & Common Exceptions  

| Rule | Explanation | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **1. Use the present stem** | Take the same stem that appears in the present indicative (e.g., *λύ‑* from *λύω*). | *λύ‑* → *λύ‑οιμι* |
| **2. Insert the diphthong ‑οι‑** | The optative always contains **‑οι‑** before the personal ending (except for irregular verbs). | *λύ‑* + **‑οι‑** + **‑μι** → *λύοιμι* |
| **3. Contract verbs lose the final vowel** | When the stem ends in ‑α, ‑ε, or ‑ο, the thematic ‑ο‑ contracts, giving a long vowel before **‑οι‑** (e.g., *φιλε‑* → *φιλο‑*). | *φιλε‑* → *φιλο‑οιμι* → *φιλοίμην* |
| **4. The 3rd‑person plural often shows **‑ησαν** in poetry** | In Homeric and later poetic language, **‑ησαν** replaces **‑οιεν**. | *λύοισαν* (poetic) vs. *λύοιεν* (prose) |
| **5. Irregular verbs have unique stems** | Verbs like *εἰμί*, *ἔχω*, *ἔρχομαι* form the optative from special stems. | *εἰμί* → *εἴην* |
| **6. The optative is rarely used in everyday prose after the Classical period** | By the Koine period the optative is largely replaced by the subjunctive, but it survives in formulaic expressions (*εἴθε*, *γένοιτο*, *ὦ*). | *εἴθε* + optative = “may it be that …” |

## 4. Example Sentences  

| Greek (optative) | Transliteration | English |
|------------------|-----------------|---------|
| **εἴθε ὁ διδάσκαλος ἐλθῇ.** | *eíthe ho didáskalos elthē.* | “May the teacher come.” |
| **γένοιτο ὁ πόλεμος εἰρήνην ἀνέχοι.** | *génoito ho pólemos eirḗnēn anéchoi.* | “Let the war bring peace.” |
| **Ἀνέλθοιμεν εἰς τὴν ἀγορὰν.** (optative of *ἀνέρχομαι*) | *Anélthoimen eis tēn agoràn.* | “May we go to the market.” |
| **εἴη ὁ ἥλιος ἀνέφθαρτος.** (optative of *εἰμί*) | *eíē ho hḗlios anéphartos.* | “May the sun be everlasting.” |

*Note:* The first two sentences use **εἴθε** and **γένοιτο**, the most common “wish‑particles” that trigger the optative.

---

## 5. Usage & Context  

1. **Polite requests** – In Classical prose, the optative softens a request, especially after *ἄν* or *εἰ* in indirect speech.  
   *Example:* *ἔφη ἵνα ἡμᾶς βοηθῇ* → “He said that he would help us” (optative after *ἵνα* in indirect speech).  

2. **Wishes** – The particle **εἴθε** + optative expresses a sincere wish.
