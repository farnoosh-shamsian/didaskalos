# Lesson: The Perfect Indicative Active — Irregular Verbs

*Target level: Beginner–Intermediate*
*Prerequisites: Greek alphabet, basic knowledge of present-tense conjugation.*

---

## 1. What the Perfect Indicative Active Means

| Term | Meaning |
|------|--------------------|
| **Perfect** | An action that **has been completed** and whose **result still holds**. |
| **Indicative** | The "real" mood — statements of fact, not wishes or commands. |
| **Active** | The subject **does** the action. |

The perfect answers the question: *"What has the subject done, and what is the situation now?"*

> **γέγραφα τὴν ἐπιστολήν** — "I have written the letter (and the letter now stands written)."

---

## 2. The Regular Formation (for comparison)

1. **Perfect stem** = reduplication + stem (+ κ in the first perfect).
2. **The perfect endings**:

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|--------|----------|--------|
| 1st | ‑α | ‑αμεν |
| 2nd | ‑ας | ‑ατε |
| 3rd | ‑ε(ν) | ‑ασι(ν) |

### Regular Paradigm: **λύω** "to loose"

| | Singular | Plural |
|----------|----------|--------|
| **1st** | **λέλυκα** | **λελύκαμεν** |
| **2nd** | **λέλυκας** | **λελύκατε** |
| **3rd** | **λέλυκε(ν)** | **λελύκασι(ν)** |

*Stem = **λελυκ‑** (reduplication λε‑ + λυ + κ).*

---

## 3. Irregular Perfects

The most frequent verbs do not follow the simple pattern above: they have altered stems, different roots (suppletion), or "Attic reduplication." The most important:

### 3.1 ἔρχομαι "to come" → **ἐλήλυθα**

| Person | Form | Meaning |
|--------|------|-----------------|
| 1 sg. | **ἐλήλυθα** | I have come |
| 2 sg. | **ἐλήλυθας** | you have come |
| 3 sg. | **ἐλήλυθε(ν)** | he/she/it has come |
| 1 pl. | **ἐληλύθαμεν** | we have come |
| 2 pl. | **ἐληλύθατε** | you (pl.) have come |
| 3 pl. | **ἐληλύθασι(ν)** | they have come |

*"Attic reduplication": the first two letters of the root are repeated and the vowel lengthened (ἐλ-ηλυθ‑). The root is that of the aorist ἦλθον.*

### 3.2 γίγνομαι "to become" → **γέγονα**

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|--------|------|--------|
| 1st | **γέγονα** | **γεγόναμεν** |
| 2nd | **γέγονας** | **γεγόνατε** |
| 3rd | **γέγονε(ν)** | **γεγόνασι(ν)** |

*A second perfect (without κ) with vowel change (γεν‑ → γον‑); meaning: "I have become, I am."*

### 3.3 Other Frequent Irregular Perfects

| Verb | Meaning | Perfect | Note |
|------|------|------|-----------------|
| λέγω | to say | **εἴρηκα** | from a different root (ϝερ‑/ῥη‑) |
| ὁράω | to see | **ἑώρακα** | unusual reduplication |
| ἀκούω | to hear | **ἀκήκοα** | Attic reduplication |
| λαμβάνω | to take | **εἴληφα** | εἰ‑ instead of reduplication |
| φέρω | to carry | **ἐνήνοχα** | root ἐνεκ‑, Attic reduplication |
| ἔχω | to have | **ἔσχηκα** | on the aorist stem σχ‑ |
| γιγνώσκω | to recognize | **ἔγνωκα** | without reduplication |
| πάσχω | to suffer | **πέπονθα** | second perfect |

### 3.4 Two Special Cases

- **οἶδα** "I know" — an ancient perfect of the root ἰδ‑ "see" with present meaning. Conjugation: **οἶδα, οἶσθα, οἶδε(ν), ἴσμεν, ἴστε, ἴσασι(ν)**.
- **εἰμί** "to be" — has no perfect at all; γέγονα often takes its place.

## 4. Key Rules & Memory Tips

| Rule | Explanation |
|------|-------------|
| **1. The endings are always the same** | ‑α, ‑ας, ‑ε(ν), ‑αμεν, ‑ατε, ‑ασι(ν) — for regular and irregular stems alike. |
| **2. Attic reduplication** | Vowel-initial verbs sometimes repeat their first two letters: ἀκ-ηκο-α, ἐλ-ηλυθ-α, ἐν-ηνοχ-α. |
| **3. Second perfect = no κ** | Usually with a changed root vowel: γέγονα, πέπονθα, λέλοιπα. |
| **4. Link to the other stems** | Learn the perfect stem alongside the aorist: ἦλθον/ἐλήλυθα, ἔλαβον/εἴληφα, ἔγνων/ἔγνωκα. |
| **5. Accent** | Recessive, as in other finite forms: ἐλήλυθα, ἐληλύθαμεν. |

**Memory tip:** learn these forms within each verb's principal parts — the dictionary's six-column table is your best friend.

---

## 5. Example Sentences

| Greek (perfect indicative active) | Translation |
|--------------------------------------|-------------|
| **ἐλήλυθα εἰς τὴν ἀγοράν.** | I have come to the marketplace (and here I am). |
| **γέγονε βασιλεύς.** | He has become king (and now is king). |
| **εἴρηκα ὅτι ἔρχεται.** | I have said that he is coming. |
| **ἔγνωκα τὸν ἥρωα.** | I have come to know the hero (I know him). |
| **ἀκηκόαμεν τοὺς λόγους.** | We have heard the speeches. |

Notice the **link to the present**: the result of the action is still available to the speaker.

---

## 6. Usage & Context

| Situation | Typical perfect use | Why the perfect? |
|-----------|--------------------|------------------|
| **Resulting state** | ἐλήλυθα "I have come (and am here)." | Emphasizes the present state. |
| **Track record and responsibility** | πεποίηκε ταῦτα "he has done these things." | Orators tie the past to the present with the perfect. |
| **With adverbs** | ἤδη "already," οὔπω "not yet" often accompany perfects. | Temporal nuance. |
| **In indirect statement** | ἔφη ὅτι ἐλήλυθεν "he said that he had come." | The perfect is retained in the subordinate clause. |

**Bottom line:** the irregulars of this lesson are far more frequent in texts than the regular perfects; make ἐλήλυθα, γέγονα, εἴρηκα, and οἶδα second nature and your reading will flow.
