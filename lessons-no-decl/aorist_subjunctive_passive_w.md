# Lesson: The Aorist Passive Subjunctive of ω-Verbs

*Target level: Intermediate*
*Prerequisites: Aorist indicative passive, present subjunctive active.*

---

## 1. What This Form Is

The **aorist passive subjunctive** expresses a single, complete action **undergone by** the subject, in a clause of possibility, purpose, or condition:

> ἐὰν **λυθῶ** — "if I am released"
> ἵνα **σωθῶσιν** — "so that they may be saved"

It has no past meaning. The aorist supplies **aspect** (one complete act), not time.

**The one surprise:** although the meaning is passive, the endings are the **active** ones. The passive is carried entirely by the ‑θη‑/‑η‑ in the stem, and on top of that stem the verb takes ‑ῶ, ‑ῇς, ‑ῇ.

| | Stem | Endings | Result |
|---|---|---|---|
| Aorist passive indicative | ἐ‑λυθη‑ | secondary | ἐλύθην |
| **Aorist passive subjunctive** | λυθε‑ | **active subjunctive** | **λυθῶ** |

---

## 2. Forms and Patterns

### 2.1 First (θη‑) Aorist Passive — **λύω**

The stem λυθε‑ contracts with the long subjunctive vowel exactly like an ε‑contract verb:

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|---|---|---|
| 1st | **λυθῶ** | **λυθῶμεν** |
| 2nd | **λυθῇς** | **λυθῆτε** |
| 3rd | **λυθῇ** | **λυθῶσι(ν)** |

More verbs of the same type:

| Verb | Aorist passive indicative | Subjunctive 3 sg |
|---|---|---|
| παιδεύω "educate" | ἐπαιδεύθην | **παιδευθῇ** |
| πείθω "persuade" | ἐπείσθην | **πεισθῇ** |
| σῴζω "save" | ἐσώθην | **σωθῇ** |
| ἄγω "lead" | ἤχθην | **ἀχθῇ** |
| ποιέω "make" | ἐποιήθην | **ποιηθῇ** |

### 2.2 Second (η‑) Aorist Passive

Identical endings, simply without the θ:

| Verb | Aorist passive indicative | Subjunctive 3 sg |
|---|---|---|
| γράφω "write" | ἐγράφην | **γραφῇ** |
| φαίνω "show" | ἐφάνην | **φανῇ** |
| βλάπτω "harm" | ἐβλάβην | **βλαβῇ** |
| φθείρω "destroy" | ἐφθάρην | **φθαρῇ** |
| στέλλω "send" | ἐστάλην | **σταλῇ** |

Full paradigm of **γραφῶ**: γραφῶ, γραφῇς, γραφῇ, γραφῶμεν, γραφῆτε, γραφῶσι(ν).

## 3. Key Rules & Exceptions

| Rule | Explanation | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Active endings, passive meaning** | ‑ῶ, ‑ῇς, ‑ῇ, ‑ῶμεν, ‑ῆτε, ‑ῶσι(ν) — do not look for ‑ωμαι here. | λυθῶ, not *λυθῶμαι |
| **2. No augment** | ἐλύθην (indicative) but λυθῶ (subjunctive). | |
| **3. Always a circumflex** | The contraction of ε + ω/η always produces a circumflex: λυθῶ, λυθῇς. | |
| **4. Aspect, not time** | ἐὰν λυθῇ = "if he is released," not "if he was released." | |
| **5. Contrast with the present** | λύηται (present passive subjunctive, ongoing) vs. λυθῇ (aorist, single act). | |
| **6. Many are intransitive** | φανῇ usually means "may appear," σωθῇ "may get away safe" — not a true passive. | |
| **7. Extremely common after ἐάν and μή** | This form is the workhorse of conditional and fear clauses in prose. | |

## 4. Example Sentences

| Greek | Translation |
|---|---|
| ἐὰν ἡ πόλις **ἁλῷ**, πάντες **διαφθαρῶσιν**. | "If the city is captured, all will be destroyed." |
| ἔπεμψαν πρέσβεις ἵνα αἱ σπονδαὶ **λυθῶσιν**. | "They sent envoys so that the treaty might be dissolved." |
| φοβοῦνται μὴ ἡ ναῦς **διαφθαρῇ**. | "They fear that the ship may be destroyed." |
| ὅταν ταῦτα **λεχθῇ**, παύσονται. | "When this has been said, they will stop." |
| δέδοικα μὴ οὐ **σωθῶμεν**. | "I am afraid we may not be saved." |

## 5. Usage & Context

1. **Future more vivid conditions** — ἐάν + aorist passive subjunctive is one of the commonest constructions in Thucydides' speeches: ἐὰν νικηθῶμεν …
2. **Purpose clauses** — ἵνα, ὅπως + subjunctive after a primary tense.
3. **Clauses of fearing** — μή "lest" + subjunctive: φοβοῦμαι μὴ βλαβῇ. Add οὐ for a negative fear: μὴ οὐ σωθῶμεν.
4. **Indefinite temporal clauses** — ὅταν, ἐπειδὰν + aorist subjunctive for a completed event within a repeated frame.
5. **Prohibitions** — μή + aorist subjunctive, 2nd person: μὴ φοβηθῇς "do not be afraid."
6. **Aspect choice** — use the aorist for the event, the present for the ongoing state: ἐὰν λυθῇ "if he gets released" vs. ἐὰν λύηται "if he is being released."

---

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology

- The **‑θη‑** passive is a Greek innovation built on an older stative/intransitive suffix, which is why its endings are active in form: originally these were intransitive verbs, not passives, and they simply took the ordinary active endings.
- The **‑η‑ (second) passive** preserves the older bare stative suffix, seen also in ἐμάνην "I went mad."
- Because the stem ends in a short ε, the subjunctive contraction is identical to that of ε‑contract verbs — which is why λυθῶ looks like φιλῶ.
- The ‑θη‑ formation kept expanding through Koine and survives as the Modern Greek passive aorist (λύθηκα), making it one of the most durable innovations in the history of the Greek verb.
