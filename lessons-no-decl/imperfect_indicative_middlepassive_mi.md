# Lesson: The Imperfect — Indicative — Middle/Passive (μι)

---

## 1. What the Imperfect Middle/Passive Is  

| Term | Meaning in English | When it is used |
|------|-------------------|-----------------|
| **Imperfect** | A past‑time “continuous” or “repeated” aspect. It describes an action that was ongoing, habitual, or repeated in the past, or a past state. | “He was speaking”, “They used to go”, “I was happy”. |
| **Indicative** | The “real” mood – statements of fact, questions, or ordinary narration. | Not a wish, command, or potential. |
| **Middle/Passive** | The **middle** voice shows the subject acting on itself, for its own benefit, or in a reflexive‑like way. The **passive** voice shows the subject receiving the action. In the imperfect, the middle and passive share the same endings **‑μι** (‑mi). | “He was washing himself” (middle) vs. “He was washed” (passive). |

**Bottom line:** The form **‑μι** in the imperfect indicates either a middle or a passive meaning, depending on the verb’s semantics and the context. It is always **indicative** (i.e., a factual past statement).

---

## 2. Forms and Patterns  

### 2.1. The General Imperfect Middle/Passive Ending  

| Person | Ending | Example with **λῡ́ω** (to loosen) – middle/passive |
|--------|--------|---------------------------------------------------|
| 1st sg. | **‑μι** | λῡ́ομαι – “I was being loosened / I was loosening (myself)”. |
| 2nd sg. | **‑σο** | λῡ́ῃ· (rare, mostly poetic) – “you were being loosened”. |
| 3rd sg. | **‑το** | λῡ́εται – “he/she/it was being loosened”. |
| 1st pl. | **‑μεν** | λῡ́ομεν – “we were being loosened”. |
| 2nd pl. | **‑τε** | λῡ́εσθε – “you (pl.) were being loosened”. |
| 3rd pl. | **‑ντο** | λῡ́οντο – “they were being loosened”. |

> **Note:** The 2nd‑person singular **‑σο** is very rare in Attic prose; it survives mainly in poetry and in some irregular verbs (e.g., **ἔρχομαι** → ἔρχου). In most textbooks it is omitted, and the 2nd‑sg. middle/passive is given as **‑σαι** in the present, but in the imperfect the standard form is **‑σο**.

### 2.2. Building the Imperfect Stem  

1. **Take the present stem** (the one used for the present system).  
2. **Add the augment** (a short **ε‑** before a consonant, or lengthen the initial vowel).  
3. **Insert the thematic vowel** **‑ε‑** (for thematic verbs) or **‑ο‑** (for athematic verbs).  
4. **Add the middle/passive ending** from the table above.

#### Example: **φιλέω** “to love” (thematic)

| Step | Form |
|------|------|
| Present stem | **φιλε‑** |
| Augment | **ε‑** → **εφιλε‑** |
| Thematic vowel **‑ε‑** | **εφιλε‑ε‑** |
| Add endings | **εφιλε‑ε‑μι**, **εφιλε‑ε‑το**, … |

Result (selected forms):

| Person | Imperfect middle/passive |
|--------|--------------------------|
| 1sg | **εφιλέμην** – “I was loving (myself) / I was being loved”. |
| 3sg | **εφιλέτο** – “he/she/it was loving (himself) / was being loved”. |
| 1pl | **εφιλέμεν** – “we were loving / were being loved”. |

#### Example: **ἔρχομαι** “to come, go” (middle) – irregular

| Person | Imperfect middle |
|--------|------------------|
| 1sg | **ἤρχοντο** (rare) – “I was coming (myself)”. |
| 3sg | **ἤρχετο** – “he/she/it was coming”. |
| 1pl | **ἤρχομεν** – “we were coming”. |

> **Tip:** Many verbs that are **deponent** (middle‑only in the present system) keep only the middle forms in the imperfect; they never have a passive meaning.

### 2.3. Full Paradigm for a Regular Thematic Verb  

Below is the complete imperfect middle/passive paradigm for **παίω** “to spare, be kind to” (regular thematic -ω verb). The augment is **ε‑**; the thematic vowel is **‑ε‑**.

| Person | Form | Transliteration | Gloss |
|--------|------|----------------|-------|
| 1sg | **ἐπαίμην** | *epáimēn* | “I was sparing / I was being spared”. |
| 2sg | **ἐπαίσο** | *epáiso* | “you were sparing / you were being spared”. |
| 3sg | **ἐπαίτο** | *epáito* | “he/she/it was sparing / was being spared”. |
| 1pl | **ἐπαίμεν** | *epáimen* | “we were sparing / were being spared”. |
| 2pl | **ἐπαίτε** | *epáite* | “you (pl.) were sparing / were being spared”. |
| 3pl | **ἐπαίντο** | *epáinto* | “they were sparing / were being spared”. |

### 2.4. Paradigm for an A‑thematic Verb (e.g., **ἔρχομαι** – irregular)

| Person | Form | Transliteration | Gloss |
|--------|------|----------------|-------|
| 1sg | **ἤρχετο** | *ērchéto* | “I was coming (myself)”. |
| 2sg | **ἤρχου** (poetic) / **ἤρχου** (rare) | *ērchou* | “you were coming”. |
| 3sg | **ἤρχετο** | *ērchéto* | “he/she/it was coming”. |
| 1pl | **ἤρχομεν** | *ērchomen* | “we were coming”. |
| 2pl | **ἤρχεσθε** | *ērchesthe* | “you (pl.) were coming”. |
| 3pl | **ἤρχοντο** | *ērchonto* | “they were coming”. |

## 3. Key Rules & Common Exceptions  

| Rule | Explanation | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **1. Augment** – add **ε‑** before a consonant; lengthen an initial vowel (α → ἠ, ε → η, ι → η, ο → ω, υ → ῡ). | **λῡ́ω** → **ἐλῡ́μην**; **ἀκούω** → **ἤκουον**. |
| **2. Thematic vowel** – **‑ε‑** for regular -ω verbs; **‑ο‑** for athematic verbs (e.g., **ἔρχομαι**, **ἵστημι**). | **φιλέω** → **εφιλέμην**; **ἵστημι** → **ἔστην** (active) but **ἔστην** (middle) → **ἔστην** (no distinct middle/passive). |
| **3. Middle vs. Passive meaning** – determined by the verb’s lexical entry. Deponent verbs are always middle; verbs that admit a true passive will use the same forms for both meanings. | **παίω** (active) → **ἐπαίμην** (middle = “I was sparing”) vs. **παίω** (passive) → **ἐπαίμην** (passive = “I was being spared”). |
| **4. 2nd‑sg. **‑σο** is rare** – most prose writers replace it with the 3rd‑sg. **‑το** or simply avoid the imperfect middle/passive in 2nd‑sg. | In Attic prose you will see **ἐπαίτο** used for “you were being spared”. |
| **5. Verbs with a **‑σ‑** in the present stem often lose the **σ** in the imperfect middle/passive.** | **πράσσω** → **ἐπρά̄σθην** (passive) but **πράσσω** → **ἐπρά̄σθην** (same). |
| **6. Irregular verbs** – some have suppletive stems (e.g., **εἰμί** “to be” → imperfect **ἦν** (active) has no middle/passive). | No imperfect middle/passive for **εἰμί**. |
| **7. Contract verbs** – the augment may cause contraction. | **φιλέω** → **εφιλέμην** (no contraction), but **δείκνυμι** → **ἐδείκνυτο** (contracted). |

## 4. Example Sentences  

| Greek (Imperfect, middle/passive) | Transliteration | English |
|-----------------------------------|----------------|---------|
| 1. **ἐπαίμην τὸν φίλον** | *epáimen tòn phílon* | “I was sparing my friend (or I was being spared by my friend).” |
| 2. **ἤρχετο εἰς τὴν ἀγορὰν** | *ērchéto eis tḕn agoràn* | “He was coming to the market.” (middle) |
| 3. **ἐπαίτο ὑπὸ τῶν ἀνθρώπων** | *epáito hypò tôn anthrṓpōn* | “He was being spared by the people.” |
| 4. **ἐπαίσθην ὑπὸ τῆς θύρας** | *epáistēn hypò tês thýras* | “I was being opened by the door.” (passive of **ἀνοίγω**). |
| 5. **ἔπλεον ἐπὶ τῆς θαλάσσης** (active for contrast) | *épleon epì tês thalássēs* | “We were sailing on the sea.” (active imperfect). |

*Notice how the same ending **‑μην / ‑το / ‑ντο** can convey either a middle or a passive sense.*

## 5. Usage & Context  

1. **Narrative Past** – The imperfect is the default tense for background actions in stories, myths, and histories.  
2. **Habitual Action** – When a past habit is described, the imperfect is used, even with middle/passive meaning.  
   - *ἦλθον ἐπὶ τὴν ἀγορὰν ὡς ἔπλεον* – “I used to go to the market as I was sailing.”  
3. **State of Being** – The imperfect can describe a past state, especially with verbs of feeling or condition.  
   - *ἦν ἐπαίμην* – “I was (in a state of) being spared.”  
4. **Poetic License** – In poetry the rare 2nd‑sg. **‑σο** and other irregular forms appear more often.  
5. **Deponent Verbs** – Since they lack a true passive, the imperfect middle is the only voice available.  
   - *ἔχοντο* (from **ἔχω**) – “they were having (themselves)”.

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology  

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Proto‑Indo‑European Roots** | The imperfect derives from a **suffix *‑s‑* + *‑t‑*** that expressed past continuous aspect. The augment **ε‑** is a later Greek innovation to mark past time. |
| **Middle Voice Origin** | The middle originally expressed reflexive or “self‑acting” actions, later broadened to include “benefactive” and “reciprocal” meanings. The passive developed from the middle by adding the notion of the subject being acted upon. |
| **‑μι Ending** | The **‑μι** ending is inherited from PIE *‑mi* (first‑person singular) and later generalized to all persons in the middle/passive imperfect. The other endings (**‑σο, ‑το, ‑μεν, ‑τε, ‑ντο**) reflect older PIE personal endings. |
| **Loss of Distinct Passive** | In the imperfect, Greek merged the middle and passive endings, unlike the present system where they are separate (**‑ομαι** vs. **‑μαι**). This reflects a phonological simplification in the past‑tense system. |
