# Lesson: The Future Middle Participle of ω-Verbs

*Target level: Intermediate*
*Prerequisites: Future indicative middle, present middle participle.*

---

## 1. What This Form Is

The **future middle participle** means "about to … for oneself," "going to …":

> **λυσόμενος** "about to ransom" · **μαχούμενος** "going to fight" · **ἐσόμενος** "about to be"

Like the future active participle, it points **forward in time** from the moment of the main verb, and its chief use is to express **purpose** after verbs of motion.

It matters more than its modest frequency suggests, because a large number of very common verbs have **no future active at all** — their future is middle in form:

| Present (active form) | Future (middle form) | Participle |
|---|---|---|
| ἀκούω "hear" | ἀκούσομαι | **ἀκουσόμενος** |
| λαμβάνω "take" | λήψομαι | **ληψόμενος** |
| μανθάνω "learn" | μαθήσομαι | **μαθησόμενος** |
| βαίνω "go" | βήσομαι | **βησόμενος** |
| εἰμί "be" | ἔσομαι | **ἐσόμενος** |

For these verbs the future middle participle is simply *the* future participle — there is no active alternative.

---

## 2. Forms and Patterns

### 2.1 Formation

Future stem + the ordinary middle participle endings **‑όμενος, ‑ομένη, ‑όμενον**:

| Verb | Future indicative | Future middle participle |
|---|---|---|
| λύω "loose" | λύσομαι | **λυσόμενος, ‑ομένη, ‑όμενον** |
| παύω "stop" | παύσομαι | **παυσόμενος** |
| βούλομαι "want" | βουλήσομαι | **βουλησόμενος** |
| γίγνομαι "become" | γενήσομαι | **γενησόμενος** |
| μάχομαι "fight" | μαχοῦμαι | **μαχούμενος** |
| εἰμί "be" | ἔσομαι | **ἐσόμενος** |

### 2.2 Declension

This is the easiest participle in Greek: it declines like an ordinary **first-and-second declension adjective** (ἀγαθός, ἀγαθή, ἀγαθόν) — no third-declension stems, no ντ‑clusters:

| Case | Masculine | Feminine | Neuter |
|---|---|---|---|
| Nom. sg. | λυσόμενος | λυσομένη | λυσόμενον |
| Gen. sg. | λυσομένου | λυσομένης | λυσομένου |
| Dat. sg. | λυσομένῳ | λυσομένῃ | λυσομένῳ |
| Acc. sg. | λυσόμενον | λυσομένην | λυσόμενον |
| Nom. pl. | λυσόμενοι | λυσόμεναι | λυσόμενα |
| Gen. pl. | λυσομένων | λυσομένων | λυσομένων |
| Dat. pl. | λυσομένοις | λυσομέναις | λυσομένοις |
| Acc. pl. | λυσομένους | λυσομένας | λυσόμενα |

### 2.3 Liquid Futures

Stems in λ, μ, ν, ρ drop the σ and contract:

| Verb | Future | Participle |
|---|---|---|
| μάχομαι "fight" | μαχοῦμαι | **μαχούμενος** |
| μένω "remain" | μενῶ | (active: μενῶν) |
| ἀγγέλλω "report" | ἀγγελοῦμαι | **ἀγγελούμενος** |

---

## 3. Key Rules & Exceptions

| Rule | Explanation | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Regular adjective endings** | ‑ος, ‑η, ‑ον throughout. Nothing to memorise beyond the future stem. | |
| **2. Accent is fixed on the ‑ο‑** | λυσόμενος, λυσομένου — the accent does not shift as in ντ‑participles. | |
| **3. Middle form, often active sense** | For deponents and for verbs like ἀκούω the middle form carries no middle meaning. | ἀκουσόμενος "about to hear" |
| **4. Not the same as the future passive participle** | λυσόμενος (middle) vs. λυθησόμενος (passive, "about to be loosed"). | |
| **5. Purpose after verbs of motion** | Same construction as the active: ἦλθον μαχούμενοι. | |
| **6. ὡς + participle marks alleged purpose** | ὡς μαχούμενοι "on the pretext of intending to fight." | |
| **7. τὸ ἐσόμενον = "the future"** | The substantivised neuter is a standard idiom: τὰ ἐσόμενα "what is to come." | |

---

## 4. Example Sentences

| Greek | Translation |
|---|---|
| ἦλθον **μαχούμενοι** ὑπὲρ τῆς πατρίδος. | "They came to fight for their fatherland." |
| ἔπεμψαν ναῦς **ληψομένας** τὸν σῖτον. | "They sent ships to fetch the grain." |
| οἱ **ἐσόμενοι** κίνδυνοι μεγάλοι εἰσίν. | "The dangers that are to come are great." |
| παρῆσαν ὡς **ἀκουσόμενοι** τοὺς λόγους. | "They were present in order to hear the speeches." |
| οὐδεὶς ᾔδει τὰ **γενησόμενα**. | "No one knew what was going to happen." |

---

## 5. Usage & Context

1. **Purpose after verbs of motion** — the primary use, exactly parallel to the future active participle: πέμπω, ἔρχομαι, ἥκω + future middle participle.
2. **With ὡς** — the intention is presented as the subject's own, not vouched for by the narrator.
3. **Substantival neuter** — τὰ ἐσόμενα, τὰ γενησόμενα "future events." Thucydides uses these abstractions constantly when discussing foresight and prediction.
4. **Attributive** — ὁ ἀκουσόμενος "the man who is going to listen."
5. **Deponent futures** — remember that many verbs active in the present are middle in the future; their participle will be this form and no other.

---

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology

- The middle participle suffix **‑μενο‑** is inherited directly from Indo-European *‑mh₁no‑, the same element visible in Sanskrit *‑māna‑* and, more distantly, in Latin *alumnus*.
- Because ‑μενο‑ makes a plain thematic adjective, the middle participle escaped the complications of the ντ‑stems, which is why it is the most regular participle in the language.
- The **future middle for active meaning** (ἀκούσομαι, λήψομαι) is an archaism: in early Greek the middle voice was much broader, and these verbs preserve an older distribution.
- The form faded with the rest of the future system in Koine; Modern Greek expresses futurity analytically with θα and has no future participle at all.
