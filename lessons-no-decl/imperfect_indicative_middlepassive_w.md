# Lesson: The Imperfect Indicative – Middle/Passive (‑ω)  

*Target level: Beginner‑Intermediate*  
*Prerequisites: Greek alphabet, basic noun declension, present‑indicative active endings, concept of voice.*

---

## 1. What is the Imperfect Indicative – Middle/Passive?

The **imperfect** is a past‑tense mood that describes an **ongoing, repeated, or habitual** action in the past, or a past state.  
When the **middle/passive** voice is used, the subject is either:

* **Middle** – acting on its own behalf, for its own benefit, or in a reflexive sense (e.g., “I was washing **myself**”).  
* **Passive** – receiving the action (e.g., “The house was being built”).

In the **middle/passive** the **ending –ω** (omega) marks the **first‑person singular**. Other persons have their own characteristic endings (‑εις, ‑ετο, ‑ομεν, ‑εσθε, ‑οντο).

> **Key idea:** The imperfect middle/passive tells us that *in the past* the speaker (or another subject) was **in the middle of** or **undergoing** an action.

---

## 2. Forms and Patterns  

### 2.1. Regular –ω Conjugation (the “ω‑type” verbs)

Most **ω‑type** verbs form the imperfect middle/passive by adding the **imperfect middle/passive endings** to the **present stem** (the same stem used for the present middle/passive).  

| Person | Active ending | Middle/Passive ending | Example with **λύω** “to loose / to release” |
|--------|---------------|-----------------------|--------------------------------------------|
| 1st sg | –ον          | **–ομην** (later –ον) | ἔλυον **(active)** → ἔλυον **(active)** – not needed here |
| 1st sg | –ον          | **–ομην** → **–ον** (later) | **ἔλυον** (active) → **ἔλυον** (middle/passive) → **ἔλυον**?  *Better example:* **ἔλυον** is active; the middle/passive is **ἔλυον**? Actually the imperfect middle/passive of λύω is **ἔλυον** (active) vs **ἔλυον**? Let's use a clearer verb: **πράττω** (to do) → middle/passive **πράττομαι** present; imperfect **πράττομην** (I was doing, for my own sake). The 1st sg ending is **–μην**. The later contracted form **–ν** appears in poetry. |
| 2nd sg | –ες          | **–ου** (later –σο) | **πράττοσο** (poetic) / **πράττο** (later) |
| 3rd sg | –ε(ν)        | **–ετο** | **πράττοτο** → **πράττοτο** (archaic) → **πράττοτο**? Actually the standard is **πράττοτο**? The usual form is **πράττοτο** (rare) → **πράττοτο**? Let's give the standard table: |
| 1st pl | –ομεν        | **–ομεθα** | **πράττομεθα** |
| 2nd pl | –ετε         | **–εσθε** | **πράττεσθε** |
| 3rd pl | –ον          | **–οντο** | **πράττοντο** |

Because the above is confusing, we will present a clean paradigm using a well‑known verb **πράττω** (to do, act) whose middle/passive present is **πράττομαι**.  

### 2.2. Full Imperfect Middle/Passive Paradigm  

| Person | Ending | Form (πράττω) | Gloss |
|--------|--------|---------------|-------|
| 1st sg | **‑μην** | **ἔπραττομην** | I was doing (for myself / was being done) |
| 2nd sg | **‑σο** (poetic) / **‑σο** → **‑σο** (later **‑σο**) | **ἔπραττοσο** | you were doing |
| 3rd sg | **‑το** | **ἔπραττο** | he/she/it was doing |
| 1st pl | **‑μεθα** | **ἔπραττομεθα** | we were doing |
| 2nd pl | **‑σθε** | **ἔπραττοσθε** | you (pl.) were doing |
| 3rd pl | **‑ντο** | **ἔπραττον** | they were doing |

> **Note:** The **ε** of the imperfect marker (the “ε‑” prefix) is placed before the middle/passive ending. In the 1st sg the **‑μην** ending already contains the “‑ν” of the imperfect, so the prefix appears as **ἔ‑** + **πραττ‑** + **ο‑μην** → **ἔπραττομην**.

### 2.3. Verbs with a **‑ε‑** (contracted) stem  

Some verbs have a **contracted** present stem (ending in **‑ε‑** or **‑ο‑**). Their imperfect middle/passive forms contract similarly.

| Verb (present middle/passive) | Stem | Imperfect 1st sg | Example |
|------------------------------|------|------------------|---------|
| **ἔρχομαι** “to come” | ἐρχ‑ | **ἔερχον** (active) → middle/passive **ἔερχον**? Actually the imperfect middle/passive of ἔρχομαι is **ἔερχον**? The correct form is **ἔερχον** (active) vs **ἔερχον**? The middle/passive is **ἔερχον**? This is messy. Let's choose a clearer verb: **βούλομαι** “to wish, want”. Stem **βουλ‑**. Imperfect 1st sg middle/passive: **ἔβουλομην**. |
| **δείκνυμι** “to show” (middle/passive **δείκνομαι**) | δείκν‑ | **ἔδεικνυμην** (active) vs **ἔδεικνυμην**? Use **δείκνομαι** → imperfect **ἔδεικνυμην**? Actually the imperfect middle/passive of **δείκνῑ** is **ἔδεικνυμην**. |

For the purpose of this lesson we will focus on the regular **‑ω** verbs, which cover the majority of middle/passive imperfect forms.

## 3. Key Rules & Common Exceptions  

| Rule | Explanation |
|------|-------------|
| **1. Add the imperfect marker ε‑** before the present middle/passive stem. | Example: **πράττω** → stem **πραττ‑** → ε‑ + **πραττ‑** + ending. |
| **2. Use the middle/passive endings, not the active ones.** | Endings: ‑μην, ‑σο, ‑το, ‑μεθα, ‑σθε, ‑ντο. |
| **3. The 1st sg ending is ‑μην (later contracted to ‑ν).** | In poetry you will often see **‑ν** alone (e.g., **ἔπραττον**). |
| **4. Verbs whose present middle/passive ends in ‑ομαι** (‑ο‑‑μαι) keep the **‑ο‑** before the ending. | **πράττομαι → ἔπραττομην** (the **‑ο‑** stays). |
| **5. Contracted verbs (‑ε‑/‑ο‑) contract the ε‑ with the stem vowel.** | **βούλομαι → ἔβουλομην** (ε‑ + βουλ‑ → ἐβουλ‑). |
| **6. The imperfect middle/passive can be used for **reflexive** meaning even when the verb is normally active.** | **ἔπλῡν** (active) vs **ἔπλῡν**? Better example: **ἔπλῡν** is active; the middle/passive of **πλέω** “to wash” is **πλέομαι** → imperfect **ἔπλεομην** “I was washing myself”. |
| **7. The middle and passive are not distinguished in form in the imperfect; context tells you which voice is meant.** | **ἔπραττο** can mean “he was doing (for himself)” or “he was being done (to him)”. |
| **8. Irregular verbs** (e.g., **εἰμί** “to be”) have their own imperfect middle/passive forms: **ἦν** (active) has no middle/passive; **εἰμί** is defective in the middle/passive. | No middle/passive imperfect for **εἰμί**. |

## 4. Example Sentences  

| Greek (Imperfect Middle/Passive) | Transliteration | English Translation |
|----------------------------------|----------------|---------------------|
| **ἔπραττομην ἐν τῇ ἀγορᾷ.** | *eprattomēn en tē agorā* | I was working (for my own sake) in the marketplace. |
| **ἔπραττοσαν τὸν πόλεμον.** | *eprattosan ton polemon* | They were being waged (the war was being waged). |
| **ἔπλεομεν ἐν τῷ ποταμῷ.** | *epleomen en tō potamō* | We were sailing (or were being sailed) on the river. |
| **ἔβλεπον ἑαυτὸν ἐν τῇ ἀνθρώπῳ.** (reflexive) | *eblepōn heauton en tē anthrōpō* | I was looking at myself in the mirror. |

*Notice the prefix **ἔ‑** indicating the imperfect, and the middle/passive endings.*

## 5. Usage & Context  

1. **Narrative past** – In prose and epic, the imperfect middle/passive often describes background actions or states:  
   *“While the soldiers were being armed, the commander was speaking.”* → **ἔπυγμάζοντο** (passive) + **ἔλεγε** (active).

2. **Habitual or repeated past actions** – “He used to wash himself every morning.” → **ἔπλετο** (reflexive) repeated.

3. **Describing a past condition** – “The city was being defended.” → **ἔφυλασσοντο**.

4. **Poetic contraction** – In poetry the **‑ν** ending (instead of **‑μην**) is common, especially in the 1st sg: **ἔπραττον** for **ἔπραττομην**.

5. **Distinguishing voice** – Since the forms are identical for middle and passive, look for:
   * a reflexive pronoun (ἑαυτοῦ) → middle  
   * a logical patient (object) → passive.

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology  

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Proto‑Indo‑European origin** | The imperfect derives from the PIE *‑eH‑* suffix (aorist‑like) combined with the *‑s‑* marker of the indicative. The middle/passive endings stem from the PIE *‑h₁‑* (middle) and *‑to‑* (passive) series. |
| **Greek evolution** | Early Greek (Homeric) used a **‑ν** ending for the 1st sg middle/passive (e.g., **ἔπραττον**). Later Classical Greek standardized **‑μην**. The **‑σο** ending for 2nd sg is a relic of the older *‑s‑* middle. |
| **Phonological change** | The vowel **ε** of the imperfect marker merged with the preceding vowel of the stem, producing contractions (e.g., **ἔβουλομην** from *ε‑βουλ‑*). |
| **Semantic shift** | Originally the middle expressed “self‑action” (reflexive) and the passive “being acted upon”. Over time the distinction blurred in the imperfect, and context became the main cue. |
| **Relation to other tenses** | The imperfect shares its endings with the **present middle/passive**; the only difference is the **ε‑** prefix signalling past time. This makes the imperfect easy to form once the present middle/passive paradigm is known. |
