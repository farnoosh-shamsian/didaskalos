# Lesson: The Present‑Active Infinitive (ending ‑ειν, ‑ᾶν, ‑εῖν, ‑οῦν)  

*Target level: Beginner → Intermediate*  

In this lesson we will learn what the **present‑active infinitive** is, how it is formed for the different verb classes, and how it is used in Ancient Greek.  The ending **‑ω** that you will see in the first‑person singular present indicative (e.g., *λύω*) is the “signal” that the verb belongs to the present‑active system, and it helps us recognise the stem from which the infinitive is built.

---

## 1. What is the Present‑Active Infinitive?

| Term | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Infinitive** | The “to‑do” form of a verb. It is non‑finite, i.e., it has no person or number. |
| **Present** | Refers to the present time (or a timeless, general action). |
| **Active** | The subject of the verb performs the action (as opposed to middle or passive). |
| **Form** | In Ancient Greek the present‑active infinitive ends in **‑ειν** for regular verbs, and in **‑ᾶν, ‑εῖν, ‑οῦν** for the three groups of contract verbs. |

The infinitive can be thought of as the “dictionary entry” of a verb: it tells you the basic meaning and the conjugational class.

---

## 2. Forms and Patterns  

### 2.1 Regular (non‑contract) verbs  

The present‑active infinitive is built from the **present stem** + **‑ειν**.

| Example verb | Present stem | Infinitive |
|--------------|--------------|------------|
| λύω “I loose / I release” | λυ‑ | λύειν |
| γράφω “I write” | γραφ‑ | γράφειν |
| παιδέω “I teach” | παιδε‑ | παιδέειν |
| ἀκούω “I hear” | ακου‑ | ἀκούειν |

*Note*: The accent of the infinitive is placed on the penult (the vowel before **‑ειν**) unless the stem ends in a long vowel, in which case the accent falls on the long vowel itself (e.g., **ἀκούειν**).

---

### 2.2 Contract verbs  

Contract verbs have a vowel (α, η, ω) at the end of the stem that “contracts” with the infinitive ending. Three patterns exist:

| Contract type | Stem ending | Infinitive ending | Example (verb) | Infinitive |
|---------------|-------------|-------------------|----------------|------------|
| **‑α‑** (α‑contract) | ‑α | ‑ᾶν | τιμάω “I honor” | τιμᾶν |
| **‑ε‑** (ε‑contract) | ‑ε | ‑εῖν | ποιέω “I do, make” | ποιεῖν |
| **‑ο‑** (ο‑contract) | ‑ο | ‑οῦν | δηλόω “I show” | δηλοῦν |

**Why the different endings?**  
The vowel of the stem merges with the vowel of the infinitive suffix **‑ειν**:

* α + ει → ᾶν  
* ε + ει → εῖν  
* ο + ει → οῦν  

---

### 2.3 Full paradigm for a regular verb (λύω)

| Person/Number | Present‑Active Indicative | Present‑Active Infinitive |
|---------------|---------------------------|---------------------------|
| 1st sg. | λύω | — |
| 2nd sg. | λῦς | — |
| 3rd sg. | λῦει(ν) | λύειν |
| 1st pl. | λῦομεν | — |
| 2nd pl. | λῦετε | — |
| 3rd pl. | λῦουσι(ν) | — |

The infinitive is the same for all persons; it is listed only once.

---

## 3. Key Rules & Common Exceptions  

| Rule | Explanation | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **1. Use the present stem** | The infinitive is formed from the same stem that appears in the present indicative (λυ‑, γράφ‑, τιμα‑, …). | λύειν from λυ‑ |
| **2. Accent placement** | Accent the penult unless the stem ends in a long vowel; then the accent stays on that long vowel. | ἀκούειν (penult), ἀγγέλλειν (stem‑long) |
| **3. Contract verbs** | Contract the vowel of the stem with **‑ειν** → **‑ᾶν, ‑εῖν, ‑οῦν**. | τιμᾶν, ποιεῖν, δηλοῦν |
| **4. Irregular infinitives** | A few very common verbs have irregular infinitives: *εἰμί* (to be), *ἔχω* (to have) → *ἔχειν*, *ἔχειν* is regular; *φημί* → *φαίνειν* (rare). | εἰμί → εἶναι (suppletive) |
| **5. No infinitive for some verbs** | Verbs that are defective in the present (e.g., *ἔρχομαι* has no present‑active infinitive; its infinitive is *ἔρχεσθαι* – middle). | — |
| **6. Double‑contraction** | In some dialects (e.g., Ionic) the infinitive of a‑contract verbs may appear as **‑αειν** (e.g., *τιμαειν*), but the Classical Attic form **‑ᾶν** is standard. | — |

## 4. Example Sentences  

| Greek (with transliteration) | Translation |
|------------------------------|-------------|
| **Λύειν** τὸν δεσμώτην ἐστὶν ἡ ἀπόστολος. | “To loose the prisoner is the mission.” |
| **Τιμᾶν** τὸν θεόν ἐστὶν ἀρετή. | “Honoring the god is a virtue.” |
| **Ποιεῖν** τὸ ἔργον ἀγαθὸν ἐστὶν ἡ ἀρετή. | “To do a good work is virtue.” |
| **Δηλοῦν** τὰ ῥήματα τῆς δικαιοσύνης. | “To show the words of justice.” |
| **Ἀκούειν** τὸν σοφον ἀγαθὸν ἐστὶν χρήσιμον. | “To hear the wise man is useful.” |

Notice that the infinitive can function as a **subject**, **object**, or **complement** of another verb.

---

## 5. Notes on Usage & Context  

1. **Subject of a clause** – The infinitive can stand at the beginning of a sentence, acting like a noun:  
   *Λύειν τὸν δεσμώτην ἐστὶν ἀνάγκη.* – “To free the prisoner is necessary.”

2. **Object of a verb** – Many verbs of saying, thinking, or wanting take an infinitive complement:  
   *Βούλομαι λύειν τὸν δεσμώτην.* – “I want to free the prisoner.”

3. **After verbs of perception** – *ὁρῶ, ἀκούω* often take the infinitive:  
   *Ὁρῶ λύειν τὸν δεσμώτην.* – “I see (him) freeing the prisoner.”

4. **With prepositions** – The infinitive can follow *πρὸς* (toward) or *εἰς* (into) to express purpose:  
   *Ἔρχεται πρὸς λύειν τὸν δεσμώτην.* – “He comes in order to free the prisoner.”

5. **Poetic vs. Prose** – In epic poetry the infinitive is often omitted in favor of a **verbal noun** (the *gerundive*), but in prose the infinitive is the normal way to express “to do …”.

---

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology  

| Period | Development |
|--------|--------------|
| **Proto‑Indo‑European** | The infinitive originally ended in *‑ti* (e.g., *bʰéreti* “to carry”). Greek inherited a different formation, using the suffix *‑ειν* attached to the present stem. |
| **Mycenaean Greek** | The infinitive is already attested in Linear B tablets with the *‑ei* suffix. |
| **Classical Attic** | The three contract patterns (‑ᾶν, ‑εῖν, ‑οῦν) become regularized; the non‑contract **‑ειν** dominates for regular verbs. |
| **Koine & Byzantine** | The infinitive survives largely unchanged, though the use of the infinitive in subordinate clauses becomes more flexible. |
| **Etymology of the suffix** | The suffix **‑ειν** is related to the Proto‑Greek *‑ei* and ultimately to the PIE *‑ti* infinitive marker, showing a shift from a *‑ti* to a vowel‑plus‑n construction. |

### Bottom Line  

- The **present‑active infinitive** expresses a present‑time, active action in a non‑finite way.  
- It is built from the **present stem** + **‑ειν** (regular) or the **contracted** endings **‑ᾶν, ‑εῖν, ‑οῦν**.  
- Recognising the **‑ω** ending in the first‑person singular present indicative helps you locate the stem needed for the infinitive.  
- Use the infinitive as a noun‑like element (subject, object, complement) or after certain verbs and prepositions.  

Now you have the tools to identify, form, and understand the present‑active infinitive in Ancient Greek!
