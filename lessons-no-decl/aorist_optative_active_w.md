# Lesson: The Aorist Optative Active (ω-Verbs)

**Goal:** By the end of this lesson you will be able to recognize, form, and translate the aorist optative active of regular verbs and to know its main uses.

---

## 1. What Is the Aorist Optative?

| Term | Meaning in Ancient Greek |
|------|--------------------------|
| **Aorist** | The aspect of a "single event": the action is viewed as a whole, without reference to internal duration. Outside the indicative it **does not convey past time**. |
| **Optative** | The mood of wish, remote possibility, and polite request; in Classical Greek it also appears in indirect speech after a past-tense main verb and in conditional clauses. |
| **Active** | The subject performs the action. |

The **aorist optative active** thus expresses a wish or possibility concerning a **single** action: εἴθε νικήσαιμι "may I win!"

---

## 2. Forms and Patterns

### 2.1 The First (Sigmatic) Aorist Optative

From the **unaugmented aorist stem** + the optative marker **‑αι‑** + the endings:

| Person | Ending | Example with **λύω** "to loose" |
|--------|--------|-----------------------------------|
| 1 sg | **‑σαιμι** | **λύσαιμι** |
| 2 sg | **‑σαις** (or ‑σειας) | **λύσαις / λύσειας** |
| 3 sg | **‑σαι** (or ‑σειε(ν)) | **λύσαι / λύσειε(ν)** |
| 1 pl | **‑σαιμεν** | **λύσαιμεν** |
| 2 pl | **‑σαιτε** | **λύσαιτε** |
| 3 pl | **‑σαιεν** (or ‑σειαν) | **λύσαιεν / λύσειαν** |

> **Note:** the alternative forms of the 2nd/3rd singular and 3rd plural (‑ειας, ‑ειε(ν), ‑ειαν) — known as the "Aeolic forms" — are very common in Attic prose.

### 2.2 The Second Aorist Optative

Verbs with a second aorist take the **present** optative endings (‑οιμι…) on the **aorist stem**:

| Person | Example with **λαμβάνω** → stem **λαβ‑** |
|--------|-----------------------------------|
| 1 sg | **λάβοιμι** |
| 2 sg | **λάβοις** |
| 3 sg | **λάβοι** |
| 1 pl | **λάβοιμεν** |
| 2 pl | **λάβοιτε** |
| 3 pl | **λάβοιεν** |

*The only difference from the present optative is the stem: λαμβάνοιμι (present) vs. λάβοιμι (aorist).*

### 2.3 Three Points of Formation

1. **No augment**: ἔλυσα but λύσαιμι (not *ἐλύσαιμι*).
2. **‑αι‑ is the distinguishing mark**: whenever you see the sign αι after a σα-stem, it is an aorist optative; the aorist subjunctive has a long vowel instead (λύσω, λύσῃς).
3. **Contract verbs** lengthen the stem vowel before σ: τιμάω → **τιμήσαιμι**; ποιέω → **ποιήσαιμι**.

### 2.4 Irregular Forms

| Verb | Aorist stem | Optative (1st sg.) | Note |
|------|-------------|------------------------------|---------|
| ἔρχομαι "to come" | ἐλθ‑ | **ἔλθοιμι** | second aorist |
| λέγω "to say" | εἰπ‑ | **εἴποιμι** | second aorist |
| ὁράω "to see" | ἰδ‑ | **ἴδοιμι** | second aorist |
| εἰμί "to be" | — (no aorist) | εἴην (present optative) | special form |

## 3. Key Rules

| Rule | Explanation | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **1. Aorist stem, no augment** | The augment belongs to the indicative. | λύσαιμι, λάβοιμι |
| **2. The mood marker: ‑αι‑ / ‑οι‑** | First aorist ‑αι‑, second aorist ‑οι‑. | λύσαιμεν; λάβοιμεν |
| **3. Aspect, not time** | The aorist optative is a wish/possibility about a single action, not about the past. | εἴθε λάβοιμι "may I take" |
| **4. Do not confuse with the infinitive** | λῦσαι (infinitive) and λύσαι (3rd sg. optative) differ only in accent position! | λῦσαι ≠ λύσαι |
| **5. Learn the Aeolic forms** | ‑ειας, ‑ειε(ν), ‑ειαν are frequent in texts. | λύσειεν |

---

## 4. Example Sentences

| Greek (aorist optative active) | Translation | Use |
|----------------------------|-------------|---------|
| **εἴθε νικήσαιμι.** | May I win! | wish |
| **ἡδέως ἂν ἀκούσαιμι.** | I would gladly hear it. | potential with ἄν |
| **εἰ τοῦτο ποιήσειας, εὐδαίμων ἂν εἴης.** | If you should do this, you would be happy. | remote condition |
| **ἔλεξεν ὅτι ἴδοι τοὺς πολεμίους.** | He said that he had seen the enemy. | oblique optative after a past verb |
| **μὴ γένοιτο.** | May it not happen! | negative wish (with μή) |

---

## 5. Usage & Context

1. **Wish** — with εἴθε or εἰ γάρ (sometimes without a particle): εἴθε λύσειε τὸν δεσμόν "may he loose the bond." A wish is negated with **μή**.
2. **Potential (optative with ἄν)** — a cautious or polite assertion: λύσαιμι ἄν "I might loose it / I would be willing to."
3. **Remote condition** — "if this should ever happen…": εἰ + optative in the protasis, optative + ἄν in the apodosis.
4. **The oblique optative (optativus obliquus)** — after a past main verb, the moods of subordinate clauses may shift to the optative; its aorist preserves the priority of the event: ἔλεξεν ὅτι ἴδοι "he said that he had seen."
5. **Choosing the aspect** — present optative = action in progress; aorist optative = a single act: εἴθε γράφοις "may you write (continually)" vs. εἴθε γράψειας "may you write (this)."

---

## 6. Historical Note

The optative marker (‑ιη‑/‑ι‑) reached Greek from Proto-Indo-European; combined with the sigmatic stem it produced ‑σαι‑. The optative declined in Koine Greek — in the New Testament it scarcely appears outside idioms such as μὴ γένοιτο "God forbid!" — and it vanished entirely in Modern Greek; mastering it therefore matters above all for reading classical prose.
