# Introduction to Greek Nouns and Declensions

## 1. One Word, Many Forms

An Ancient Greek noun changes its ending to show what it is doing in the sentence. English mostly relies on word order ("the dog bites the man" vs. "the man bites the dog"); Greek instead marks the roles on the words themselves:

- ὁ ἄνθρωπος τὸν ἵππον ὁρᾷ — "the man sees the horse"
- τὸν ἄνθρωπον ὁ ἵππος ὁρᾷ — "the horse sees the man"

The endings (-ος for subject, -ον for object), not the position, tell you who sees whom. This is why Greek word order is so free — and why learning the endings is the heart of learning Greek.

Every noun form combines three pieces of information: **case**, **number**, and **gender**.

## 2. Case: the Noun's Job in the Sentence

| Case | Core function | English hint | Example |
|---|---|---|---|
| Nominative | subject | "the X" as doer | ὁ λόγος |
| Genitive | possession, source | "of X" | τοῦ λόγου |
| Dative | indirect object, instrument | "to/for/with X" | τῷ λόγῳ |
| Accusative | direct object | "X" as receiver | τὸν λόγον |
| Vocative | direct address | "O X!" | ὦ φίλε |

Each case has more uses than this table shows — prepositions, time expressions, and idioms all select particular cases — but these core functions cover most of what you will read at first.

## 3. Number

- **Singular** — one: ὁ λόγος "the word"
- **Plural** — more than one: οἱ λόγοι "the words"
- **Dual** — exactly two (rare; mostly poetry and fixed pairs like "both hands"): τὼ χεῖρε

## 4. Gender

Every noun is **masculine**, **feminine**, or **neuter** — a grammatical classification, not a biological one: ἡ ὁδός "road" is feminine, τὸ τέκνον "child" is neuter. Learn every noun together with its article (ὁ, ἡ, τό), which wears the gender on its sleeve.

## 5. Declensions: the Three Patterns

Nouns fall into three families of endings, called **declensions**:

| Declension | Typical look | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| First | -η / -α (mostly feminine; some masculines in -ης/-ας) | τιμή, χώρα, πολίτης |
| Second | -ος (mostly masculine), -ον (neuter) | λόγος, δῶρον |
| Third | consonant or ι/υ stems; gen. sg. -ος/-ως | φύλαξ, σῶμα, πόλις |

A noun's declension determines *which* set of endings it uses — but the *information* the endings carry (case and number) is the same across all three.

## 6. Anatomy of a Noun Form

**stem + ending**: λόγ- (the meaning "word") + -ου (genitive singular) = λόγου "of a word".

Dictionaries therefore list nouns with the nominative, the genitive, and the article: λόγος, -ου, ὁ. The genitive reveals both the declension and the stem — make it a habit to learn all three parts.

## Summary

- Greek nouns encode **case** (role), **number**, and **gender** in their endings.
- Word order is flexible because the endings, not the position, mark the grammar.
- Three declension patterns supply the endings; learn each noun as *nominative + genitive + article*.
