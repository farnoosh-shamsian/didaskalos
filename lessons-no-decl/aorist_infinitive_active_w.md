# Lesson: The Aorist Infinitive Active of **‑ω** Verbs

---

## 1. What Is the Aorist Infinitive Active?

The **aorist** expresses a **simple, complete action**, without reference to duration or repetition.

The **infinitive** is the non-finite form of the verb: it has no person or number and can function like a noun (subject, object, complement).

The aorist infinitive **active** means that the (logical) subject of the infinitive performs the action (as opposed to middle and passive).

The principal forms for ω-verbs:

- **First aorist (sigmatic): ‑σαι** → **λῦσαι** "to loose (once)"
- **Second aorist (thematic): ‑εῖν** → **λαβεῖν** "to take (once)"

> **Typical meaning:** "to do (once)." The fundamental point: the aorist infinitive **has no past time**; it conveys only the **aspect** of a single event. λῦσαι means "to loose (once and done)," not "to have loosed."

---

## 2. Forms and Patterns

### 2.1 The Basic Point

The infinitive has **only one form** — it takes neither person nor number. All you need is to know the aorist stem and add the right ending. **The augment never appears in the infinitive** (it belongs to the indicative alone): ἔλυσα but λῦσαι.

### 2.2 How to Form It

1. Find the **aorist stem** (from the verb's third principal part: ἔλυσα → λυσ‑; ἔλαβον → λαβ‑).
2. Remove the augment.
3. For the first aorist add **‑αι** (σα-stem → ‑σαι); for the second aorist add **‑εῖν**.

#### 2.2.1 First Aorist (Sigmatic)

| Verb (present) | Aorist stem | Aorist infinitive active |
|----------------|-------------|---------------------------|
| **λύω** "to loose" | λυσ‑ | **λῦσαι** |
| **γράφω** "to write" | γραψ‑ | **γράψαι** |
| **παιδεύω** "to educate" | παιδευσ‑ | **παιδεῦσαι** |
| **ἀκούω** "to hear" | ἀκουσ‑ | **ἀκοῦσαι** |
| **τιμάω** "to honor" | τιμησ‑ | **τιμῆσαι** |
| **ποιέω** "to do, make" | ποιησ‑ | **ποιῆσαι** |

*The accent of the first aorist infinitive falls on the penult: παιδεῦσαι.*

#### 2.2.2 Second Aorist (Thematic)

| Verb (present) | Aorist stem | Aorist infinitive active |
|----------------|-------------------|---------------------------|
| **λαμβάνω** "to take" | λαβ‑ | **λαβεῖν** |
| **ἔρχομαι** "to come" | ἐλθ‑ | **ἐλθεῖν** |
| **λέγω** "to say" | εἰπ‑ | **εἰπεῖν** |
| **ὁράω** "to see" | ἰδ‑ | **ἰδεῖν** |
| **ἔχω** "to have" | σχ‑ | **σχεῖν** |

*The second aorist infinitive always carries a circumflex on the final syllable: λαβεῖν — this distinguishes it from the present infinitive (λαμβάνειν).*

#### 2.2.3 Special Cases

| Verb | Situation | Correct form |
|----------------|-------------|---------------------------|
| **εἰμί** "to be" | Has no aorist | Only the present infinitive **εἶναι** |
| **γίγνομαι** "to become" | Has a middle aorist | **γενέσθαι** (middle; treated in the middle infinitive lesson) |
| **δίδωμι, τίθημι** | μι-aorists | **δοῦναι**, **θεῖναι** (treated in the μι-verb lessons) |

---

## 3. Key Rules & Exceptions

| Rule | Explanation | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **1. Build on the aorist stem** | The same stem as the aorist indicative, without the augment. | ἔγραψα → γραψ‑ → **γράψαι** |
| **2. First aorist: ‑σαι** | Sigmatic stem + ‑αι; accent on the penult. | **παιδεῦσαι** |
| **3. Second aorist: ‑εῖν** | Final circumflex — the mark that distinguishes it from the present infinitive. | **λαβεῖν** ≠ λαμβάνειν |
| **4. Contract verbs lengthen the vowel** | α/ε → η, ο → ω before σ. | τιμάω → **τιμῆσαι**; δηλόω → **δηλῶσαι** |
| **5. No augment** | The augment belongs only to the indicative. | Not *ἐλῦσαι*; correctly: λῦσαι |
| **6. εἰμί has no aorist infinitive** | Only εἶναι (present). | βούλομαι σοφὸς εἶναι |
| **7. Do not confuse with middle and passive** | Middle: **‑σασθαι** (λύσασθαι); passive: **‑θῆναι** (λυθῆναι). | Three forms, three meanings |
| **8. Consonant changes before σ** | Labial + σ → ψ; velar + σ → ξ. | γράφω → γράψαι; πράττω → πρᾶξαι |

## 4. Example Sentences

| Greek (aorist infinitive active) | Transliteration | English |
|----------------------------|----------------|---------------------|
| **ἐθέλω λῦσαι τὸν δεσμόν.** | ethélō lŷsai ton desmón | I want to loose the bond. |
| **ἐκέλευσε τοὺς στρατιώτας ἐλθεῖν.** | ekéleuse tous stratiṓtas eltheîn | He ordered the soldiers to come. |
| **ἔδει αὐτὸν εἰπεῖν τὴν ἀλήθειαν.** | édei autòn eipeîn tēn alḗtheian | He had to tell the truth. |
| **δεινὸς λέγειν, δεινὸς δὲ καὶ πρᾶξαι.** | deinòs légein, … prâxai | Skilled in speaking, and skilled in acting too. |
| **ἔφη τὸν στρατηγὸν νικῆσαι.** | éphē ton stratēgòn nikêsai | He said that the general had won. |

*In the last sentence, the aorist infinitive in indirect statement marks an event **prior to** the verb of saying.*

---

## 5. Usage & Context

1. **After verbs of wishing, being able, commanding** — βούλομαι, ἐθέλω, δύναμαι, κελεύω + infinitive: ἐκέλευσε λῦσαι "he ordered (him) to loose."
2. **Indirect statement (accusative + infinitive)** — after verbs of saying and thinking, the aorist infinitive conveys a prior event: ἔφη αὐτὸν λῦσαι "he said that he (had) loosed."
3. **Articular infinitive** — τὸ λῦσαι "the (single) act of loosing" as a noun: πρὸ τοῦ λῦσαι "before loosing."
4. **Result clauses with ὥστε** — ὥστε λαβεῖν τὴν πόλιν "so as to take the city."
5. **πρίν + infinitive** — πρὶν ἐλθεῖν τοὺς πολεμίους "before the enemy came."
6. **Aspectual contrast with the present infinitive** — present = ongoing/repeated; aorist = single: ἐκέλευε γράφειν "he kept ordering (him) to write" vs. ἐκέλευσε γράψαι "he ordered (him) to write (this)." The infinitive is always negated with **μή**.

---

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology

- The ending **‑σαι** arose from the sigmatic marker ‑σ(α)‑ combined with the ancient infinitive ending *‑ai.
- The second aorist ending **‑εῖν** developed from the contraction of the thematic vowel with old *‑εν — the same ending seen in the present infinitive (‑ειν); the difference lies only in stem and accent.
- Infinitives were originally **verbal nouns** fossilized in locative/dative case forms; that is why they behave like nouns with the article (τὸ λῦσαι).
- In Koine Greek the infinitive gradually yielded to ὅτι/ἵνα clauses, and in Modern Greek it has disappeared entirely — its legacy taken over by να + subjunctive constructions.
