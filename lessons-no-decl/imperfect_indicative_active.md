### Introduction to ω-Verbs: Imperfect, Indicative, Active
The Greek language features a complex system of verb conjugation, and ω-verbs form a large part of it. In this lesson we explore the **imperfect** tense of ω-verbs in the indicative mood and active voice. Understanding the imperfect is essential for reading Greek texts accurately.

### Explanation of the Grammatical Concept
The imperfect describes actions that were **ongoing or repeated** in the past: "I was loosing," "he used to write (every day)." It is also used for an action in progress when another action occurred. The choice between the imperfect and the aorist (a single completed action) is a matter of **aspect** and depends on the speaker's point of view.

### Forms and Patterns
The imperfect is built on the **present stem**: **augment + present stem + thematic vowel + secondary endings**. The present stem is obtained by removing the ‑ω from the first person singular of the present.

The conjugation of the regular verb λύω, "to loose," in the imperfect indicative active:

| Person | Singular | Plural |
| --- | --- | --- |
| 1st | ἔλυον | ἐλύομεν |
| 2nd | ἔλυες | ἐλύετε |
| 3rd | ἔλυε(ν) | ἔλυον |

Another example, the verb ἀγοράζω, "to buy" — since it begins with a vowel, the augment lengthens the initial vowel (ἀ → ἠ):

| Verb | Present stem | Impf. 1 sg. | Impf. 2 sg. | Impf. 3 sg. | Impf. 1 pl. | Impf. 2 pl. | Impf. 3 pl. |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| ἀγοράζω | ἀγοραζ‑ | ἠγόραζον | ἠγόραζες | ἠγόραζε(ν) | ἠγοράζομεν | ἠγοράζετε | ἠγόραζον |

### Key Rules and Exceptions
- The endings of the imperfect active: **‑ον, ‑ες, ‑ε(ν), ‑ομεν, ‑ετε, ‑ον** (secondary endings with the thematic vowel).
- **The augment is obligatory**: consonant-initial verbs take the syllabic augment ἐ‑ (λύω → ἔλυον); vowel-initial verbs lengthen the initial vowel (ἀκούω → ἤκουον, ἀγοράζω → ἠγόραζον).
- **The 1st singular and 3rd plural are identical** (ἔλυον); context decides.
- Contract verbs merge the stem vowel with the thematic vowel: ποιέω → ἐποίουν; τιμάω → ἐτίμων.
- εἰμί has an irregular imperfect: ἦν "was" (ἦ/ἦν, ἦσθα, ἦν, ἦμεν, ἦτε, ἦσαν).

### Example Sentences with Translations
1. **ἔλυον τὴν σφραγῖδα.** — I was loosing the seal.
2. **οἱ ναῦται ἔλυον τὰς ἀγκύρας.** — The sailors were loosing the anchors.
3. **ἤλγουν τὸν πόδα.** — My foot was hurting me.
4. **σὺ ἔγραφες τὴν ἐπιστολήν.** — You were writing the letter.

### Notes on Usage and Context
The imperfect narrates ongoing or repeated past actions and is used to **set the scene and provide background**: the imperfect paints the setting, while the aorist advances the events. It also expresses **attempted** action (conative: ἔπειθον "I was trying to persuade") and **beginning** action (inchoative) — nuances well worth capturing in translation.

### Historical Development and Etymology
The Greek imperfect is rooted in Proto-Indo-European: the combination of the past-tense augment (*e‑) with the present stem and the secondary endings — the same formation seen in Sanskrit. Because it is built on the present stem, the imperfect is the "past partner" of the present: everything you know about the present system (contraction, stem irregularities) applies here as well. Recognizing this connection makes the imperfect much easier to learn.
