# Introduction to Greek Verbs

## 1. One Word, a Whole Sentence

A single Greek verb form packs in everything English needs several words for:

- παιδεύομεν = "we are educating" (who: *we*; when/how: *ongoing present*; voice: *active*)
- ἐπαιδεύθης = "you were educated" (*you*, *past, single event*, *passive*)

Every finite verb form encodes five things: **person**, **number**, **tense/aspect**, **voice**, and **mood**.

## 2. The Five Components

### Person and Number

| | Singular | Plural |
|---|---|---|
| 1st | I | we |
| 2nd | you (sg.) | you (pl.) |
| 3rd | he/she/it | they |

Because the ending shows the person, subject pronouns are usually omitted: λέγω by itself means "I speak." (There is also a rare dual for pairs.)

### Tense — Really Aspect

Greek "tenses" say less about *when* than about *how the action unfolds* (its **aspect**):

| Tense | Aspect + time | English feel |
|---|---|---|
| Present | ongoing, now | "I am loosening" |
| Imperfect | ongoing, past | "I was loosening" |
| Future | later | "I will loosen" |
| Aorist | single event, past (in the indicative) | "I loosened" |
| Perfect | completed, with present result | "I have loosened" |
| Pluperfect | completed, with past result | "I had loosened" |

Outside the indicative mood (e.g., in infinitives and commands), aorist vs. present marks *only* aspect — single event vs. ongoing — not past time. This idea takes getting used to, and the lessons will return to it often.

### Voice

- **Active**: the subject acts — λύω "I loosen"
- **Middle**: the subject acts on/for itself or with self-involvement — λύομαι "I ransom (get someone freed for myself)"
- **Passive**: the subject is acted upon — λύομαι "I am being loosened"

Middle and passive share their forms in most tenses; context decides. Some verbs — called **deponents** — have only middle/passive forms but active meanings: ἔρχομαι "I come", βούλομαι "I want".

### Mood

| Mood | Signals | Example |
|---|---|---|
| Indicative | statement of fact | λύει "he loosens" |
| Subjunctive | possibility, purpose, exhortation | ἵνα λύῃ "so that he may loosen" |
| Optative | wish, remote possibility | λύοι "may he loosen!" |
| Imperative | command | λῦε "loosen!" |

Two further verb forms behave like nouns and adjectives: the **infinitive** (λύειν "to loosen") and the **participle** (λύων "loosening"), both of which Greek uses far more than English does.

## 3. Anatomy of a Verb Form

A typical form assembles like this:

**(augment) + stem + tense marker + ending**

ἐ-παίδευ-σα-μεν "we educated": augment ἐ- (past time), stem παιδευ-, aorist marker -σα-, ending -μεν (1st plural).

## 4. Principal Parts

Because stems change across tenses, dictionaries list **six principal parts** for each verb, from which every form can be built. For παιδεύω "educate":

1. παιδεύω (present)
2. παιδεύσω (future)
3. ἐπαίδευσα (aorist active)
4. πεπαίδευκα (perfect active)
5. πεπαίδευμαι (perfect middle/passive)
6. ἐπαιδεύθην (aorist passive)

Regular verbs like this are predictable; frequent irregular verbs must be memorized — and the frequency-based lessons in this book prioritize exactly those.

## Summary

- One Greek verb form = person + number + tense/aspect + voice + mood.
- Aspect (ongoing vs. single event vs. completed-with-result) matters more than time.
- Learn verbs by their principal parts; the endings then follow regular patterns.
