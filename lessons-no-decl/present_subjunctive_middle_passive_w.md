# Lesson: The Present Subjunctive Middle/Passive of ω-Verbs

*Target level: Intermediate*
*Prerequisites: Present subjunctive active, present indicative middle/passive.*

---

## 1. What This Form Is

The **subjunctive** is the mood of what is possible, intended, or indefinite — purpose, probable condition, exhortation. The **present** stem gives it imperfective aspect (ongoing or repeated). The **middle/passive** endings mean the subject either acts with reference to itself or is acted upon.

As always in the present system, **one set of forms serves both middle and passive**; only context tells you which:

- φοβῶμαι (middle) — "let me fear"
- λύηται (passive) — "that it be loosed"

The mark of the subjunctive is the same as in the active: the thematic vowel **lengthens**, ο/ε → **ω/η**.

| Indicative | Subjunctive |
|---|---|
| λύεται "he is loosed" | **λύηται** "that he be loosed" |
| λυόμεθα | **λυώμεθα** |

---

## 2. Forms and Patterns

### 2.1 Regular Thematic Verbs — **λύω**

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|---|---|---|
| 1st | **λύωμαι** | **λυώμεθα** |
| 2nd | **λύῃ** | **λύησθε** |
| 3rd | **λύηται** | **λύωνται** |

> **The 2nd singular λύῃ** comes from λύη‑σαι with loss of σ. It is spelled exactly like the 3rd singular *active* subjunctive λύῃ and like the 2nd singular indicative middle λύῃ — three forms, one spelling. Context is your only guide.

### 2.2 Contract Verbs — **φιλέω**

The stem vowel contracts with the long vowel of the ending:

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|---|---|---|
| 1st | **φιλῶμαι** | **φιλώμεθα** |
| 2nd | **φιλῇ** | **φιλῆσθε** |
| 3rd | **φιλῆται** | **φιλῶνται** |

α‑contracts (τιμάω): τιμῶμαι, τιμᾷ, τιμᾶται, τιμώμεθα, τιμᾶσθε, τιμῶνται.

### 2.3 Deponents

Deponent verbs use exactly these endings with active meaning:

| Verb | 1 sg | 1 pl |
|---|---|---|
| βούλομαι "want" | **βούλωμαι** | **βουλώμεθα** |
| μάχομαι "fight" | **μάχωμαι** | **μαχώμεθα** |
| φοβέομαι "fear" | **φοβῶμαι** | **φοβώμεθα** |
| γίγνομαι "become" | **γίγνωμαι** | **γιγνώμεθα** |

## 3. Key Rules & Common Exceptions

| Rule | Explanation | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Long vowel, no augment** | ω/η throughout; the subjunctive never takes the augment. | λυώμεθα |
| **2. Middle and passive share one form** | Only aorist and future separate them. | λύηται = "let him ransom" or "let him be loosed" |
| **3. 2 sg ‑ῃ from ‑η‑σαι** | The σ drops between vowels. | λύῃ |
| **4. Iota subscript is written** | λύῃ, λύηται — a reliable visual cue. | |
| **5. Contract verbs contract as usual** | ε + ω → ω, ε + η → η with circumflex. | φιλῆται |
| **6. Beware the triple ambiguity of ‑ῃ** | Indicative middle 2 sg, subjunctive middle 2 sg, and subjunctive active 3 sg all appear as ‑ῃ. | |

---

## 4. Example Sentences

| Greek | Translation |
|---|---|
| μὴ **φοβώμεθα** τοὺς πολεμίους. | "Let us not fear the enemy." (hortatory) |
| ἐὰν οἱ σύμμαχοι **μάχωνται**, νικήσομεν. | "If the allies fight, we shall win." |
| ἦλθον ἵνα οἱ αἰχμάλωτοι **λύωνται**. | "They came so that the prisoners might be released." |
| ὅταν ἡ πόλις **τειχίζηται**, ἀσφαλὴς ἔσται. | "Whenever the city is being fortified, it will be safe." |
| τί **βουλώμεθα** ποιεῖν; | "What are we to want to do?" (deliberative) |

## 5. Usage & Context

1. **Purpose clauses** — ἵνα, ὅπως, ὡς + subjunctive after a primary tense.
2. **Future more vivid conditions** — ἐάν + subjunctive: ἐὰν μάχωνται …
3. **Indefinite clauses** — ὅταν, ὃς ἄν, ἐπειδὰν + subjunctive for repeated or unspecified occasions.
4. **Hortatory** — 1st person plural, especially with deponents: μὴ φοβώμεθα, πειθώμεθα τοῖς νόμοις.
5. **Clauses of fearing** — φοβοῦμαι μὴ γίγνηται "I fear that it is happening."
6. **Aspect** — present subjunctive = ongoing or repeated; aorist subjunctive = a single act. The choice is aspectual, never temporal.

---

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology

- The subjunctive's long thematic vowel continues the Indo-European subjunctive formation directly; Greek is unusually conservative here.
- The middle endings **‑μαι, ‑σαι, ‑ται** are the primary middle set, shared with the indicative; the subjunctive is marked purely by the vowel that precedes them.
- The loss of σ in ‑η‑σαι → ‑ῃ is part of a wider Greek tendency to drop intervocalic σ, the same change that produced indicative λύῃ from λύε‑σαι.
- In Koine the subjunctive absorbed the functions of the dying optative, and the ἵνα + subjunctive construction eventually replaced the infinitive — the ancestor of Modern Greek να.
