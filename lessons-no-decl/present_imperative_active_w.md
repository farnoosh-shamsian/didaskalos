# Lesson: The Present‑Active Imperative (and the Present‑Active Indicative ‑ ω)

**Goal:**  By the end of this lesson you will be able to recognise, form, and translate the present‑active imperative in Ancient Greek, and you will understand how the characteristic ending **‑ω** of the present‑active indicative relates to the formation of the imperative.

---

## 1. What is the Present‑Active Imperative?

| Term | Function in a sentence | Typical English equivalent |
|------|------------------------|----------------------------|
| **Present‑Active** | Action happening **now** (or habitually) and performed **by the subject**. | “you write”, “let him speak”. |
| **Imperative Mood** | A **command, request, or exhortation** directed to the addressee(s). | “Write!”, “Let us speak!”. |

The present‑active imperative therefore expresses a **present‑time command** (or a polite request) performed by the person(s) addressed.

> **Important:** The imperative **does not have a first‑person form**. It is used only for the **second** and **third** persons (singular and plural). The “‑ω” you see in the title belongs to the **present‑active indicative** (1st‑person singular) and is the **stem‑ending** from which the imperative endings are built.

---

## 2. Forms and Patterns

### 2.1. The Basic Stem

1. **Take the present‑active stem** (the part of the verb that appears before the personal endings).  
   - For a regular **‑ω** verb like **λύ‑** (to loosen), the stem is **λυ‑**.  
   - For a **contract verb** ending in **‑άω, ‑έω, ‑όω**, the stem ends in a vowel that contracts with the ending (e.g., **τιμά‑** from **τιμάω**).

2. **Add the appropriate imperative endings** (see tables below).

### 2.2. Imperative Endings (Present‑Active)

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|--------|----------|--------|
| **2nd** | **‑ε** (or **‑ετω** for contract verbs) | **‑ετε** |
| **3rd** | **‑ετω** | **‑ετωσαν** |

> **Why no 1st‑person?** The imperative is a command *to* someone else, so Greek never uses a “we‑command” form. Instead a **hortatory** (let us …) is expressed with the **subjunctive** (e.g., **γράψωμεν** “let us write”).

### 2.3. Full Paradigm for a Regular Verb – **λύω** “to loosen”

|               | **Indicative** (present‑active) | **Imperative** (present‑active) |
|---------------|--------------------------------|---------------------------------|
| **1st sg**    | λύ**ω**                         | — (no form)                     |
| **2nd sg**    | λύ**εις**                      | **λύε** (or **λύετω** for a more formal tone) |
| **3rd sg**    | λύ**ει**                       | **λύετω**                       |
| **1st pl**    | λύ**ομεν**                     | — (no form)                     |
| **2nd pl**    | λύ**ετε**                      | **λύετε**                       |
| **3rd pl**    | λύ**ουσι(ν)**                  | **λύετωσαν**                    |

> The **‑ω** in the 1st‑person indicative (λύ**ω**) is the “base” from which the stem **λυ‑** is derived. The imperative endings are attached directly to this stem.

### 2.4. Contract Verbs (‑άω, ‑έω, ‑όω)

| Verb | Meaning | Stem | 2nd sg Imperative | 3rd sg Imperative |
|------|---------|------|-------------------|-------------------|
| **τιμάω** | “to honour” | τιμα‑ | **τιμᾶ** (or **τιμᾶτε** for plural) | **τιμᾶτω** |
| **ποιέω** | “to make” | ποιε‑ | **ποίησον** (aorist) – *but present*: **ποίη** | **ποιείτω** |
| **δείκνυμι** (irregular) – see note below | “to show” | δεικνυ‑ | **δείξον** (aorist) – *present*: **δείξ** (no regular present‑imperative) | — |

*Contract verbs drop the vowel of the stem when the ending begins with a vowel, producing a short‑vowel contraction (‑α, ‑ε, ‑ο).*

## 3. Key Rules & Exceptions

| Rule | Explanation | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **1. Use the stem, not the 1st‑person form** | The imperative is built on the present‑active stem (the part before **‑ω**). | λύ**ω** → stem **λυ‑** → **λύε!** |
| **2. 2nd sg ending –ε** | For regular verbs the ending is **‑ε**. For contract verbs the ending is **‑α, ‑ε, ‑ο** (the contracted vowel). | γράφ**ε**! “write!”; τιμ**ᾶ**! “honour!” |
| **3. 3rd sg ending –ετω** | Adds a **‑τω** to the stem; used for a more formal or polite command, or in poetry. | λυ**ετω** “let him loosen”. |
| **4. Plural 2nd sg –ετε** | Same as the indicative 2nd pl, but the meaning is imperative. | λυ**ετε** “you (all) loosen!”. |
| **5. Plural 3rd sg –ετωσαν** | Rare, mostly in poetry or formal exhortations. | λυ**ετωσαν** “let them loosen”. |
| **6. No 1st‑person imperative** | Use the **subjunctive** for “let us …”. | γράψ**ωμεν** “let us write”. |
| **7. Verbs with a **‑σ** in the stem** | The **‑σ** of the present‑active indicative disappears in the imperative. | **πίνω** → stem **πι‑** → **πίνε!** (not *πίνσε*). |
| **8. Irregular verbs** | Some verbs lack a regular present‑imperative (e.g., **εἰμί**, **ἔχω**). They use special forms: **ἔστω** “let it be”, **ἔχε** “have!”. | — |
| **9. Strong vs. weak verbs** | Verbs whose stems end in a vowel may show **vowel lengthening** in the imperative (e.g., **ἔρχομαι** → **ἔρχε**). | — |

## 4. Example Sentences

| Greek (imperative) | Gloss | English Translation |
|--------------------|-------|---------------------|
| **Λύε τὸν δεσμὸν!** | (2nd sg) | “Loosen the bond!” |
| **Τιμᾶτε τοὺς παῖδας.** | (2nd pl) | “Honor the children.” |
| **Γράφε τὸν λόγον.** | (2nd sg) | “Write the word.” |
| **Ἐλθάτω ὁ ἥρως.** | (3rd sg) | “Let the hero come.” |
| **Ἀκούετε τὴν διδασκαλίαν.** | (2nd pl) | “Listen to the teaching.” |
| **Ἀσπασάσθω ὁ διδάσκαλος.** | (3rd sg, middle‑voice) – shows that the same endings apply to middle/passive forms. | “Let the teacher be greeted.” |

## 5. Notes on Usage & Context

1. **Politeness vs. Force** – The plain **‑ε / ‑ετε** forms are direct commands (often used in military or urgent contexts). The **‑ετω / ‑ετωσαν** forms sound more **formal, polite, or poetic**.
2. **Prose vs. Poetry** – In everyday prose the 2nd sg **‑ε** is most common. In epic poetry you will encounter the 3rd sg **‑ετω** and the 3rd pl **‑ετωσαν**.
3. **Negative Commands** – Use **μὴ** + infinitive for present‑active imperatives (e.g., **μὴ λῦς** “don’t loosen”). For aorist imperatives use **οὐ** + aorist infinitive.
4. **Crasis & Elision** – When the imperative is followed by a word beginning with a vowel, Greek often contracts (crasis) or elides the final vowel: **Λύε ἄνθρωπον** → **Λύε’ ἄνθρωπον**.
5. **Hortatory Subjunctive** – To say “let us …” use the present‑active **subjunctive** (e.g., **γράψωμεν**), not the imperative.
