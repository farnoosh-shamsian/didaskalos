# Lesson: The Present Imperative Active (‑ω Verbs)

**Aim:** by the end of this lesson you will be able to recognise, form, and translate the present active imperative in Ancient Greek, and you will know how the characteristic **‑ω** ending of the present indicative relates to building the imperative.

---

## 1. What the Present Active Imperative Is

| Term | Function in the sentence | Rough English equivalent |
|------|--------------------------|--------------------------|
| **Present active** | An action going on **now** (or habitually), performed by the **subject**. | "you write," "he speaks." |
| **Imperative mood** | A **command, request, or exhortation** addressed to someone. | "write!", "let him speak!" |

The present imperative is therefore a command with **imperfective aspect**: "keep writing / always write" — as against the aorist imperative, which commands a single act.

> **Important:** the imperative has **no first person.** It is used only in the **second** and **third** persons (singular and plural). The "‑ω" in the title is the 1st singular ending of the present indicative, and merely shows which verb the imperative stem is built from.

---

## 2. Forms and Patterns

### 2.1 The Base Stem

1. Take the **present active stem** (the part before the endings).
   - For a regular ‑ω verb such as **λύω** "to loose," the stem is **λυ‑**.
   - For a **contract verb** in ‑άω, ‑έω, ‑όω, the final stem vowel contracts with the ending.

2. **Add the appropriate imperative endings** (tables below).

### 2.2 Endings of the Present Active Imperative

| Person | Singular | Plural |
|--------|----------|--------|
| **2nd** | **‑ε** | **‑ετε** |
| **3rd** | **‑έτω** | **‑όντων** (classical) / **‑έτωσαν** (later) |

> **Why is there no first person?** A command is always addressed to someone else. For "let us …" Greek uses the **hortatory subjunctive**: **γράφωμεν** "let us write."

### 2.3 Full Paradigm of a Regular Verb — **λύω** "to loose"

| | **Indicative** (present active) | **Imperative** (present active) |
|--------------|--------------------------------|---------------------------------|
| **1st sg.** | λύω | — (no form) |
| **2nd sg.** | λύεις | **λῦε** "loose!" |
| **3rd sg.** | λύει | **λυέτω** "let him loose" |
| **1st pl.** | λύομεν | — (no form) |
| **2nd pl.** | λύετε | **λύετε** "loose!" |
| **3rd pl.** | λύουσι(ν) | **λυόντων / λυέτωσαν** "let them loose" |

> Note: the **2nd plural imperative is identical to the indicative** (λύετε) — context determines the meaning.

### 2.4 Contract Verbs (‑άω, ‑έω, ‑όω)

| Verb | Meaning | 2nd sg. | 3rd sg. | 2nd pl. |
|------|---------|---------|---------|---------|
| **τιμάω** | to honour | **τίμα** | **τιμάτω** | **τιμᾶτε** |
| **ποιέω** | to do, make | **ποίει** | **ποιείτω** | **ποιεῖτε** |
| **δηλόω** | to make clear | **δήλου** | **δηλούτω** | **δηλοῦτε** |

*The stem vowel contracts with the ending: τίμα from τίμα‑ε, ποίει from ποίε‑ε, δήλου from δήλο‑ε.*

## 3. Key Rules & Exceptions

| Rule | Explanation | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **1. Build from the stem, not the 1st sg. form** | The imperative is built on the present stem (the part before ‑ω). | λύω → stem λυ‑ → **λῦε!** |
| **2. 2nd singular in ‑ε** | Regular verbs take ‑ε; contract verbs show the contracted vowel. | γράφε! "write!"; τίμα! "honour!" |
| **3. 3rd person in ‑έτω** | This is the third-person command: "let him …". | λυέτω "let him loose" |
| **4. 2nd plural in ‑ετε** | Identical to the indicative; the sense comes from context. | λύετε "loose!" |
| **5. 3rd plural** | Classical Attic ‑όντων; later texts use ‑έτωσαν. | λυόντων / λυέτωσαν |
| **6. No first person** | For "let us …" use the hortatory subjunctive. | γράφωμεν "let us write" |
| **7. Negative commands with μή** | A prohibition is never formed with οὐ. | **μὴ λῦε** "do not loose / stop loosing" |
| **8. Irregular verbs** | εἰμί has special forms: **ἴσθι** "be!", **ἔστω** "let him be!", **ἔστε**, **ἔστων**. ἔχω is regular: **ἔχε** "have!". | — |

## 5. Notes on Usage & Context

1. **Aspect, not time** — the present imperative commands a continuous or repeated action ("always tell the truth"); the aorist imperative commands a single act ("say this"). You will meet this contrast in the aorist imperative lesson.
2. **Prohibitions** — **μή + present imperative** usually means "stop …ing / do not make a habit of …": μὴ κλέπτε "do not steal." For a single prohibition Greek uses **μή + aorist subjunctive** (μὴ κλέψῃς).
3. **Prose and verse** — the second person is common everywhere; third-person forms abound in laws, prayers, and stage directions: ἔστω "so be it."
4. **Politeness and tone** — Greek softens a command with particles (δή, νυν) or with the optative plus ἄν; the imperative itself is not inherently harsh.
5. **Collective exhortation** — "let us …" uses the hortatory subjunctive: γράφωμεν "let us write," μὴ φοβώμεθα "let us not be afraid."

---

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology

- **Indo-European roots** — the 2nd singular imperative was originally the **bare stem** (Indo-European *‑e); Greek λῦε is exactly this ancient formation (compare Sanskrit *bhára* "carry!", Latin *age* "do!").
- **The ending ‑τω** — built from the deictic element *‑tōd, also visible in Old Latin (*estōd* "let it be" = Greek ἔστω).
- **‑έτωσαν** — a later formation that replaced classical ‑όντων in Koine (including the New Testament).
- **Durability** — the imperative is one of the few moods to survive into Modern Greek (γράψε "write!").
