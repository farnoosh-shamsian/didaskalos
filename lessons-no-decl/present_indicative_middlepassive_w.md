# Lesson: The Present‑Indicative Middle/Passive (‑ω) in Ancient Greek  

*Target level: Beginner → Intermediate*  
*Prerequisites: Greek alphabet, basic idea of verb stems, the concept of voice (active vs. middle vs. passive).*

---

## 1. What the Present‑Indicative ‑ω Means  

| Term | Meaning in English | Greek grammatical function |
|------|-------------------|-----------------------------|
| **Present** | Action happening now, a habitual action, or a general truth. | Tense = present. |
| **Indicative** | The “statement” mood – used for ordinary factual statements. | Mood = indicative. |
| **Middle/Passive** | The verb form that either (a) shows the subject acting **for its own benefit** (middle) or (b) shows the subject **being acted upon** (passive). In the present indicative these two voices share the same endings **‑ω** (and its variants). | Voice = middle / passive. |
| **‑ω** | The characteristic ending of the present‑indicative middle/passive for **first‑person singular**. All other persons end in **‑εις, ‑εσθαι, ‑εται, ‑εσθε, ‑ονται** etc., but the stem‑ending **‑ω** is the anchor of the paradigm. | Ending for 1st‑sg. |

Thus, **‑ω** marks a verb that is **present‑tense, indicative mood, and either middle or passive voice**. The same set of endings is used for both middle and passive; the distinction is understood from the verb’s meaning or from context.

---

## 2. The Regular Paradigm  

### 2.1. Template for a Regular **‑ω** Verb  

Take a regular **contract verb** in **‑εω** (e.g., *φιλέω* “to love”) or a **‑ομαι** verb (e.g., *παίω* “to strike”). The present‑indicative middle/passive is built on the **present stem** plus the endings below.

| Person | Active ending (for comparison) | **Middle/Passive ending** | Example with *φιλέω* (to love) |
|--------|--------------------------------|---------------------------|---------------------------------|
| 1st sg. | ‑ω | **‑ω** | **φιλέω** – “I love (for myself / am loved)” |
| 2nd sg. | ‑εις | **‑εσαι** | **φιλεῖσαι** – “you love (for yourself / are loved)” |
| 3rd sg. | ‑ει | **‑εται** | **φιλεῖται** – “he/she/it loves (for himself/herself / is loved)” |
| 1st pl. | ‑ομεν | **‑ομεν** | **φιλοῦμεν** – “we love (for ourselves / are loved)” |
| 2nd pl. | ‑ετε | **‑εσθε** | **φιλεῖσθε** – “you (pl.) love (for yourselves / are loved)” |
| 3rd pl. | ‑ουσι(ν) | **‑ονται** | **φιλοῦνται** – “they love (for themselves / are loved)” |

> **Note:** The middle/passive endings are the same for both voices; the meaning decides which voice is intended.

### 2.2. Full Conjugation Table (Regular **‑εω** Verb)  

|          | **Active** (for reference) | **Middle/Passive (‑ω)** |
|----------|----------------------------|--------------------------|
| **1 sg.** | λύω “I loose” | **λύω** – “I loose (for myself) / I am loosed” |
| **2 sg.** | λῦς | **λῦ͂** – “you loose (for yourself) / you are loosed” |
| **3 sg.** | λῦει | **λῦεται** – “he/she/it looses (for himself) / is loosed” |
| **1 pl.** | λῦομεν | **λῦομεν** – “we loose (for ourselves) / we are loosed” |
| **2 pl.** | λῦετε | **λῦεσθε** – “you (pl.) loose (for yourselves) / are loosed” |
| **3 pl.** | λῦουσι(ν) | **λῦονται** – “they loose (for themselves) / are loosed” |

*(The active column is shown only to illustrate that the middle/passive endings differ.)*

### 2.3. Conjugation of a **‑ομαι** Verb (e.g., *παίω* “to strike”)  

| Person | Stem + Ending | Form | Translation |
|--------|---------------|------|-------------|
| 1 sg. | παι‑ + ‑ω | **παίω** | “I strike (for myself) / I am struck” |
| 2 sg. | παι‑ + ‑εσαι | **παῖσῃ** | “you strike (for yourself) / you are struck” |
| 3 sg. | παι‑ + ‑εται | **παῖται** | “he/she/it strikes (for himself) / is struck” |
| 1 pl. | παι‑ + ‑ομεν | **παῖμεν** | “we strike (for ourselves) / we are struck” |
| 2 pl. | παι‑ + ‑εσθε | **παῖσθε** | “you (pl.) strike (for yourselves) / are struck” |
| 3 pl. | παι‑ + ‑ονται | **παῖνται** | “they strike (for themselves) / are struck” |

## 3. Key Rules & Common Exceptions  

| Rule | Explanation | Example / Note |
|------|-------------|----------------|
| **3.1. Contract verbs** | Verbs whose stem ends in **‑ε** or **‑ο** contract with the endings. <br>• **‑ε + ‑ω → ‑ω** (long vowel) <br>• **‑ε + ‑ει → ‑ει** (spurious diphthong) <br>• **‑ο + ‑ει → ‑οι** (rare) | *φιλέω → φιλεῖται* (ε+ει → ει) |
| **3.2. Lengthening before a vowel** | When the stem ends in **‑ε** and the ending begins with **‑ε**, the **ε** is lengthened to **η** (or **ει**). | *ποιέω → ποιεῖται* (ε+ει → ει) |
| **3.3. Verbs with a **‑σ** in the stem** | The **‑σ** is retained in the middle/passive forms (unlike the active where it often drops). | *διδάσκομαι → διδάσκομαι* (no change) |
| **3.4. Irregular middle/passive stems** | Some verbs have a different present middle/passive stem from the active. <br>• *ἔρχομαι* (to come) → stem **ἔρχο‑** (active) vs. **ἔρχο‑** (middle) – same here. <br>• *γίγνομαι* (to become) has stem **γίγ‑**; middle/passive forms are **γίγνομαι, γίνῃ, γίγνεται…** | *γίγνομαι* → **γίγνεται** “he/she/it becomes” |
| **3.5. Verbs that are **deponent** | Deponent verbs have **no active forms**; they exist only in middle/passive. Their present indicative uses the **‑ω** endings. | *ἔρχομαι* “I come” (active meaning, but only middle/passive forms). |
| **3.6. The “‑σθαι” infinitive** | The present infinitive of the middle/passive is **‑σθαι** (e.g., *φιλεῖσθαι* “to love (for oneself) / to be loved”). | *φιλεῖσθαι* – “to love (middle) / to be loved (passive)”. |
| **3.7. The “‑μαι” ending** | In many **‑ομαι** verbs the **‑ο** contracts with **‑μαι** → **‑μαι** (no change). In **‑εω** verbs the middle/passive 1sg ends in **‑ω** (no **‑μαι**). | *παίω* → **παίω** (1sg). |
| **3.8. Accent placement** | In the present middle/passive the accent normally falls on the **penult** if it is long; otherwise on the **ultima**. <br>• *φιλεῖται* (penult long → accent on penult). <br>• *παῖται* (penult short → accent on ultima). | Follow the usual accent rules for present tense. |

## 4. Example Sentences  

| Greek (middle) | Greek (passive) | Translation |
|----------------|----------------|-------------|
| **Ἀσπάζομαι τὸν φίλον.** | **Ἀσπάζομαι** can be interpreted as middle “I greet (myself) the friend” → “I greet my friend.” | “I greet my friend.” |
| **Ὁ ἀνήρ ἀσπάζεται.** | **ἀσπάζεται** (passive) – “The man is greeted.” | “The man is being greeted.” |
| **Ἐγὼ φιλοῦμαι τὸν διδάσκαλον.** | **φιλοῦμαι** (middle) – “I love the teacher (for my own benefit).” | “I love the teacher.” |
| **Ὁ διδάσκαλος φιλεῖται ὑπὸ τῶν μαθητῶν.** | **φιλεῖται** (passive) – “The teacher is loved by the students.” | “The teacher is loved by the students.” |
| **Παίομεν τὰ ὅπλα.** | **παίομεν** (middle) – “We strike (for ourselves) the weapons.” | “We strike the weapons.” |
| **Τὰ ὅπλα παίονται ὑπὸ τῶν ἀνδρῶν.** | **παίονται** (passive) – “The weapons are struck by the men.” | “The weapons are being struck by the men.” |

*Notice how the same form can be interpreted as middle or passive depending on context.*

## 5. Usage & Context  

1. **Middle voice** often expresses:  
   - **Self‑benefit or self‑interest** (*ἐγὼ λούομαι* “I wash myself”).  
   - **Reciprocal actions** (*ἀγγέλλομαι* “I announce to each other”).  
   - **Reflexive or internal actions** (*συγγίγνεται* “it happens to me”).  

2. **Passive voice** expresses:  
   - **Being acted upon** (*ὁ ἥλιος φαίνεται* “the sun is shown/appears”).  
   - **Resultant states** (*ἡ πόλις ἀνέγεται* “the city is built”).  

3. **Literary style**:  
   - In **prose**, the middle is more common for verbs of personal interest.  
   - In **poetry**, the passive may be used for dramatic effect or to fit meter.  

4. **Verb classes**:  
   - **Deponent verbs** (e.g., *ἔρχομαι, ἀποκρίνομαι*) appear only in middle/passive forms, yet they convey an active meaning.  
   - **Semi‑deponent verbs** have active forms in some tenses but middle/passive in others (e.g., *ἀποκρίνεσθαι*).

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology  

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Proto‑Indo‑European origin** | The middle voice derives from an **in‑transitive reflexive** construction in PIE, where the subject performed the action on or for itself. The passive developed from a **mediopassive** voice that merged the two functions in Greek. |
| **Sound changes** | The **‑ω** ending goes back to the PIE **‑ō** (first‑person singular present). The middle/passive endings **‑εσθαι, ‑εται, ‑ονται** reflect the PIE **‑to‑** (middle) and **‑h₁‑** (passive) suffixes that later converged. |
