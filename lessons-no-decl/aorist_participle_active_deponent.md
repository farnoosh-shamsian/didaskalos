# Lesson: The Aorist Active Participle (and Deponent Verbs)

---

## 1. What Is the Aorist Active Participle?

| Term | Meaning in Greek | Rough English equivalent |
|------|------------------|--------------------------|
| **Aorist** | The aspect of a single, complete event. In the participle it usually conveys **priority** to the main verb. | "having done," "after doing" |
| **Participle** | A verbal adjective: both verb and adjective; it can modify a noun or itself stand in for one. | "…ing / having …," "the one who …" |
| **Active** | The (modified) subject of the participle performs the action. | "the man **who struck**" |
| **Deponent** | Verbs with middle shape but active meaning. Important: some of them have an **active** aorist! | ἔρχομαι → aorist participle **ἐλθών** "having come" |

The aorist active participle describes a **completed** action performed by the noun it modifies; like any adjective it declines for gender, number, and case.

> **Typical translation:** "having done," "after doing," "the one who did"

---

## 2. Forms & Patterns

### 2.1 Regular Verbs (First Aorist)

Aorist active participle = **aorist stem (without the augment) + ‑σας/‑σασα/‑σαν**:

| Gender / Number | Ending | Example with **λύω** "to loose" (stem **λυσ‑**) |
|-----------------|-------------------|--------------------------------------------------------|
| **Masculine singular** | ‑ας | **λύσας** "having loosed" |
| **Feminine singular** | ‑ασα | **λύσασα** |
| **Neuter singular** | ‑αν | **λῦσαν** |
| **Masculine plural** | ‑αντες | **λύσαντες** |
| **Feminine plural** | ‑ασαι | **λύσασαι** |
| **Neuter plural** | ‑αντα | **λύσαντα** |

**Full paradigm (principal cases):**

| Case | Masc. sg. | Fem. sg. | Neut. sg. | Masc. pl. | Fem. pl. | Neut. pl. |
|------|-------------|------------|-------------|-----------|----------|-----------|
| Nominative | λύσας | λύσασα | λῦσαν | λύσαντες | λύσασαι | λύσαντα |
| Genitive | λύσαντος | λυσάσης | λύσαντος | λυσάντων | λυσασῶν | λυσάντων |
| Dative | λύσαντι | λυσάσῃ | λύσαντι | λύσασι(ν) | λυσάσαις | λύσασι(ν) |
| Accusative | λύσαντα | λύσασαν | λῦσαν | λύσαντας | λυσάσας | λύσαντα |

*The masculine and neuter follow the third declension (ντ-stem) and the feminine follows the first. The same pattern works for any regular verb: γράφω → **γράψας**, παιδεύω → **παιδεύσας**.*

### 2.2 Contract Verbs and Second Aorists

| Verb | Aorist stem | Participle (masc. nom.) |
|------|-------------|---------------------------------|
| **φιλέω** "to love" | φιλησ‑ | **φιλήσας** (lengthened vowel + ‑σας) |
| **δείκνυμι** "to show" | δειξ‑ | **δείξας** |
| **λαμβάνω** "to take" (second aorist) | λαβ‑ | **λαβών, λαβοῦσα, λαβόν** (genitive λαβόντος) |
| **ὁράω** "to see" (second aorist) | ἰδ‑ | **ἰδών, ἰδοῦσα, ἰδόν** |

*The second aorist participle takes the endings ‑ών/‑οῦσα/‑όν — the same endings as the present participle, but on the aorist stem and with the accent on the ending.*

### 2.3 Deponent Verbs

Deponents have only middle forms in the present, but in the aorist there are two behaviors:

| Deponent verb | Meaning | Aorist | Aorist participle |
|--------------|---------|-------------|----------------------------------------|
| **ἔρχομαι** | to come | ἦλθον (active!) | **ἐλθών, ἐλθοῦσα, ἐλθόν** — active in form |
| **γίγνομαι** | to become | ἐγενόμην (middle) | **γενόμενος, ‑η, ‑ον** — middle in form |
| **δέχομαι** | to receive | ἐδεξάμην (middle) | **δεξάμενος** |
| **ἀποκρίνομαι** | to answer | ἀπεκρινάμην (middle) | **ἀποκρινάμενος** (in the Koine: ἀποκριθείς) |

**The key point of this lesson:** the apparently "middle" verb ἔρχομαι has an aorist participle that is **active in both form and meaning**: **ἐλθών** "having come" — one of the most frequent participles in all of Greek literature.

## 3. Key Rules & Exceptions

| Rule | Explanation | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **1. Aorist stem, without the augment** | The augment belongs to the indicative. | ἔλυσα → **λύσας** (not *ἐλύσας*) |
| **2. First aorist: ‑σας** | ‑σας/‑σασα/‑σαν on the sigmatic stem. | γράψας |
| **3. Second aorist: ‑ών** | ‑ών/‑οῦσα/‑όν with the accent on the ending. | λαβών, ἐλθών, ἰδών |
| **4. Deponents** | If their aorist is active, the participle is active (ἐλθών); if middle, the participle is middle (γενόμενος). | — |
| **5. Agreement** | The participle agrees with its noun in case, number, and gender. | τὴν λύσασαν γυναῖκα "the woman who loosed" |
| **6. With the article = relative clause** | ὁ λύσας "the one who loosed." | οἱ ἐλθόντες "those who came" |
| **7. Priority in time** | The aorist participle usually marks an action prior to the main verb. | λύσας … ἐξῆλθεν "he loosed it and then went out" |

## 4. Example Sentences

| Greek | Transliteration | Translation |
|-------|----------------|-------------|
| **λύσας τὸν δεσμόν, ὁ ἀνὴρ ἐξῆλθεν.** | lýsas ton desmón… | Having loosed the bond, the man went out. |
| **ἐλθὼν εἰς τὴν πόλιν, ὁ στρατηγὸς ἐβόησεν.** | elthṑn eis tēn pólin… | Having come into the city, the general shouted. |
| **παιδεύσασα τὸν παῖδα, ἡ γυνὴ ἀνεπαύσατο.** | paideúsasa ton paîda… | Having educated the child, the woman rested. |
| **οἱ λαβόντες τὰ ὅπλα ἐμαχέσαντο.** | hoi labóntes ta hópla… | Those who had taken up arms fought. |

## 5. Usage & Context

1. **Circumstantial (adverbial) participle** — at the head of its clause, it states the circumstances of the main action: **ἐλθών, ὁ ἄνθρωπος ἐβόησεν** "having come, the man shouted."
2. **Attributive participle** — with the article, like a relative clause: **ὁ λύσας τὸν δεσμὸν ἄνθρωπος** "the man who loosed the bond."
3. **Substantive** — the participle with the article becomes a noun: **οἱ ἐλθόντες** "those who came."
4. **With verbs of perception** — after verbs of seeing and hearing, the participle expresses the perceived action: **εἶδον αὐτὸν ἐλθόντα** "I saw him come."
5. **In narrative** — chains of "aorist participle + main verb" are the skeleton of Greek narrative; you will meet ἐλθών, λαβών, and ἰδών constantly.

---

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology

The active participle suffix **‑ντ‑** comes from Proto-Indo-European (compare Latin ‑nt‑ in *amans, amantis*); combined with the sigmatic marker it produced ‑σαντ‑ (λύσαντος). The masculine nominative ‑σας results from the simplification of the cluster *‑σαντς. The second aorist participle (ἐλθών, λαβών) sets the same suffix directly on the old stem. The survival of these formations in the most frequent verbs — including deponents like ἔρχομαι — testifies to how indispensable the participle is to the texture of Greek narrative.
