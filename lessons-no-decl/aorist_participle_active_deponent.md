# Lesson: The Aorist Active Participle (including Deponent Verbs)

---

## 1. What is the Aorist Active Participle?

| Term | Meaning in Greek | Rough English Equivalent |
|------|------------------|--------------------------|
| **Aorist** | Simple past aspect – an action viewed as a single, complete event. | “did / have done” |
| **Participle** | Verbal adjective that can modify a noun or stand alone. | “having …”, “who …” |
| **Active** | The subject of the verb performs the action. | “the man **who struck** the dog” |
| **Deponent** | Verbs that have only middle‑/passive‑form endings but a **active** meaning. | “I went” = ἐλθών (aorist *active* participle of the deponent ἔρχομαι) |

The **aorist active participle** therefore describes a **completed** action performed **by** the noun it modifies. It is a **verbal adjective** that can appear in any case, number, or gender, just like a regular adjective.

> **Typical translation:** “having …”, “who/which …”, “the … who …”

---

## 2. Forms & Patterns  

### 2.1. Regular (non‑deponent) verbs  

The aorist active participle is built from the **aorist stem** + the **suffix ‑σ‑** + the ordinary adjective endings.

| Gender / Number | Ending (after ‑σ‑) | Example with **λύω** “to loose” (aorist stem **λυσ‑**) |
|-----------------|-------------------|--------------------------------------------------------|
| **Masculine Singular** | ‑ας | **λῦσας** “having loosed” |
| **Feminine Singular** | ‑ασα | **λῦσασα** “having loosed (f.)” |
| **Neuter Singular** | ‑αν | **λῦσαν** “having loosed (n.)” |
| **Masculine Plural** | ‑αντες | **λῦσαντες** “having loosed (m.pl.)” |
| **Feminine Plural** | ‑ασαι | **λῦσασαι** “having loosed (f.pl.)” |
| **Neuter Plural** | ‑αντα | **λῦσαντα** “having loosed (n.pl.)” |

**Full paradigm (nominative case shown; other cases follow regular adjective declension):**

| Case | Masc. Sing. | Fem. Sing. | Neut. Sing. | Masc. Pl. | Fem. Pl. | Neut. Pl. |
|------|-------------|------------|-------------|-----------|----------|-----------|
| Nominative | λῦσας | λῦσασα | λῦσαν | λῦσαντες | λῦσασαι | λῦσαντα |
| Genitive   | λῦσαντος | λῦσασας | λῦσαντος | λῦσαντων | λῦσασῶν | λῦσαντων |
| Dative     | λῦσαντι | λῦσασῃ | λῦσαντι | λῦσασαῖς | λῦσασαῖς | λῦσασαῖς |
| Accusative | λῦσαντα | λῦσασαν | λῦσαν | λῦσασαντες | λῦσασας | λῦσαντα |
| Vocative   | λῦσας | λῦσασα | λῦσαν | λῦσαντες | λῦσασαι | λῦσαντα |

> **Note:** The endings above are the **standard adjective endings** for the first‑aorist participle (‑σ‑). The same pattern works for any regular verb (e.g., *γράφω → γράψας*, *παιδεύω → παιδεύσας*).

### 2.2. Contract verbs  

For verbs whose stem ends in a vowel, the **‑σ‑** may be dropped or merged.

| Verb | Aorist Stem | Active Participle (Masc. Sing.) |
|------|-------------|---------------------------------|
| **φιλέω** “to love” | φιλησ‑ | **φίλας** (or **φίλας** with ‑σ‑ retained) |
| **δείκνυμι** “to show” | δεῖξ‑ | **δείξας** |
| **πίνω** “to drink” | ἐπίν‑ (aorist) → *πίν* + ‑σ‑ → **πίνσας** → regularised as **πίνσας** (rare) → more common **πίνων** (present participle) – note that many contract verbs simply use the **‑σ‑**‑less form: **ἔδωκας** → **δῶκας** (archaic).  

### 2.3. Deponent verbs  

Deponent verbs **do not have active forms** in the present, imperfect, or future. Their aorist active participle, however, **does exist** and is formed exactly as for a regular verb, but it uses the **aorist stem of the deponent** and the **‑σ‑** suffix.

| Deponent verb | Meaning | Aorist stem | Aorist active participle (Masc. Sing.) |
|--------------|---------|-------------|----------------------------------------|
| **ἔρχομαι** “to go, come” | go | ἐλθ‑ | **ἐλθών** (masc. nom. sg.) |
| **ἀποκρίνομαι** “to answer” | answer | ἀπεκρί‑ | **ἀπεκρίσας** (masc. nom. sg.) |
| **προσεύχομαι** “to pray” | pray | προσευξ‑ | **προσευξάμενος** (note: often uses the **‑αμενος** form, aorist middle participle with active sense) |

**Key point:** Even though the form is formally *middle/passive* (‑σ‑ + middle endings), the meaning is **active**: *ἐλθών* = “having come / who came”.

## 3. Key Rules & Exceptions  

| Rule | Explanation | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **1. Use the aorist stem** | The participle is built on the same stem used for the aorist indicative (e.g., λυ‑, γράψ‑). | λῦσας from λυ‑ |
| **2. Add ‑σ‑** | Insert the suffix **‑σ‑** before the adjective endings. | γράψ‑ + ‑ας → **γράψας** |
| **3. Contract verbs** | If the stem ends in a vowel, the ‑σ‑ may be omitted or merged; the participle often looks like a simple past participle. | φιλε‑ + ‑ας → **φίλας** |
| **4. Deponent verbs** | Use the aorist stem of the deponent; the participle is formally middle but translates actively. | ἔρχομαι → ἐλθ‑ + ‑ών → **ἐλθών** |
| **5. Irregular stems** | Some verbs have irregular aorist stems (e.g., *εἰμί* → *ἐγεν* → **ἐγένοντα**). | εἰμί → **ἐγένοντα** (neuter sg.) |
| **6. Gender agreement** | The participle must agree with the noun it modifies in **case, number, gender**. | **λῦσας τὸν ἄνδρα** “having loosed the man”. |
| **7. Use with the verb *εἰμί*** | The aorist active participle can serve as a **noun** (aorist substantive) when combined with *εἰμί*: *λῦσας ἐγώ εἰμι* “I am the one who loosed”. | — |
| **8. No future aorist participle** | The aorist participle always refers to a **completed** action; there is no future aorist participle. | — |

## 4. Example Sentences  

| Greek | Transliteration | Translation |
|-------|----------------|-------------|
| **λῦσας τὸν δεσμὸν, ὁ ἀνὴρ ἐξῆλθεν.** | **lūsas ton desmon, ho anēr exēlthen.** | “Having loosed the bond, the man went out.” |
| **Ἐλθών εἰς τὴν πόλιν, ὁ στρατηγός ἐβόησεν.** | **Elthōn eis tēn polin, ho stratēgos ebōesen.** | “Having come into the city, the general shouted.” (aorist active participle of the deponent ἔρχομαι) |
| **Παιδεύσας τὸν παῖδα, ἡ γυνὴ ἀπέπνευσε.** | **Paideusas ton paida, hē gynē apepneuse.** | “Having educated the child, the woman died.” |
| **Ἀπεκρίσας τὸν ἀγγέλοντα, ὁ ἱερεὺς ἐξέπνευσε.** | **Apekrisas ton angelonta, ho hiereus exepneuse.** | “Having answered the messenger, the priest breathed out.” (deponent *ἀποκρίνομαι*) |

## 5. Usage & Context  

1. **Circumstantial (adverbial) participle** – Often placed at the beginning of a clause to indicate the circumstance under which the main action occurs.  
   *Example:* **Ἐλθών, ὁ ἄνθρωπος ἐβόησεν.** “Having come, the man shouted.”

2. **Attributive participle** – Directly modifies a noun, like an adjective.  
   *Example:* **Ὁ λῦσας τὸν δεσμὸν ἄνθρωπος** “the man who loosed the bond”.

3. **Aorist substantive** – When used with *εἰμί* or as a noun in its own right.  
   *Example:* **Λῦσας ἐγώ εἰμι** “I am the one who loosed”.

4. **With verbs of perception, causation, or command** – The aorist participle can express a completed action that is the object of another verb.  
   *Example:* **Ἐγὼ ἀκούω λῦσας τὸν ἥλιον** “I hear (that) the sun has set”.

5. **Deponent participles** – Because deponents lack active forms, the aorist active participle is the primary way to give them an *active* sense in a participial clause.

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology  

- **Indo‑European Roots:** The participle originates from the PIE *‑nt‑* (present) and *‑s‑* (aorist)
