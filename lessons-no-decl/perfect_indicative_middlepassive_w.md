# Lesson: The Perfect Indicative Middle/Passive (‑ω)  

*Target level: Beginner → Intermediate*  
*Prerequisites: Greek alphabet, basic verb concepts (present, aorist), idea of voice.*

---

## 1. What the Perfect Middle/Passive Is  

| Term | Meaning in English | Typical English equivalent |
|------|-------------------|----------------------------|
| **Perfect** | A past action whose **result is still true** in the present. | “I have written”, “I am (now) written”. |
| **Indicative** | The ordinary “statement” mood (as opposed to subjunctive, optative, etc.). |
| **Middle/Passive** | The verb is either **reflexive / for the subject’s own benefit** (middle) or **receives the action** (passive). In the perfect the middle often has a **stative** sense: “I have (for myself) …”. |
| **‑ω** | The first‑person singular ending of the perfect middle/passive (‑**μαι** after the stem). In the tables the ending is shown as **‑μαι**; the “‑ω” label in some textbooks simply reminds you that the form is a **present‑type** ending (‑ω) attached to the perfect stem. |

**Key idea:** The perfect middle/passive tells the listener that *a completed action* has left the subject in a *new state* (often “for himself/herself”).  

*Example*:  
- **λέλυμαι** – “I have loosed (for myself) / I am (now) loosed”.

---

## 2. Forming the Perfect Middle/Passive  

### 2.1 The Perfect Stem  

1. **Reduplication** – most verbs repeat the first consonant + a short vowel (usually *ε*).  
   - λύω → **λελυ‑** (reduplication of *λ* + *ε*)  
2. **Vowel change** – some verbs change the vowel of the present stem (e.g., *γίγνομαι* → *γεγόνα*).  
3. **Irregular stems** – a handful of verbs have unique perfect stems (e.g., *εἰμί* → *εἶπον* → perfect *εἶπον* is not used; *εἰμί* has no perfect).  

### 2.2 Adding the Middle/Passive Endings  

| Person | Ending (Middle/Passive) | Example with **λέλυμαι** (perfect of λύω) |
|--------|------------------------|--------------------------------------------|
| 1 sg   | **‑μαι**               | **λέλυμαι** – “I have loosed (for myself)” |
| 2 sg   | **‑σαι**               | **λέλυσαι** – “you have loosed (for yourself)” |
| 3 sg   | **‑ται**               | **λέλυται** – “he/she/it has been loosed” |
| 1 pl   | **‑μεθα**              | **λέλυμεθα** – “we have loosed (for ourselves)” |
| 2 pl   | **‑σθε**               | **λέλυσθε** – “you (pl.) have loosed (for yourselves)” |
| 3 pl   | **‑νται**              | **λέλυνται** – “they have been loosed” |

> **Note:** The perfect middle/passive uses the **same endings** as the present middle/passive (‑μαι, ‑σαι, …) but the **stem is the perfect stem**.

### 2.3 Accentuation  

- The accent is **always on the penult** (the second‑to‑last syllable) **if that syllable is long**; otherwise it falls on the ultima.  
- In the perfect middle/passive the penult is usually long because the perfect stem ends in a long vowel or diphthong (e.g., **λέ**‑**λυ**‑**μαι** → accent on the first syllable).

## 4. Key Rules & Common Exceptions  

| Rule | Explanation | Example / Exception |
|------|-------------|----------------------|
| **A. Reduplication with ε** | Most verbs form the perfect stem by adding **ε‑** before the root and repeating the first consonant. | λύω → **λελυ‑**; ποίεω → **πεποί** (no ε‑reduplication, but a different pattern). |
| **B. Verbs with a‑stem** | Verbs whose present stem begins with a vowel often get a **σ‑** or **ε‑** before the stem. | ἔρχομαι → **ἐληλυθ** (perfect of ἔρχομαι is ἐλήλυθα, not formed by simple reduplication). |
| **C. Irregular perfect stems** | Some verbs have historically irregular perfect stems. | φημί → **εἰπ** (perfect: εἶπον, but middle/passive not used). |
| **D. Contracted endings** | When the perfect stem ends in a vowel, the middle/passive endings may contract (‑α + ‑μαι → ‑μαι). | γιγνώσκω → perfect stem **ἐγνώ** → **ἐγνώμην
