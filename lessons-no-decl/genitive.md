## Introduction to the Genitive Case

In ancient Greek the genitive is expressed by adding a fixed suffix to the noun's stem. Which suffix is used depends on the declension to which the noun belongs and on whether the form is singular or plural.

**How the genitive is built**

1. **Find the stem** — strip the nominative ending (‑α, ‑η, ‑ος, ‑ον, etc.) to obtain the base.
2. **Add the appropriate genitive suffix** — each declension has its own characteristic endings for singular and plural.
3. **Watch the accent** — the accent usually stays where it was in the nominative, unless the rules of accentuation force a shift.

---

### First-declension (‑α / ‑η stems, mainly feminine)

| Number | Genitive ending | Example |
| -------- | -------------------- | ---------------------------- |
| Singular | **‑ης** (or **‑ας**) | τῆς τιμ‑ῆς "of the honor" |
| Plural | **‑ῶν** | τῶν τιμ‑ῶν "of the honors" |

The plural ending ‑ῶν of the first declension always carries a circumflex, whatever the accent of the other forms.

---

### Second-declension (‑ο stems, masculine and neuter)

| Number | Genitive ending | Masculine example | Neuter example |
| -------- | --------------- | --------------------------- | ----------------------------- |
| Singular | **‑ου** | τοῦ φίλ‑ου "of the friend" | τοῦ δώρ‑ου "of the gift" |
| Plural | **‑ων** | τῶν φίλ‑ων "of the friends" | τῶν δώρ‑ων "of the gifts" |

The accent normally stays where it was in the nominative form.

---

### Third-declension (various stems, all three genders)

The ending varies with the shape of the stem.

| Stem type | Singular ending | Plural ending | Example |
| -------------------------------------------------------------- | --------------- | ------------- | -------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| Consonant stem (masc./fem./neut.) | **‑ος** | **‑ων** | τοῦ φύλακ‑ος "of the guard," τῶν φυλάκ‑ων |
| ι-stem (like πόλις) | **‑εως** | **‑εων** | τῆς πόλε‑ως "of the city," τῶν πόλε‑ων |
| Sigma-stem neuter (like γένος) | **‑ους** | **‑ῶν** | τοῦ γέν‑ους "of the family," τῶν γεν‑ῶν |

In consonant stems the genitive singular in ‑ος is often the only place where the noun's true stem shows itself (σῶμα → σώματος); that is why third-declension nouns are always learned together with their genitive.

---

### Summary of the pattern

| Declension | Singular suffix | Plural suffix |
| ---------- | --------------- | ------------- |
| 1st | ‑ης / ‑ας | ‑ῶν |
| 2nd | ‑ου | ‑ων |
| 3rd | ‑ος (or ‑εως, ‑ους) | ‑ων |

The morphology of the genitive in ancient Greek is thus a systematic addition: the suffix is determined by the noun's declension and number. This yields forms such as **τῆς τιμῆς**, **τοῦ φίλου**, **τῶν τιμῶν**, **τῶν φίλων**, **τοῦ φύλακος**, and **τῆς πόλεως**.
