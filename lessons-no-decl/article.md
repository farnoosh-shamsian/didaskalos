# The Definite Article

## 1. Introduction

Ancient Greek has a definite article corresponding to English "the" — but unlike English *the*, it **declines**: its form changes with the gender, number, and case of its noun. It is the single most frequent word in Greek texts, and because it wears its noun's grammar so visibly, it is also the reader's best guide through a sentence.

There is **no indefinite article** ("a/an"): ἄνθρωπος by itself means "a man" or simply "man".

## 2. Declension

| Case | M. sg. | F. sg. | N. sg. | M. pl. | F. pl. | N. pl. |
|---|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| Nominative | ὁ | ἡ | τό | οἱ | αἱ | τά |
| Genitive | τοῦ | τῆς | τοῦ | τῶν | τῶν | τῶν |
| Dative | τῷ | τῇ | τῷ | τοῖς | ταῖς | τοῖς |
| Accusative | τόν | τήν | τό | τούς | τάς | τά |

(The dual — τώ, τοῖν — is rare and can be recognized when met.)

### Key observations

- The **genitive plural is τῶν for all three genders**.
- Neuter nominative and accusative are identical (τό, τά) — as with all neuters.
- Four forms lack an initial τ and carry a rough breathing instead: ὁ, ἡ, οἱ, αἱ.
- There is **no vocative article**; direct address uses the particle ὦ: ὦ ἄνδρες "gentlemen!"

## 3. Basic Usage

- ὁ λόγος — "the word" · ἡ γυνή — "the woman" · τὸ δῶρον — "the gift"
- The article agrees with its noun in gender, number, and case, exactly like an adjective: τοῦ ἀγαθοῦ ἀνθρώπου "of the good man".
- Greek regularly uses the article where English does not: with **proper names** (ὁ Σωκράτης "Socrates"), **abstract nouns** (ἡ ἀρετή "virtue"), and **possessives** (ὁ πατήρ often = "my/his father", clear from context).

## 4. The Article as a Noun-Maker

The article can turn almost anything into a noun phrase:

| With... | Example | Meaning |
|---|---|---|
| an adjective | οἱ σοφοί | "the wise (people)" |
| a participle | οἱ λέγοντες | "those who speak" |
| an adverb | οἱ νῦν | "the people of today" |
| a genitive | τὰ τῆς πόλεως | "the affairs of the city" |
| an infinitive | τὸ λέγειν | "speaking" (as a noun) |

## 5. The Article as a Pronoun

The article began life as a demonstrative pronoun ("that one"), and two traces of that older use are everywhere in Classical prose:

- **ὁ μέν … ὁ δέ …** — "the one … the other …", "some … others …"
- **ὁ δέ** at the start of a sentence — "and he / but he" (marking a change of subject): ὁ δὲ ἀπεκρίνατο "and he answered".

In Homer this demonstrative force is still the norm; by the Classical period the article is fully grammaticalized, and in Koine its use becomes even more systematic.

## 6. Why It Matters

Watch the article and it will tell you:

- the **gender, number, and case** of a noun whose own ending is ambiguous (ἡ ὁδός: feminine!),
- where a noun phrase **begins and ends** (everything between article and noun belongs to the phrase: ἡ τῶν Ἀθηναίων πόλις "the city of the Athenians"),
- whether an adjective is **attributive or predicate** (ὁ σοφὸς ἀνήρ "the wise man" vs. σοφὸς ὁ ἀνήρ "the man is wise").

Learning the article's 17 distinct forms thoroughly is the single best early investment in reading Greek.
