## Introduction to the Dative Case

The dative case marks the indirect object, the recipient, or the "to/for" relation. Its form is created by attaching a case-ending to the noun's stem; the shape of that ending depends on the declension to which the noun belongs.

---

### 1. First-declension (‑ᾱ / ‑η stems)

| Gender | Singular ending | Example | Plural ending | Example |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Feminine | ‑ᾳ / ‑ῃ | τῇ γλώττῃ "to/for the tongue" | ‑αις | ταῖς γλώτταις "to/for the tongues" |
| Masculine (less common) | ‑ᾳ / ‑ῃ | τῷ νεανίᾳ "to/for the young man" | ‑αις | τοῖς νεανίαις "to/for the young men" |

_Morphology_ — The stem ends in a long vowel (‑ᾱ or ‑η). In the singular, iota comes directly after that vowel and is written as an **iota subscript** (‑ᾳ, ‑ῃ). In the plural the ending is **‑αις**.

---

### 2. Second-declension (‑ο stems, including ‑ο-stem adjectives)

| Gender | Singular ending | Example | Plural ending | Example |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Masculine / neuter | ‑ῳ | τῷ φίλῳ "to/for the friend" | ‑οις | τοῖς φίλοις "to/for the friends" |
| Feminine (‑ος nouns) | ‑ῳ | τῇ ὁδῷ "to/for the road" | ‑οις | ταῖς ὁδοῖς "to/for the roads" |

_Morphology_ — The stem ends in **‑ο**. The dative singular takes the iota subscript, giving **‑ῳ**; the plural takes **‑οις**. The noun's original accent stays put as far as the phonological rules of Greek allow.

---

### 3. Third-declension (various stems)

Because third-declension stems vary (consonantal, ι-stems, υ-stems, etc.), several patterns occur. The most frequent are:

| Stem type | Singular ending | Example | Plural ending | Example |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Consonant stem | ‑ι | τῷ φύλακι "to/for the guard" | ‑σι(ν) | τοῖς φύλαξι(ν) "to/for the guards" |
| ι-stem | ‑ει | τῇ πόλει "to/for the city" | ‑εσι(ν) | ταῖς πόλεσι(ν) "to/for the cities" |
| υ-stem | ‑υι | τῷ ἰχθύι "to/for the fish" | ‑υσι(ν) | τοῖς ἰχθύσι(ν) "to/for the fish (pl.)" |
| Irregular: ναῦς | — | τῇ νηί "to/for the ship" | — | ταῖς ναυσί(ν) "to/for the ships" |

_Morphology_ — The stem may change slightly before the dative ending attaches (usually where the stem consonant meets ‑σι: φυλακ‑ + σι → φύλαξι). The default endings are **‑ι** for the singular and **‑σι(ν)** for the plural.

---

### General principles that apply to all declensions

1. **Accent preservation** — The noun's original accent stays on the same syllable whenever the addition of the dative ending does not force a shift. An acute on the penult of the nominative typically remains on the penult in the dative; an acute on the antepenult may be pushed forward when the final syllable becomes long.
2. **Length of the ultima** — Dative endings are either long (**‑ῳ, ‑οις**) or short (**‑ι, ‑σι**). This length influences accent placement and the behavior of any following enclitic.
3. **Enclitic interaction** — When a dative form is followed by an enclitic (e.g., the verb _εἰμί_), the distribution of accents follows the usual enclitic rules.

---

### Summary of the core endings

| Declension | Singular ending | Plural ending |
| --- | --- | --- |
| First | ‑ᾳ / ‑ῃ | ‑αις |
| Second | ‑ῳ | ‑οις |
| Third (most common) | ‑ι | ‑σι(ν) |

These endings attach directly to the noun's stem, producing the dative forms that express "to" or "for" the noun in Ancient Greek.
