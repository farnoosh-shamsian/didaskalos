# Future Indicative Active (‑ω)  
*Ancient Greek – Beginner‑Intermediate Lesson*

---

## 1. What the Future Indicative Active Is  

The **future indicative active** expresses an action that **will happen** (or is intended to happen) in the future.  
In the classical language it is built on the **future stem** plus the **active personal endings** that end in **‑ω** (1st sg.) and **‑εις, ‑ει, ‑ομεν, ‑ετε, ‑ουσι(ν)** (the other persons).  

The characteristic element of the Greek future is the **‑σ‑** suffix (the “future marker”).  
When the suffix is attached to the verb stem and the ordinary active endings are added, we obtain the full future paradigm.

> **Future = present‑stem + ‑σ‑ + active endings**  

The resulting first‑person singular form always ends in **‑σω** (or ‑‑σω for contract verbs), which is why the whole tense is often called the **‑σω‑form**.

---

## 2. Forming the Future Stem  

| Verb type | How the future stem is built | Example (present stem) | Future stem |
|-----------|-----------------------------|------------------------|-------------|
| **Thematic –ω verbs** (e.g., *λύ‑ω*) | Add **‑σ‑** to the present stem | λύ‑ | **λυσ‑** |
| **Contract verbs** ending in **‑αω, ‑εω, ‑οω** | Lengthen the vowel (α→α, ε→η, ο→ω) and add **‑σ‑** | τιμά‑ω → *τιμά‑* | **τιμήσ‑** |
| **‑μι verbs** (e.g., *φημί*) | Add **‑σ‑** to the present stem (the stem already ends in ‑μ) | φη‑μ | **φησ‑** |
| **Irregular verbs** | Special forms (see § 4) | — | — |

*Note:* The **present stem** is the form you would find in the present indicative (e.g., *λύ‑* from *λύω*).

|--------|------|----------------|-------|
| 1 sg. | λύσω | **lūsō** | I will loosen |
| 2 sg. | λύσῃς | **lūsēs** | you (sg.) will loosen |
| 3 sg. | λύσῃ | **lūsē** | he/she/it will loosen |

| 3 pl. | λύσουσι(ν) | **lūsousi(n)** | they will loosen |

*Construction:* λυ‑ (present stem) + **σ** + active endings (‑ω, ‑εις, ‑ει, ‑ομεν, ‑ετε, ‑ουσι(ν)) → λυ‑σ‑ + ending.

## 4. Key Rules & Common Exceptions  

| Rule | Explanation | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Add ‑σ‑ before the endings** | The future marker ‑σ‑ is inserted between the stem and the personal ending. | λυ‑ **σ** ‑ω → λύσω |
| **Contract verbs lengthen the vowel** | In ‑αω, ‑εω, ‑οω verbs the vowel becomes long (α→α, ε→η, ο→ω) before ‑σ‑. | τιμά‑ **σ** → τιμήσ‑ |
| **‑μι verbs lose ‑μ** | The ‑μ of the present stem disappears; the future looks like a regular ‑ω verb. | φη‑ **σ** → φήσ‑ |
| **Irregular stems** | Some common verbs have completely different future stems. | εἰμί → ἔσομαι |
| **Accentuation** | The accent normally falls on the **penult** (the syllable before the ending) unless the ending is long, in which case it moves to the **antepenult**. | λύσω (penult), λύσουσι(ν) (antepenult) |
| **Spelling of the 3rd‑person plural** | Both **‑ουσι** and **‑ουσιν** are acceptable; the final **‑ν** is optional in prose. | λύσουσι(ν) |
| **Middle/Passive Future** | Not covered here, but note that the middle/passive uses **‑σομαι, ‑σῇ, ‑σθαι** etc., not the active ‑σω‑forms. | — |

## 6. Usage & Context  

1. **Simple Future** – Describes a straightforward future action:  
   *Ἀγορὰν ποιήσω* “I will make a purchase.”  

2. **Future of Intention** – Often used with verbs of volition (βούλομαι, θέλω) to express a planned action:  
   *Θέλω ἀναβῆναι· ἀναβήσομαι* “I want to go up; I will go up.”  

3. **Future in Narrative** – In historical or epic prose the future can give a sense of inevitability or prophecy.  

4. **Conditional Clauses** – In the protasis of a future more vivid condition (future more vivid):  
   *Ἐὰν ἔλθῃς, ἀκούσω* “If you come, I will hear.”  

5. **Future Perfect** – The future perfect (e.g., *λελύσομαι*) is a different tense; do not confuse it with the simple future.  

6. **Poetic License** – Poets sometimes retain hiatus or use crasis to avoid the clash of vowels when a verb beginning with a vowel follows another vowel‑ending word.

## 7. Historical Development & Etymology  

* **Proto‑Indo‑European Origin** – The Greek future marker **‑σ‑** derives from the PIE suffix *‑s‑* used to form future or desiderative meanings (cf. Latin *‑bō* “I shall” from *‑bʰ‑* and Sanskrit *‑syati*).  

* **Phonological Evolution** – In early Greek the suffix appeared after the stem and before the personal endings. Over time the vowel of the stem often lengthened (especially in contract verbs) and the *‑s‑* became a stable part of the future paradigm.  

* **Relation to Other Tenses** – The future active shares its endings with the present active; the only difference is the insertion of *‑σ‑*. This explains why the first‑person singular ends in **‑σω** rather than **‑ω**.
