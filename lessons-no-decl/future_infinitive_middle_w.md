# Lesson: The Future Middle Infinitive of ω-Verbs

*Target level: Intermediate*
*Prerequisites: Future indicative middle, present middle infinitive.*

---

## 1. What This Form Is

The **future middle infinitive** means "to be going to …," "that … will …":

> **λύσεσθαι** "to be about to ransom" · **μαχεῖσθαι** "to be going to fight"

Unlike the present and aorist infinitives, which express **aspect**, the future infinitive expresses **time**: it looks forward. And unlike them, it is almost entirely confined to two environments:

1. **Indirect discourse** after verbs of saying, thinking, hoping, promising, swearing.
2. **After μέλλω** "I am about to."

Outside these, Greek does not use a future infinitive at all — a complement infinitive after βούλομαι or δεῖ is present or aorist, never future.

---

## 2. Forms and Patterns

### 2.1 Formation

Future stem + the middle infinitive ending **‑εσθαι**:

| Verb | Future indicative | Future middle infinitive |
|---|---|---|
| λύω "loose" | λύσομαι | **λύσεσθαι** |
| παύω "stop" | παύσομαι | **παύσεσθαι** |
| βούλομαι "want" | βουλήσομαι | **βουλήσεσθαι** |
| γίγνομαι "become" | γενήσομαι | **γενήσεσθαι** |
| ἀκούω "hear" | ἀκούσομαι | **ἀκούσεσθαι** |
| λαμβάνω "take" | λήψομαι | **λήψεσθαι** |

The accent is **recessive within the limits of the ending**: λύσεσθαι, παύσεσθαι.

### 2.2 Verbs Whose Future Is Middle in Form

This is where the form earns its frequency. Many extremely common verbs are active in the present but middle in the future, so their future infinitive can only be this one:

| Present | Future infinitive | Gloss |
|---|---|---|
| ἀκούω | **ἀκούσεσθαι** | to be going to hear |
| λαμβάνω | **λήψεσθαι** | to be going to take |
| μανθάνω | **μαθήσεσθαι** | to be going to learn |
| φεύγω | **φεύξεσθαι** | to be going to flee |
| πάσχω | **πείσεσθαι** | to be going to suffer |
| βαίνω | **βήσεσθαι** | to be going to go |

### 2.3 Liquid Futures

Stems in λ, μ, ν, ρ lose the σ and contract:

| Verb | Future | Infinitive |
|---|---|---|
| μάχομαι "fight" | μαχοῦμαι | **μαχεῖσθαι** |
| ἀγγέλλω "report" | ἀγγελοῦμαι | **ἀγγελεῖσθαι** |

## 3. Key Rules & Exceptions

| Rule | Explanation | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Time, not aspect** | The one infinitive that genuinely means "later than the main verb." | |
| **2. Confined to indirect discourse and μέλλω** | Do not use it as a complement of βούλομαι or δεῖ. | ἐλπίζει νικήσειν, but βούλεται νικῆσαι |
| **3. Ending ‑εσθαι, always** | The middle infinitive ending is invariable across tenses; the tense lives in the stem. | λύεσθαι (pres.) / λύσεσθαι (fut.) |
| **4. One σ makes the difference** | λύσεσθαι vs. λύεσθαι — a single letter separates future from present. Read carefully. | |
| **5. Middle form, often active meaning** | For ἀκούω, λαμβάνω, φεύγω the middle form has no middle sense. | |
| **6. Not the future passive infinitive** | λύσεσθαι (middle) vs. λυθήσεσθαι (passive, "to be going to be loosed"). | |
| **7. μέλλω governs it directly** | μέλλω λύσεσθαι "I am about to ransom." μέλλω is the one verb that regularly takes a future infinitive outside indirect discourse. | |

## 4. Example Sentences

| Greek | Translation |
|---|---|
| ἔφασαν τοὺς συμμάχους **μαχεῖσθαι**. | "They said that the allies would fight." |
| ἤλπιζον τὴν πόλιν **λήψεσθαι**. | "They hoped to take the city." |
| ὑπέσχετο ταῦτα **γενήσεσθαι**. | "He promised that this would happen." |
| ἔμελλον οἱ πολέμιοι **φεύξεσθαι**. | "The enemy were about to flee." |
| ἐνόμιζον οὐδὲν κακὸν **πείσεσθαι**. | "They thought they would suffer no harm." |

## 5. Usage & Context

1. **Indirect discourse** — after φημί, λέγω, νομίζω, οἴομαι, ἐλπίζω, ὑπισχνέομαι, ὄμνυμι. The future infinitive represents a future indicative of the direct speech: "they will fight" → ἔφασαν μαχεῖσθαι.
2. **After μέλλω** — the standard periphrasis for imminent action: μέλλει γενήσεσθαι "it is about to happen." (μέλλω also occurs with the present infinitive, with little difference of sense.)
3. **Verbs of hoping, promising, swearing** — these regularly take the future infinitive precisely because their content is future: ἐλπίζω, ὑπισχνέομαι, ὄμνυμι.
4. **Subject accusative** — as always in indirect discourse, the subject of the infinitive stands in the accusative if it differs from that of the main verb: ἔφασαν **τοὺς συμμάχους** μαχεῖσθαι.
5. **Thucydidean use** — the speeches are dense with future infinitives, since the whole rhetorical genre consists of claims about what will follow.

---

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology

- The infinitive ending **‑σθαι** is the invariable middle marker across the Greek verb; only the stem changes from tense to tense.
- The future's **σ** continues the Indo-European desiderative *‑sye/o‑, which explains the natural fit between the future infinitive and verbs of hoping, promising, and intending.
- The **middle-for-active futures** (ἀκούσομαι, λήψομαι, πείσομαι) are archaisms preserving a wider Indo-European use of the middle voice for verbs of perception and experience.
- The future infinitive was one of the earliest casualties of Koine: as ὅτι-clauses replaced infinitival indirect discourse, it disappeared, leaving no trace in Modern Greek.
