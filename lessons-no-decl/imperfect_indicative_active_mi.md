# Lesson: The Imperfect – Indicative – Active (μι)

**Target:** Beginner‑intermediate students of Ancient Greek  
**Focus:** Understanding, forming, and using the imperfect tense of the active voice in the indicative mood (the “μι‑form”).

---

## 1. What the Imperfect Indicative Active Means  

| Term | Meaning in English | Greek function |
|------|-------------------|----------------|
| **Imperfect** | A past‑time action that was **ongoing, repeated, or incomplete** (e.g., “was doing”, “used to do”). | Shows a **continuous or habitual** past situation. |
| **Indicative** | The “real” mood – statements of fact or belief. | Used for ordinary narration. |
| **Active** | The subject performs the action. | The verb’s ending shows the subject directly. |
| **(μι)** | The label we give to this particular **tense‑voice‑mood combination** in Smyth’s tables. | It is the **imperfect‑indicative‑active**. |

The imperfect therefore answers the question *“What was happening?”* in a narrative past. It is not a simple “past” (that is the aorist) but a **past progressive**.

> **English parallel:** “I was walking”, “He used to love”, “They were fighting”.

---

## 2. Forms and Patterns  

### 2.1 The Regular (thematic) Paradigm  

For a regular **thematic** verb (stem ends in a vowel before the personal endings) the imperfect is built from three pieces:

1. **Augment** – a prefixed **ε‑** (or **η‑** after a vowel) that marks past time.  
2. **Verb stem** – the present stem (the one you find in the present indicative).  
3. **Imperfect endings** – ‑ον, ‑ες, ‑ε(ν), ‑ομεν, ‑ετε, ‑ον.

```
          ε‑ + stem + ὀν   ε‑ + stem + ες   ε‑ + stem + ε(ν)   ε‑ + stem + ομεν   ε‑ + stem + ετε   ε‑ + stem + ον
```

#### Full paradigm (example: λύω “to loose, to release”)

| Person | Form | Transliteration | Gloss |
|--------|------|----------------|-------|
| 1 sg | ἔλυον | **éluon** | I was loosing |
| 2 sg | ἔλυσες | **éluse(s)** | you were loosing |
| 3 sg | ἔλυε(ν) | **élue(n)** | he/she/it was loosing |
| 1 pl | ἐλύομεν | **elúomen** | we were loosing |
| 2 pl | ἐλύετε | **elúete** | you (pl.) were loosing |
| 3 pl | ἔλυον | **éluon** | they were loosing |

*Notice the augment ε‑ before the stem and the characteristic endings.*

---

### 2.2 Contract Verbs  

When the present stem ends in a vowel that contracts with the augment, the vowel **shortens** and the **ε‑** may disappear or become part of the contraction.

| Verb | Present stem | Imperfect 1 sg | Note |
|------|--------------|----------------|------|
| **φιλέω** “to love” | φιλε‑ | ἔφιλον | ε‑ + φιλε‑ → ἐφιλε‑ → ἔφιλον (contracted -ε‑) |
| **πράττω** “to do” (non‑contract) | πρατ‑ | ἐπράττων | No contraction; just ε‑ + πρατ‑ + ‑ων |

*The ending **‑ων** appears only in **‑μι**‑type verbs (see §2.4).*

---

### 2.3 Verbs Ending in **‑μι**  

Verbs whose present 1 sg ends in **‑μι** (e.g., **φημί**, **ἵστημι**) have a **special imperfect** pattern: the **‑μι** disappears and the ending **‑ν** is added.

| Verb | Present 1 sg | Imperfect 1 sg | Imperfect 2 sg | Imperfect 3 sg |
|------|--------------|----------------|----------------|----------------|
| **φημί** “to say” | φημί | ἔφην | ἔφησας | ἔφη(ν) |
| **ἵστημι** “to stand” | ἵστημι | ἔστην | ἔστησας | ἔστη(ν) |

The rest of the paradigm follows the regular endings (‑ς, ‑ν, ‑μεν, ‑τε, ‑ν).

---

### 2.4 The Verb **εἰμί** “to be”  

**εἰμί** is highly irregular. Its imperfect forms are:

| Person | Form | Transliteration | Gloss |
|--------|------|----------------|-------|
| 1 sg | ἦν | **ēn** | I was |
| 2 sg | ἦσθα | **ēstha** | you were |
| 3 sg | ἦν | **ēn** | he/she/it was |
| 1 pl | ἦμεν | **ēmen** | we were |
| 2 pl | ἦτε | **ēte** | you (pl.) were |
| 3 pl | ἦσαν | **ēsan** | they were |

No augment is added because the forms already contain the past vowel **η**.

## 3. Key Rules & Exceptions  

| Rule | Explanation | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **1. Add the augment ε‑** | Place ε‑ before the stem unless the stem begins with a vowel; then use **η‑** (the “long augment”). | λυ‑ → **ἔ**λυ‑; ἀ‑ → **ἦ** (as in ἦν). |
| **2. Use the imperfect endings** | ‑ον, ‑ες, ‑ε(ν), ‑ομεν, ‑ετε, ‑ον. | ἔλυον, ἔλυσες, ἔλυε(ν)… |
| **3. Contract verbs** | When the augment meets a vowel‑ending stem, the vowel shortens; the resulting form may look like a simple ε‑ + stem. | φιλε‑ → ἔφιλον (ε‑ + φιλε‑ → ἐφιλε‑ → ἔφιλον). |
| **4. ‑μι‑verbs** | Drop the ‑μι and add ‑ν (1 sg) or the regular endings thereafter. | φημί → ἔφην, ἔφησας, ἔφη(ν)… |
| **5. No augment for εἰμί** | The verb “to be” already contains the past vowel η; the augment is not added. | ἦν, ἦσαν, etc. |
| **6. Spelling of 3 sg** | The 3 sg ending can appear as **‑ε** or **‑εν**; both are correct. | ἔλυε / ἔλυεν. |
| **7. Reduplication is **not** used** | Unlike the perfect, the imperfect never reduplicates the stem. | — |

## 4. Example Sentences  

| Greek (Imperfect) | Transliteration | English |
|-------------------|----------------|---------|
| 1. ἦν ὁ ἀνήρ ἐν τῇ ἀγορᾷ. | **ēn ho anēr en tē agorā.** | The man **was** in the marketplace. |
| 2. ἔλυον τὸν δεσμὸν ἀεὶ. | **éluon ton desmon aei.** | I **was constantly loosening** the knot. |
| 3. ἔφησας ὅτι ἔρχεσθαι. | **éphēsas hoti érchesthai.** | You **were saying** that you were coming. |
| 4. ἔστην ἐπὶ τὸν λόφον. | **éstēn epi ton lophon.** | I **was standing** on the hill. |
| 5. ἦσαν οἱ παῖδες ἐν τῷ δάσῳ. | **ēsan hoi paides en tō dásō.** | The children **were** in the garden. |

## 5. Usage & Context  

1. **Narrative background** – The imperfect sets the scene: “While X was happening, Y occurred.”  
2. **Repeated or habitual past actions** – “He **used to** go to the market every day.”  
3. **Simultaneity** – Two actions occurring at the same past moment often both appear in the imperfect.  
4. **Contrast with the aorist** – The aorist presents a **simple, completed** past action; the imperfect stresses **duration or incompleteness**.  

> **Typical pattern:**  
> *ἦν ὁ ἥλιος ἀνατέλλων, καὶ ἐγὼ ἔγραφον τὸν λόγον.*  
> “The sun **was rising**, and I **was writing** the word.”

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology  

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Proto‑Indo‑European origin** | The imperfect derives from the PIE **suffix *‑‑t‑** (a past‑time marker) combined with the **augment** (originally a pre‑verb *e‑* meaning “before”). |
| **Augment** | The ε‑ (or η‑) is a relic of the PIE *e‑* that was added to verbs to indicate past time in the Indo‑European *satem* languages. Greek retained it only in the imperfect and aorist (the latter only in the “e‑aorist”). |
| **Loss of reduplication** | Earlier Indo‑European languages used reduplication for the imperfect; Greek dropped it, keeping only the augment and the characteristic endings. |
| **‑μι‑verbs** | These are remnants of an older **present‑stem in *‑mi*** (cf. Latin *amō* → *amābam*). In the imperfect the *‑mi* disappears, leaving the simple **‑ν** ending. |
| **εἰμί** | The verb “to be” shows a **suppletive** paradigm: its imperfect stems from a different root (*h₁es‑* → *ē‑*), explaining the irregular forms. |
