# Lesson: The Pluperfect Indicative Middle/Passive of ω-Verbs

*Target level: Intermediate*
*Prerequisites: Perfect indicative middle/passive, the augment, reduplication.*

---

## 1. What This Form Is

The **perfect** middle/passive describes a **state resulting from a completed action**, valid now: λέλυται "it has been loosed / it stands loosed."

The **pluperfect** shifts that whole picture into the past: **the state was already in place at some past moment.**

> **ἐλέλυτο** — "it had been loosed / it was (already) in a loosed state."

This is the tense of scene-setting and background. In Thucydides it is everywhere in the sentences that describe how things already stood before the action began: the walls **had been built**, the ships **had been drawn up**, the treaty **had been made**.

| Tense | Form | Sense |
|---|---|---|
| Perfect | λέλυται | "it has been loosed" (state now) |
| **Pluperfect** | **ἐλέλυτο** | "it had been loosed" (state then) |
| Aorist | ἐλύθη | "it was loosed" (the event itself) |

The aorist reports the **event**; the pluperfect reports the **state that the event had already produced**.

---

## 2. Forms and Patterns

### 2.1 Formation

**augment + reduplication + perfect middle stem + secondary middle endings**

The endings attach **directly to the stem** — there is no thematic vowel, exactly as in the perfect middle/passive.

| Person | Ending | λύω (stem λελυ‑) | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1 sg | ‑μην | **ἐλελύμην** | I had been loosed |
| 2 sg | ‑σο | **ἐλέλυσο** | you had been loosed |
| 3 sg | ‑το | **ἐλέλυτο** | he/she/it had been loosed |
| 1 pl | ‑μεθα | **ἐλελύμεθα** | we had been loosed |
| 2 pl | ‑σθε | **ἐλέλυσθε** | you (pl.) had been loosed |
| 3 pl | ‑ντο | **ἐλέλυντο** | they had been loosed |

Compare the perfect: λέλυμαι, λέλυσαι, λέλυται … The pluperfect adds the augment ἐ‑ and swaps the primary endings for secondary ones.

### 2.2 Consonant Stems and the Sound Changes

When the stem ends in a consonant, the ending collides with it and both adjust. This is where the forms start to look unfamiliar:

**γράφω "write"** (perfect middle γέγραμμαι):

| Person | Form | |
|---|---|---|
| 1 sg | **ἐγεγράμμην** | (γεγραφ‑μην → γεγραμμην) |
| 2 sg | **ἐγέγραψο** | (γεγραφ‑σο → γεγραψο) |
| 3 sg | **ἐγέγραπτο** | (γεγραφ‑το → γεγραπτο) |
| 1 pl | **ἐγεγράμμεθα** | |
| 2 pl | **ἐγέγραφθε** | |
| 3 pl | **γεγραμμένοι ἦσαν** | *periphrastic* |

> **The 3rd plural rule:** consonant stems cannot take ‑ντο (the cluster is unpronounceable), so Greek uses a **periphrasis**: perfect middle participle + ἦσαν. γεγραμμένοι ἦσαν "they had been written"; τεταγμένοι ἦσαν "they had been drawn up."

### 2.3 A Few High-Frequency Examples

| Verb | Perfect m/p | Pluperfect 3 sg | Gloss |
|---|---|---|---|
| παύω "stop" | πέπαυμαι | **ἐπέπαυτο** | had ceased |
| τάττω "arrange" | τέταγμαι | **ἐτέτακτο** | had been stationed |
| ποιέω "make" | πεποίημαι | **ἐπεποίητο** | had been made |
| τειχίζω "fortify" | τετείχισμαι | **ἐτετείχιστο** | had been walled |
| ἄγγελλω "report" | ἤγγελμαι | **ἤγγελτο** | had been announced |

## 3. Key Rules & Exceptions

| Rule | Explanation | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Reduplication AND augment** | The reduplication marks the perfect system; the augment marks past time. Both are present. | ἐ‑λε‑λύμην |
| **2. The augment is often omitted** | Especially in Herodotus and in poetry, and not rarely in Attic prose: λελύμην for ἐλελύμην. Do not be thrown by its absence. | |
| **3. Vowel-initial stems** | If the stem begins with a vowel, reduplication is already a lengthening, and no separate augment appears: ἠγγέλμην. | ἤγγελτο |
| **4. No thematic vowel** | Endings attach straight to the stem: ἐλέλυ‑το, not *ἐλελύ‑ε‑το. | |
| **5. Consonant-stem 3 pl is periphrastic** | participle + ἦσαν. This is the norm, not a rarity. | τεταγμένοι ἦσαν |
| **6. Middle or passive?** | One form serves both, as always in the perfect system. Context decides: ἐπέπαυτο "he had ceased" (middle) vs. "he had been stopped" (passive). | |
| **7. Distinguish from the aorist passive** | ἐλύθη "was loosed" (event) ≠ ἐλέλυτο "had been loosed" (state). | |

## 4. Example Sentences

| Greek | Translation |
|---|---|
| τὰ τείχη ἤδη **ἐτετείχιστο**. | "The walls had already been built." |
| αἱ νῆες ἐν τῷ λιμένι **ἐτετάχατο**. | "The ships had been drawn up in the harbour." (Ionic 3 pl) |
| ὁ πόλεμος οὔπω **ἐπέπαυτο**. | "The war had not yet ceased." |
| ταῦτα πάντα **ἐγέγραπτο** ἐν ταῖς σπονδαῖς. | "All this had been written in the treaty." |
| οἱ στρατιῶται **τεταγμένοι ἦσαν** πρὸ τῆς πόλεως. | "The soldiers had been stationed before the city." |

---

## 5. Usage & Context

1. **Background before narrative** — the pluperfect sets the stage: it tells you what was already true when the main action started. Thucydides opens episodes this way constantly.
2. **In subordinate clauses of time and cause** — ἐπειδὴ … ἐτετείχιστο "when it had been fortified …".
3. **State, not event** — if you can translate with "was (already) in the condition of having been X-ed," you have the force right.
4. **Relative rarity** — the pluperfect is much rarer than the aorist or imperfect; when an author reaches for it, the "already" is usually doing real work in the argument.
5. **The periphrasis is not second-class** — participle + ἦν/ἦσαν is the ordinary form for consonant stems and for many verbs generally.

---

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology

- The pluperfect is a **Greek innovation**: Indo-European had a perfect but no pluperfect. Greek built one by applying the past-tense apparatus (augment + secondary endings) to the existing perfect stem.
- The **middle/passive** pluperfect is the older and more regular half of the tense; the active pluperfect (ἐλελύκη, ‑κειν) developed its ‑κ‑ endings later and varied considerably between periods.
- The **periphrastic** type (participle + ἦσαν) steadily expanded, and in Koine it became the dominant pattern — an early sign of the analytic drift that shaped Modern Greek.
- The tense did not survive: Modern Greek expresses "had been done" with είχε + a non-finite form, a wholly new construction.
