# Lesson: The Present Indicative Active — Irregular Verbs

*Target level: Beginner → Intermediate*
*Prerequisites: Greek alphabet, present indicative active of ω-verbs and μι-verbs*

---

## 1. Which Verbs Belong Here

Most Greek verbs have a citation form in **-ω** (λύω), **-μι** (δίδωμι), or **-μαι** (βούλομαι). A small but very frequent group does not fit any of these classes. Two kinds dominate:

| Type | Members | Why they are irregular |
|---|---|---|
| **Impersonal verbs** | χρή "it is necessary", δεῖ "it is necessary", ἔξεστι "it is permitted" | Occur only in the 3rd person singular; no full paradigm |
| **Perfects with present meaning** | οἶδα "I know", ἄνωγα "I command" (poetic) | Perfect in form, present in force |

These few words carry an enormous load in real texts: χρή and δεῖ alone account for most "irregular present" tokens you will meet.

## 2. The Impersonals: χρή, δεῖ, ἔξεστι

Impersonal verbs have **no personal subject** — the subject is the situation itself. The person who "must" or "may" act appears in the **accusative** (with χρή, δεῖ) or **dative** (with ἔξεστι), followed by an **infinitive**:

| Verb | Construction | Example | Translation |
|---|---|---|---|
| **χρή** | acc. + inf. | χρή σε λέγειν | "you must speak" |
| **δεῖ** | acc. + inf. | δεῖ ἡμᾶς μάχεσθαι | "we must fight" |
| **ἔξεστι** | dat. + inf. | ἔξεστί σοι μένειν | "you may stay" |

Their small sets of forms:

| | Present | Imperfect | Future | Infinitive |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| χρή | χρή | χρῆν / ἐχρῆν | χρήσται (rare) | χρῆναι |
| δεῖ | δεῖ | ἔδει | δεήσει | δεῖν |
| ἔξεστι | ἔξεστι(ν) | ἐξῆν | ἐξέσται | ἐξεῖναι |

> **Nuance:** χρή and δεῖ both mean "it is necessary", but χρή leans toward *moral obligation* ("one ought"), δεῖ toward *practical necessity* ("it is needful"). δεῖ + genitive means "there is need of": δεῖ χρημάτων "money is needed".

## 3. Perfects with Present Meaning: οἶδα and ἄνωγα

**οἶδα** "I know" is morphologically a perfect (from the root ἰδ-/οἰδ-/εἰδ-, "see" — to have seen is to know), but its meaning is a true present. Its paradigm must simply be memorized:

| Person | Form | Gloss |
|---|---|---|
| 1 sg | **οἶδα** | I know |
| 2 sg | **οἶσθα** | you know |
| 3 sg | **οἶδε(ν)** | he/she/it knows |
| 1 pl | **ἴσμεν** | we know |
| 2 pl | **ἴστε** | you (pl.) know |
| 3 pl | **ἴσασι(ν)** | they know |

Infinitive **εἰδέναι**, participle **εἰδώς, εἰδυῖα, εἰδός**, imperfect-equivalent (pluperfect) **ᾔδη / ᾔδειν** "I knew".

**ἄνωγα** "I command, I bid" works the same way in epic and tragedy: perfect form, present meaning; 3rd singular **ἀνώγει** is common in Homer.

## 4. Key Rules & Common Pitfalls

| Rule | Explanation |
|---|---|
| **1. Impersonals stay in the 3rd singular** | Never look for χρῇς or δεῖς — the person appears as an accusative or dative, not in the verb ending. |
| **2. οἶσθα, not οἶδας** | The 2nd singular of οἶδα is οἶσθα (Attic); οἶδας appears only later. |
| **3. Plural of οἶδα changes stem** | Singular οἰδ-, plural ἰσ-: ἴσμεν, ἴστε, ἴσασι. Do not build ἴσμεν from λύω-endings. |
| **4. ἴστε is ambiguous** | ἴστε = "you know" (indicative) or "know!" (imperative). Context decides. |
| **5. χρή fuses with ἐστί and ἄν** | χρῆν = χρὴ + ἦν; χρῆσθαι "to use" (from χράομαι) is a different verb — do not confuse. |

## 5. Example Sentences

- χρὴ τοὺς πολίτας τοῖς νόμοις πείθεσθαι. — "The citizens must obey the laws."
- δεῖ σε ἀληθῆ λέγειν. — "You must tell the truth."
- ἔξεστιν ὑμῖν εἰρήνην ἄγειν. — "You are permitted to live in peace."
- οἶδα οὐδὲν εἰδώς. — "I know that I know nothing." (the Socratic paradox)
- ἴσασιν οἱ θεοὶ τὰ μέλλοντα. — "The gods know what is to come."

## 6. Usage Notes

1. **Frequency:** χρή and δεῖ are among the most common verbs in all of Greek literature. Learn their constructions (accusative + infinitive) until they are automatic.
2. **οἶδα vs. γιγνώσκω:** οἶδα is knowing a fact ("savoir"); γιγνώσκω is coming to know, recognizing ("connaître"). σύνοιδα + dative = "I share knowledge with, I am conscious of".
3. In **indirect questions**, οἶδα is everywhere: οὐκ οἶδα ὅστις ἐστίν "I do not know who he is".
