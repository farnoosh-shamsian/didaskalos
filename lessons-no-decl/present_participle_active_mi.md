# Lesson: The Present Active Participle (‑μι)  

*Target level: Beginner‑Intermediate*  
*Prerequisites: Greek alphabet, basic noun declension, present indicative active endings*  

---

## 1. What is the Present Active Participle?

The **present active participle** is a verbal adjective that expresses **simultaneous or ongoing action** of the verb’s subject.  
- It answers the question *“who/what is doing …?”* (like “the one who is …”).  
- It has the **meaning of a present‑tense verb** but behaves **like an adjective**: it is declined for gender, number, and case.  

In Ancient Greek the present active participle ends in **‑μι** (pronounced *‑mee*) for the **masculine and neuter** and **‑ζῳ** (‑zōi) for the **feminine** in the nominative singular. The suffix **‑μι** is attached to the **present stem** of the verb (the same stem that appears in the present indicative).

> **Example** – from *λύω* “to loosen, release”  
> - Present stem: **λυ‑**  
> - Present active participle: **λυ‑** + **‑μι** → **λύμι** “the one who is loosening” (masc./neut.)  
> - Feminine nominative: **λυ‑** + **‑ζῳ** → **λύζῳ** “the one who is loosening” (fem.)

---

## 2. Forms and Patterns  

### 2.1. The Basic Paradigm  

The present active participle is declined like a **first‑/second‑declension adjective** (the same pattern as *ἀγαθός* “good”).  
Below is the full paradigm for a **regular consonant‑stem verb** (e.g., *γράφω* “to write”).  

| Case | Masculine Singular | Feminine Singular | Neuter Singular | Masculine Plural | Feminine Plural | Neuter Plural |
|------|-------------------|-------------------|----------------|------------------|-----------------|---------------|
| **Nominative** | **γράφ‑** **‑ων** (γράφων) | **γράφ‑** **‑ουσα** (γραφουσα) | **γράφ‑** **‑ον** (γραφον) | **γράφ‑** **‑οντες** (γραφοντες) | **γράφ‑** **‑ουσαι** (γραφουσαι) | **γράφ‑** **‑οντα** (γραφοντα) |
| **Genitive** | **γράφ‑** **‑οντος** | **γράφ‑** **‑ουσης** | **γράφ‑** **‑οντος** | **γράφ‑** **‑οντων** | **γράφ‑** **‑ουσῶν** | **γράφ‑** **‑οντων** |
| **Dative** | **γράφ‑** **‑οντι** | **γράφ‑** **‑ουσῃ** | **γράφ‑** **‑οντι** | **γράφ‑** **‑ουσι(ν)** | **γράφ‑** **‑ουσῖν** | **γράφ‑** **‑ουσι(ν)** |
| **Accusative** | **γράφ‑** **‑οντα** | **γράφ‑** **‑ουσαν** | **γράφ‑** **‑ον** | **γράφ‑** **‑οντας** | **γράφ‑** **‑ουσας** | **γράφ‑** **‑οντα** |
| **Vocative** | **γράφ‑** **‑ων** | **γράφ‑** **‑ουσα** | **γράφ‑** **‑ον** | **γράφ‑** **‑οντες** | **γράφ‑** **‑ουσαι** | **γράφ‑** **‑οντα** |

**Key points**  

* The **masculine and neuter** share the same ending in the nominative singular (**‑ων**, **‑ον**).  
* The **feminine** uses **‑ουσα** (or **‑ζῳ** in older poetry).  
* The **plural** endings are the same as those of a regular adjective of the first/second declension.  

### 2.2. Contract Verbs  

Verbs whose present stem ends in a vowel (contract verbs) have a slightly different shape.  

| Verb | Present Stem | Masculine Nom. Sing. | Feminine Nom. Sing. | Neuter Nom. Sing. |
|------|--------------|----------------------|---------------------|-------------------|
| **φιλέω** “to love” | **φιλε‑** | **φιλε‑** **‑ων** → **φιλῶν** | **φιλε‑** **‑ουσα** → **φιλοῦσα** | **φιλε‑** **‑ον** → **φιλόν** |
| **πείθω** “to persuade” | **πείθε‑** | **πείθε‑** **‑ων** → **πείθων** | **πείθε‑** **‑ουσα** → **πείθουσα** | **πείθε‑** **‑ον** → **πείθον** |

*The vowel of the stem contracts with the **‑μι** suffix, producing long vowels (‑ῶν, ‑οῦσα, ‑όν).*

### 2.3. Irregular Stems  

Some verbs have irregular present stems, but the **‑μι** suffix is still added.  

| Verb | Present Stem | Masculine Nom. Sing. | Feminine Nom. Sing. | Neuter Nom. Sing. |
|------|--------------|----------------------|---------------------|-------------------|
| **εἰμί** “to be” | **εἰ‑** | **εἰ‑** **‑ων** → **εἰών** (rare, poetic) |
| **ἔρχομαι** “to come” | **εἰσ‑** (or **εἰσ‑**) | **εἰσ‑** **‑ων** → **εἰσών** (poetic) |

These forms are **rare** and usually appear only in poetry or later prose.

## 3. Key Rules & Exceptions  

| Rule | Explanation | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **1. Use the present stem** | Attach **‑μι** (or **‑ζῳ**) to the same stem that appears in the present indicative. | *λύω* → stem **λυ‑** → *λύμι* |
| **2. Gender agreement** | The participle must agree with the noun it modifies in gender, number, and case. | *ὁ γράφων ἀνὴρ* “the man who is writing” (masc. nom.) |
| **3. Contract verbs contract** | When the stem ends in **‑ε, ‑α, ‑ο**, the vowel contracts with **‑μι** producing **‑ῶν, ‑οῦσα, ‑όν**. | *φιλέω* → *φιλῶν* |
| **4. Feminine nominative in poetry** | Older poetry sometimes uses **‑ζῳ** instead of **‑ουσα** (e.g., *παιδεζῳ*). | *παιδεζῳ* = *παιδεῦσα* “teaching (fem.)” |
| **5. No participle for some verbs** | Verbs that lack a present stem (e.g., *ἔρχομαι* “to come” has a middle form) form the participle from the **middle** stem, yielding a **present middle‑passive participle** (*ἐρχόμενος*). The active present participle is therefore **absent**. | – |
| **6. Spurious diphthongs** | In some forms the diphthong **ει** or **ου** may be “spurious” (pronounced long). This does not affect the participle endings but is useful for pronunciation. | *ποιῶν* (from *ποιέω*) – the **οι** is a genuine diphthong. |
| **7. Accentuation** | The accent falls on the **penult** if it is long; otherwise on the **antepenult**. The participle follows the same rule as adjectives. | *γραφῶν* (accent on the antepenult because the penult **‑ων** is short). |

## 4. Example Sentences  

| Greek (with transliteration) | Translation |
|------------------------------|-------------|
| **Ὁ ἀνὴρ γράφων τὸν λόγον ἐστὶ σοφός.** <br> *Ho anēr graphōn ton logon estin sophos.* | “The man who is writing the word is wise.” |
| **Ἡ γυνὴ ἀκούουσα τὴν μουσικήν χαίρει.** <br> *Hē gynē akouousa tēn mousikēn chairei.* | “The woman who is listening to the music is glad.” |
| **Τὰ παιδία παιδονταὶ ἐν τῷ κήπῳ παίζουσιν.** <br> *Ta paidia paidontai en tōi kēpōi paizousin.* | “The children who are playing in the garden are playing.” |
| **Ἀνδρῶν ἐν τῇ πόλει ὁρῶντες τὸν πόλεμον ἐφοβήθησαν.** <br> *Andrōn en tēi polei horontes ton polemon ephobēthēsan.* | “The men who were seeing the war in the city were frightened.” (Note: past participle *ὁρῶντες* is a present active participle used for a past sense.) |

## 5. Usage & Context  

1. **Temporal simultaneity** – The present active participle indicates an action **occurring at the same time** as the main verb.  
   - *Ἀνὴρ ἔρχεται γράφων.* “A man comes while writing.”  

2. **Circumstantial (adverbial) use** – Often translated with “while” or “when”.  
   - *Παιδεύων ὁ διδάσκαλος ἐπιδέχεται τὸν μαθητὴν.* “The teacher, while teaching, receives the student.”  

3. **Attributive use** – Functions like an adjective, directly modifying a noun.  
   - *Ἡ ἀγνοεῖσα γυνή* “the woman who does not know.”  

4. **Nominal (substantive) use** – The participle can stand alone as a noun, especially in the neuter plural.  
   - *Τὰ γράφοντα* “those who are writing.”  

5. **Stylistic notes** –  
   - In **poetry** the participle is frequent for compact expression.  
   - In **prose** (especially classical Attic) the participle is used more sparingly; the finite verb often carries the temporal meaning.

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology  

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Proto‑Indo‑European origin** | The participial suffix **‑mi** derives from the PIE *‑mi* participial marker, which also gave the Latin **‑ns** (present active participle) and the Sanskrit **‑ti**. |
| **Old Greek** | Early Greek (Homeric) shows the participle in both **‑μι** (masc./neut.) and **‑ζῳ** (fem.) forms. The **‑ζῳ** form later gave way to **‑ουσα** in Classical Greek. |
| **Classical period** | The present active participle becomes a regular adjective‑type declension, fully integrated into the first/second declension paradigm. |
| **Koiné and later Greek** | The participle remains productive, though the **‑ζῳ** form disappears; the **‑ουσα** form dominates. The participle is also used in the **future** and **aorist** tenses, but the **present active** retains its core meaning of simultaneous action. |
| **Etymology of the suffix** | The Greek **‑μι** is cognate with the Latin **‑ns** (as in *amans* “loving”) and the Sanskrit **‑ti** (as in *kṛti* “doing”). The suffix originally expressed an *agentive* meaning “the one who does”. |
