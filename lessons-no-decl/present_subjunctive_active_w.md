# Lesson: The Present Subjunctive Active (‑ω Verbs)

*Level: beginner–intermediate*
*Prerequisites: the Greek alphabet, basic noun declension, the present indicative active endings.*

---

## 1. What the Present Subjunctive Active Is

The **subjunctive mood** expresses an action that is *potential* or *non-factual*: purpose, condition, exhortation, or indefinite clauses.
The **present** subjunctive has imperfective aspect — a continuous or repeated action, simultaneous with or subsequent to the main verb.
When the verb is **active**, the subject performs the action itself.

The mark of the subjunctive in Greek is the **lengthening of the thematic vowel**: ο/ε give way to **ω/η**.

| Person | Ending (regular) |
|--------|------------------|
| 1st sg. | ‑ω |
| 2nd sg. | ‑ῃς |
| 3rd sg. | ‑ῃ |
| 1st pl. | ‑ωμεν |
| 2nd pl. | ‑ητε |
| 3rd pl. | ‑ωσι(ν) |

These endings are added to the **present stem** (the same stem as the present indicative).

> **Key point:** the present subjunctive **never takes the augment** (the "ἐ‑" that appears in the past tenses). It looks like the present indicative but with a long vowel: indicative λύομεν → subjunctive λύωμεν.

---

## 2. Forms and Patterns

### 2.1 Regular Thematic Verbs — *λύω* "to loose"

| | Singular | Plural |
|--------|----------|--------|
| **1st** | λύ**ω** | λύ**ωμεν** |
| **2nd** | λύ**ῃς** | λύ**ητε** |
| **3rd** | λύ**ῃ** | λύ**ωσι(ν)** |

*Note:* the 1st singular subjunctive is identical in form to the indicative (λύω); context decides.

### 2.2 Contract Verbs — *φιλέω* "to love"

The stem vowel contracts with the long vowel of the ending (ε + ω → ω; ε + ῃ → ῇ):

| Person | Form | Explanation |
|--------|------|-------------|
| 1st sg. | **φιλῶ** | φιλε‑ + ‑ω → φιλῶ |
| 2nd sg. | **φιλῇς** | φιλε‑ + ‑ῃς → φιλῇς |
| 3rd sg. | **φιλῇ** | φιλε‑ + ‑ῃ → φιλῇ |
| 1st pl. | **φιλῶμεν** | φιλε‑ + ‑ωμεν → φιλῶμεν |
| 2nd pl. | **φιλῆτε** | φιλε‑ + ‑ητε → φιλῆτε |
| 3rd pl. | **φιλῶσι(ν)** | φιλε‑ + ‑ωσι(ν) → φιλῶσι(ν) |

In α‑contracts (τιμάω) every form is built with ᾳ/ω: τιμῶ, τιμᾷς, τιμᾷ, τιμῶμεν, τιμᾶτε, τιμῶσι(ν) — identical to the indicative; again, context decides.

### 2.3 ‑μι Verbs — *δίδωμι* "to give"

In the subjunctive the ‑μι verbs also take the long subjunctive vowel, built on the reduplicated stem:

| Person | Present subjunctive | Note |
|--------|---------------------|------|
| 1st sg. | **διδῶ** | The unreduplicated form δῶ is **aorist**, not present! |
| 2nd sg. | **διδῷς** | |
| 3rd sg. | **διδῷ** | |
| 1st pl. | **διδῶμεν** | |
| 2nd pl. | **διδῶτε** | |
| 3rd pl. | **διδῶσι(ν)** | |

### 2.4 The Irregular Verb **εἰμί** "to be"

The present subjunctive of εἰμί is the bare set of long endings:

| Person | Form |
|--------|------|
| 1st sg. | **ὦ** |
| 2nd sg. | **ᾖς** |
| 3rd sg. | **ᾖ** |
| 1st pl. | **ὦμεν** |
| 2nd pl. | **ἦτε** |
| 3rd pl. | **ὦσι(ν)** |

## 3. Key Rules & Common Exceptions

| Rule | Explanation |
|------|-------------|
| **Choice of stem** | Use the **present indicative stem** (the part before ‑ω). |
| **No augment** | The present subjunctive never takes the augment ἐ‑. |
| **The main sign: a long vowel** | ω/η instead of ο/ε: λύομεν (indicative) vs. λύωμεν (subjunctive). |
| **Contract verbs** | Contraction as in the indicative, but with long vowels: φιλῶμεν, φιλῆτε. |
| **‑μι verbs** | The present subjunctive keeps the reduplication: διδῶ (present) ≠ δῶ (aorist). |
| **Irregulars** | εἰμί has special forms (§2.4). |
| **3rd plural** | ‑ωσι or ‑ωσιν; the ν is movable. |
| **Iota subscript** | Always written in ‑ῃς and ‑ῃ, and a reliable sign of the subjunctive. |

## 4. Example Sentences

| Greek (present subjunctive) | Transliteration | English |
|-----------------------------|-----------------|---------|
| **ἵνα λύῃς τὸν δεσμόν** | *hína lýēis ton desmón* | "so that you may loose the bond" |
| **μανθάνομεν ἵνα σοφοὶ ὦμεν.** | *manthánomen hína sophoì ômen* | "We learn in order to be wise." |
| **ἐὰν ζητῇς, εὑρήσεις.** | *eàn zētêis, heurḗseis* | "If you seek, you will find." |
| **γράφωμεν.** | *gráphōmen* | "Let us write!" (hortatory subjunctive) |
| **τί ποιῶμεν;** | *tí poiômen?* | "What are we to do?" (deliberative question) |

*Note the use of **ἵνα** (purpose) and **ἐάν** (condition) introducing subjunctive clauses.*

---

## 5. Usage & Context

| Context | Trigger words / structures |
|---------|----------------------------|
| **Purpose** | *ἵνα, ὅπως* + subjunctive: *ἵνα γράφῃς* "so that you may write" |
| **Indefinite clauses** | relative pronoun/adverb + **ἄν** + subjunctive: *ὃς ἂν λέγῃ* "whoever speaks," *ὅταν* "whenever" |
| **Future more vivid conditions** | *ἐάν* + subjunctive: *ἐὰν ἔλθῃς* "if you come" |
| **Hortatory** | 1st person plural with no particle: *γράφωμεν* "let us write" |
| **Deliberative question** | 1st person: *τί ποιῶμεν;* "what are we to do?" |
| **Clauses of fearing** | *φοβοῦμαι μή…* "I am afraid that…" |

The subjunctive is **not** used for a simple statement of fact; that is the domain of the indicative. It always carries a nuance of *possibility*, *will*, or *indefiniteness*.

---

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **Indo-European origin** | The Indo-European subjunctive was formed with a **lengthened thematic vowel**; Greek continues this directly (ω/η against ο/ε). |
| **Development in Greek** | In Homer the subjunctive sometimes has a future-like sense; by the classical period it is the mood of purpose, probable condition, and exhortation. |
| **Relation to the other moods** | The subjunctive (long vowel) and the optative (suffix ‑οι‑/‑ιη‑) are the two "non-factual" moods of Greek; as the optative receded in Koine, the subjunctive took over its functions. |
| **Later fate** | In Modern Greek the constructions with να + subjunctive (from ἵνα) replaced the infinitive — a direct inheritance from this mood. |
