# Lesson: The Present Subjunctive Active (‑ω)  

*Target level: Beginner‑Intermediate*  
*Prerequisites: Greek alphabet, basic noun declension, present indicative active endings.*

---

## 1. What is the Present Subjunctive Active?

The **subjunctive mood** expresses a *potential* or *non‑real* action: a wish, a purpose, a condition, or an indefinite/relative clause.  
The **present** subjunctive refers to an action that is **simultaneous with or subsequent to** the main verb’s time.  

When the verb is **active**, the subject of the verb performs the action (as opposed to passive or middle voice).

In Ancient Greek the present active subjunctive is formed with the **ending ‑ω** in the 1st person singular and a set of characteristic endings for the other persons:

| Person | Ending (regular) |
|--------|------------------|
| 1 sg.  | ‑ω               |
| 2 sg.  | ‑ῃς / ‑ῃ        |
| 3 sg.  | ‑ῃ               |
| 1 pl.  | ‑ωμεν            |
| 2 pl.  | ‑ητε             |
| 3 pl.  | ‑ωσι(ν)          |

These endings are added to the **present stem** (the same stem used for the present indicative).  

> **Key point:** The present subjunctive **does not take an augment** (the “ε‑” that appears in past tenses). It looks like the present indicative but with the subjunctive endings.

---

## 2. Forms and Patterns  

### 2.1 Regular (thematic) verbs – *λύω* “to loose”

|          | Singular | Plural |
|----------|----------|--------|
| **1st**  | λύ**ω** | λύ**ωμεν** |
| **2nd**  | λύ**ῃς** | λύ**ητε** |
| **3rd**  | λύ**ῃ** | λύ**ωσι(ν)** |

*Note:* The accent stays on the stem (λυ‑) because the subjunctive endings are “light” (no long vowel).  

### 2.2 Contract verbs – *φιλέω* “to love”

Contract verbs combine the vowel of the stem with the vowel of the ending. The pattern depends on the type of contraction.

| Person | Form | Explanation |
|--------|------|-------------|
| 1 sg. | **φιλοῦ** | *φιλε‑* + ‑ω → *φιλε‑ω* → contraction *φιλοῦ* |
| 2 sg. | **φιλεῖς** | *φιλε‑* + ‑ῃς → *φιλε‑ῃς* → *φιλεῖς* |
| 3 sg. | **φιλεῖ** | *φιλε‑* + ‑ῃ → *φιλε‑ῃ* → *φιλεῖ* |
| 1 pl. | **φιλοῦμεν** | *φιλε‑* + ‑ωμεν → *φιλε‑ωμεν* → *φιλοῦμεν* |
| 2 pl. | **φιλεῖτε** | *φιλε‑* + ‑ητε → *φιλε‑ητε* → *φιλεῖτε* |
| 3 pl. | **φιλοῦσι(ν)** | *φιλε‑* + ‑ωσι(ν) → *φιλε‑ωσι(ν)* → *φιλοῦσι(ν)* |

### 2.3 Verbs ending in **‑μι** – *δίδωμι* “to give”

These verbs have a special present stem ending in **‑δω‑** and keep the **‑μι** in the indicative, but the subjunctive uses the regular endings:

| Person | Form |
|--------|------|
| 1 sg. | **δῶ** |
| 2 sg. | **δῷς** |
| 3 sg. | **δῷ** |
| 1 pl. | **δῶμεν** |
| 2 pl. | **δῆτε** |
| 3 pl. | **δῶσι(ν)** |

### 2.4 Irregular verb **εἰμί** “to be”

The present subjunctive of *εἰμί* is **ὦ** (1 sg.) and the rest of the paradigm is built on the stem *ὤ‑*:

| Person | Form |
|--------|------|
| 1 sg. | **ὦ** |
| 2 sg. | **ᾖς** |
| 3 sg. | **ᾖ** |
| 1 pl. | **ὦμεν** |
| 2 pl. | **ἦτε** |
| 3 pl. | **ὦσι(ν)** |

## 3. Key Rules & Common Exceptions  

| Rule | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Stem selection** | Use the **present indicative stem** (the form you would have before adding *‑ω* in the indicative). |
| **No augment** | Unlike the aorist subjunctive, the present subjunctive never receives the augment *ε‑*. |
| **Accent** | The accent stays on the stem because the subjunctive endings are “light”. |
| **Contract verbs** | Vowel contraction follows the same rules as in the indicative (e.g., *φιλε‑* + ‑ω → *φιλοῦ*). |
| **‑μι verbs** | Drop the *‑μι* and use the thematic stem (e.g., *δίδωμι* → *δ‑*). |
| **Irregular verbs** | *εἰμί* and a few others have unique forms (see §2.4). |
| **3rd‑person plural** | The ending may appear as **‑ωσι** or **‑ωσιν**; the final *‑ν* is optional and often omitted in prose. |
| **Spelling of 2nd‑sg.** | The ending can be written **‑ῃς** (with iota subscript) or **‑ῃ** (if the vowel already carries a iota subscript). Both are correct. |

## 4. Example Sentences  

| Greek (present subjunctive) | Transliteration | English |
|-----------------------------|----------------|---------|
| **ἵνα λύῃς τὸν δεσμὸν** | hína lyḗis tòn desmòn | “so that you may loose the bond.” |
| **βούλομαι ἵνα φιλοῦμεν** | boúlomai hína philoûmen | “I wish that we may love (each other).” |
| **ἔσται ὅπως δῶμεν τὸ δῶρον** | estai hópōs dômen tὸ dôron | “It will be that we give the gift.” |
| **Ἐλπίζω ἵνα ὦσιν ὑγιαίνοι** | Elpízō hína ōsin hygáinoi | “I hope that they are healthy.” |

*Notice the use of **ἵνα** (purpose/indirect command) and **ἔσται** (future of *εἰμί*) introducing a subjunctive clause.*

---

## 5. Usage & Context  

| Context | Typical trigger words/structures |
|---------|-----------------------------------|
| **Purpose / indirect command** | *ἵνα, ὅπως* + subjunctive (e.g., *ἵνα γράφῃς* “so that you may write”) |
| **Indefinite or relative clauses** | *ὅς, ἥ, ὅ* + subjunctive (e.g., *ὁ ἀνὴρ ὃς λύῃ* “the man who may loose”) |
| **Conditional clauses** (future more vivid) | *ἐὰν* + subjunctive (e.g., *ἐὰν ἔλθῃς* “if you should come”) |
| **Hortatory / exhortative** | *ναί* + subjunctive (rare) or *ἔχω* + subjunctive in poetry |
| **After verbs of fearing, wishing, commanding** | *φοβέομαι ἵνα...*, *βούλομαι ἵνα...* |
| **In subordinate clauses after *ὅτι* when the meaning is potential** | *λέγει ὅτι ἔλθῃ* “he says that he may come” |

The present subjunctive is **not** used for simple statements of fact; that is the domain of the indicative. It always carries a nuance of *potentiality* or *non‑reality*.

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology  

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| **Proto‑Indo‑European origin** | The subjunctive suffix *‑y‑* (a “y‑grade”) gave rise to the Greek *‑ω* endings. It originally expressed *potential* or *optative* meaning. |
| **Evolution in Greek** | Early Greek (e.g., Homeric) used the **optative** for many of the functions later taken over by the subjunctive. By Classical times the subjunctive became the primary mood for purpose, indirect command, and future‑more‑vivid conditionals. |
| **Phonological change** | The original *‑y‑* caused vowel lengthening and contraction, which is why we see forms like *φιλοῦ* (from *φιλε‑ω*) and *δῶ* (from *δ‑ + ω*). |
| **Relation to other moods** | The subjunctive shares its endings with the **present indicative** except for the 1st‑sg. *‑ω* (instead of *‑ω* in the indicative) and the vowel changes in 2nd‑sg. and 3rd‑sg. The **optative** (‑οιμι, ‑οις, ‑οι, ‑οιμεν, ‑οιτε, ‑οιεν) is a later, more “wish‑like” form. |
| **Etymology of the ending ‑ω** | The *‑ω* ending derives from the PIE *‑ō* (first‑person singular present active) combined with the subjunctive *‑y‑* suffix, yielding a long *‑ō* in Greek. |
