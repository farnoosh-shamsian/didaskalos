# Lesson: The Perfect Active Participle of ‑μι Verbs

*Target level: Intermediate*
*Prerequisites: Perfect indicative active of ‑μι verbs, perfect active participle of ω-verbs.*

---

## 1. What This Form Is

The **perfect active participle** of a ‑μι verb describes someone **in the state resulting from having done** something:

> **δεδωκώς** "having given" · **τεθηκώς** "having placed" · **ἑστώς** "standing"

The endings are the ordinary perfect ones — **‑ώς, ‑υῖα, ‑ός**, genitive **‑ότος** — so nothing new has to be learned there. What is distinctive is the **stem**, and above all the fact that the most important member of the class, **ἵστημι**, has a **contracted** form that looks nothing like the others.

| Verb | Perfect | Participle | Meaning |
|---|---|---|---|
| δίδωμι | δέδωκα | **δεδωκώς** | having given |
| τίθημι | τέθηκα | **τεθηκώς** | having placed |
| ἵστημι | ἕστηκα | **ἑστώς** (also ἑστηκώς) | **standing** |
| ἵημι | εἷκα | **εἱκώς** (usually compound: ἀφεικώς) | having released |
| δείκνυμι | δέδειχα | **δεδειχώς** | having shown |

## 2. Forms and Patterns

### 2.1 The Regular Type — **δεδωκώς**

Perfect stem δεδωκ‑ + the perfect participle endings:

| Case | Masculine | Feminine | Neuter |
|---|---|---|---|
| Nom. sg. | **δεδωκώς** | **δεδωκυῖα** | **δεδωκός** |
| Gen. sg. | δεδωκότος | δεδωκυίας | δεδωκότος |
| Dat. sg. | δεδωκότι | δεδωκυίᾳ | δεδωκότι |
| Acc. sg. | δεδωκότα | δεδωκυῖαν | δεδωκός |
| Nom. pl. | δεδωκότες | δεδωκυῖαι | δεδωκότα |
| Gen. pl. | δεδωκότων | δεδωκυιῶν | δεδωκότων |
| Dat. pl. | δεδωκόσι(ν) | δεδωκυίαις | δεδωκόσι(ν) |
| Acc. pl. | δεδωκότας | δεδωκυίας | δεδωκότα |

Masculine and neuter follow the third declension (‑οτ‑ stem); the feminine follows the first.

### 2.2 The Contracted Type — **ἑστώς** "standing"

This is the one you will actually meet most often. ἕστηκα means "I stand" (a present state), and its participle is usually contracted:

| Case | Masculine | Feminine | Neuter |
|---|---|---|---|
| Nom. sg. | **ἑστώς** | **ἑστῶσα** | **ἑστός** |
| Gen. sg. | ἑστῶτος | ἑστώσης | ἑστῶτος |
| Dat. sg. | ἑστῶτι | ἑστώσῃ | ἑστῶτι |
| Acc. sg. | ἑστῶτα | ἑστῶσαν | ἑστός |
| Nom. pl. | ἑστῶτες | ἑστῶσαι | ἑστῶτα |

The uncontracted **ἑστηκώς** also occurs and declines regularly.

> **Meaning note:** ἑστώς is not "having stood" but simply **"standing"** — the perfect of ἵστημι has present force, like οἶδα "I know." Similarly τεθνηκώς "dead," not "having died."

### 2.3 Compounds

Compounds are commoner than the simple verbs in prose:

| Compound | Participle | Gloss |
|---|---|---|
| ἀποδίδωμι | **ἀποδεδωκώς** | having paid back |
| παραδίδωμι | **παραδεδωκώς** | having surrendered |
| ἀφίστημι | **ἀφεστώς** | in revolt |
| καθίστημι | **καθεστώς** | established, current |

> **τὰ καθεστῶτα** "the existing state of affairs" is a standing idiom of Thucydidean prose, and **ἀφεστώς** "in revolt" recurs throughout his narrative of the allies.

## 3. Key Rules & Exceptions

| Rule | Explanation | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Endings are the ordinary perfect ones** | ‑ώς, ‑υῖα, ‑ός; gen. ‑ότος. Same as λελυκώς. | |
| **2. Reduplication is kept** | δέ‑δωκα → δεδωκώς; ἕ‑στηκα → ἑστώς. | |
| **3. ‑κ‑ in δίδωμι, τίθημι, ἵστημι** | These form a κ‑perfect: δέδωκα, τέθηκα, ἕστηκα. | |
| **4. ἵστημι contracts** | ἑστώς, ἑστῶσα, ἑστός beside fuller ἑστηκώς. Learn the short form: it is the commoner. | |
| **5. Present meaning for ἵστημι** | ἑστώς = "standing," not "having stood." | |
| **6. Accent on the ending** | ‑ώς and ‑ός are always accented — a quick recognition cue. | |
| **7. Feminine in ‑υῖα, never ‑ουσα** | δεδωκυῖα, ἑστῶσα (contracted). | |

## 4. Example Sentences

| Greek | Translation |
|---|---|
| ὁ τὰ χρήματα **δεδωκὼς** τιμᾶται. | "The man who has given the money is honoured." |
| οἱ φύλακες **ἑστῶτες** πρὸ τῶν πυλῶν ἐφύλαττον. | "The guards, standing before the gates, kept watch." |
| αἱ πόλεις αἱ **ἀφεστῶσαι** οὐκ ἐπείθοντο. | "The cities in revolt would not obey." |
| τὰ **καθεστῶτα** οὐκ ἤρεσκεν αὐτοῖς. | "The existing order did not please them." |
| νόμους **τεθηκὼς** δικαίους ἐπῃνέθη. | "Having laid down just laws, he was praised." |

## 5. Usage & Context

1. **Describing a resulting state** — this is the core force: ἑστώς "standing," ἀφεστώς "in revolt," καθεστώς "established."
2. **Substantival with the article** — οἱ ἀφεστῶτες "the rebels"; τὰ καθεστῶτα "the present circumstances."
3. **Attributive** — αἱ ἀφεστῶσαι πόλεις "the cities that have revolted."
4. **Adverbial and genitive absolute** — τῶν πόλεων ἀφεστωσῶν "the cities being in revolt."
5. **Periphrastic constructions** — with εἰμί: ἀφεστῶτες ἦσαν "they were in revolt."

---

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology

- The perfect participle suffix is Indo-European *‑wos‑/‑us‑ (Sanskrit ‑vāṃs‑), which in Greek yields ‑ώς with the oblique stem ‑ότ‑ and the feminine ‑υῖα.
- The **κ‑perfect** (δέδωκα, τέθηκα, ἕστηκα) is a Greek innovation that spread from a small group of roots ending in a vowel; the ‑μι verbs were its earliest home.
- The **contracted ἑστώς** results from the loss of κ and contraction of the vowels — a change already complete in classical Attic, which is why the short form dominates.
- The perfect's **stative meaning** (ἑστώς "standing," τεθνηκώς "dead," εἰδώς "knowing") is the original Indo-European value of the category; the "have done" sense is a later Greek development.
