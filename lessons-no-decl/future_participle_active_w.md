# Lesson: The Future Active Participle of ω-Verbs

*Target level: Intermediate*
*Prerequisites: Present active participle, future indicative active.*

---

## 1. What This Form Is

The **future active participle** is a verbal adjective meaning "about to do," "going to do," "intending to do":

> **λύσων** "about to loose" · **ποιήσων** "going to do" · **ἐρῶν** "about to speak"

It is the one participle in Greek that genuinely refers to **time** rather than aspect: it looks forward from the moment of the main verb.

Its single most important job is to express **purpose**, especially with verbs of motion:

> ἦλθον **λύσοντες** τοὺς αἰχμαλώτους. — "They came to release the prisoners."

This is a construction you must be able to recognise instantly; it is far commoner in prose than ἵνα-clauses after verbs of going and sending.

---

## 2. Forms and Patterns

### 2.1 Formation

Take the **future stem** (the second principal part, minus ‑ω) and add the ordinary present-participle endings **‑ων, ‑ουσα, ‑ον**:

| Verb | Future indicative | Future participle (m./f./n.) |
|---|---|---|
| λύω "loose" | λύσω | **λύσων, λύσουσα, λῦσον** |
| παιδεύω "educate" | παιδεύσω | **παιδεύσων, παιδεύσουσα, παιδεῦσον** |
| ποιέω "make" | ποιήσω | **ποιήσων, ποιήσουσα, ποιῆσον** |
| γράφω "write" | γράψω | **γράψων, γράψουσα, γράψον** |
| ἄγω "lead" | ἄξω | **ἄξων, ἄξουσα, ἄξον** |

### 2.2 Declension (model **λύσων**)

It declines exactly like the present participle — masculine and neuter third declension (ντ‑stem), feminine first declension:

| Case | Masculine | Feminine | Neuter |
|---|---|---|---|
| Nom. sg. | λύσων | λύσουσα | λῦσον |
| Gen. sg. | λύσοντος | λυσούσης | λύσοντος |
| Dat. sg. | λύσοντι | λυσούσῃ | λύσοντι |
| Acc. sg. | λύσοντα | λύσουσαν | λῦσον |
| Nom. pl. | λύσοντες | λύσουσαι | λύσοντα |
| Gen. pl. | λυσόντων | λυσουσῶν | λυσόντων |
| Dat. pl. | λύσουσι(ν) | λυσούσαις | λύσουσι(ν) |
| Acc. pl. | λύσοντας | λυσούσας | λύσοντα |

*If you know λύων, λύοντος, you already know λύσων, λύσοντος. Only the σ is new.*

### 2.3 Liquid Futures — Contracted

Verbs whose stem ends in λ, μ, ν, ρ lose the σ and contract, giving a participle that looks like an ε‑contract:

| Verb | Future | Future participle |
|---|---|---|
| μένω "remain" | μενῶ | **μενῶν, μενοῦσα, μενοῦν** |
| ἀγγέλλω "report" | ἀγγελῶ | **ἀγγελῶν, ἀγγελοῦσα, ἀγγελοῦν** |
| λέγω "say" | ἐρῶ | **ἐρῶν, ἐροῦσα, ἐροῦν** |
| ἀποκτείνω "kill" | ἀποκτενῶ | **ἀποκτενῶν** |

---

## 3. Key Rules & Exceptions

| Rule | Explanation | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **1. Built on the future stem** | Learn it with the second principal part: λύσω → λύσων. | |
| **2. Endings are those of the present participle** | ‑ων, ‑ουσα, ‑ον. The σ (or the contraction) is the only marker of futurity. | |
| **3. It really does mark time** | Unlike the present and aorist participles, which mark aspect, the future participle looks forward. | |
| **4. ὡς + future participle = alleged purpose** | ὡς makes the purpose the *subject's own stated* intention: ὡς λύσων "on the grounds that he intended to release." | |
| **5. Agreement as always** | Gender, number, case with its noun. | |
| **6. No future participle for deponents in the active** | Deponent verbs have a future middle participle instead: βουλόμενος → βουλησόμενος. | |
| **7. Distinguish λύσων from λύσας** | λύσων "about to loose" (future) vs. λύσας "having loosed" (aorist). One letter, opposite time. | |

---

## 4. Example Sentences

| Greek | Translation |
|---|---|
| ἦλθον **λύσοντες** τοὺς αἰχμαλώτους. | "They came to release the prisoners." |
| ἔπεμψαν πρέσβεις **ἐροῦντας** ταῦτα. | "They sent envoys to say this." |
| συνέλαβον αὐτὸν ὡς **ἀποκτενοῦντα** τὸν βασιλέα. | "They arrested him on the grounds that he intended to kill the king." |
| ὁ **μενῶν** ἐν τῇ πόλει ἀσφαλὴς ἔσται. | "The one who is going to stay in the city will be safe." |
| οἱ **ποιήσοντες** ταῦτα ἤδη πάρεισιν. | "Those who are going to do this are already here." |

---

## 5. Usage & Context

1. **Purpose after verbs of motion** — ἔρχομαι, πέμπω, ἥκω + future participle. This is the primary use and the reason the form exists in prose at all.
2. **With ὡς** — marks the purpose as the subject's alleged or professed intention, not the narrator's assertion. Thucydides uses ὡς + future participle constantly when reporting motives he does not vouch for.
3. **Attributive** — with the article: ὁ ποιήσων "the man who will do it," ὁ ἐρῶν "the coming speaker."
4. **Substantival** — οἱ μαχούμενοι "those who are going to fight."
5. **After verbs of expecting** — μέλλω is the exception: it takes an **infinitive**, not a participle (μέλλω λύσειν "I am about to loose").

---

## 6. Historical Development & Etymology

- The future participle is a Greek innovation, formed by simply applying the participial suffix *‑ont‑ to the future stem — a neat piece of paradigm-building rather than an inherited category.
- The **σ** of the future continues the Indo-European desiderative *‑sye/o‑ ("be about to, want to"), which is exactly why the participle so readily expresses intention.
- The **liquid futures** (μενῶ, ἐρῶ) lost their σ between vowels and contracted, the same sound change visible throughout the Greek verb.
- The form declined in Koine as ἵνα- and ὥστε-clauses took over the expression of purpose, and it does not survive in Modern Greek.
